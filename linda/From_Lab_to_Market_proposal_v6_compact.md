# What's Left of the Dye-Free Premium? Price Signals in the Middle of America's Food-Color Transition

## Overview

In April 2025, HHS and the FDA announced the largest food-additive phase-out in American history: voluntary elimination of all six remaining petroleum-based synthetic dyes (Red 40, Yellow 5, Yellow 6, Blue 1, Blue 2, Green 3), with a voluntary compliance target of October 2027. Red No. 3 was formally revoked in January 2025 (enforcement: January 2027). As of June 2026, most major manufacturers have pledged reformulation, though the majority are still "In Progress" (FDA Industry Tracker, May 2026).

Before this shift, products marketed as "dye-free" or "no artificial colors" commanded price premiums. Meta-analyses estimated average WTP premiums of ~30% for health and sustainability claims broadly (Li & Kallas 2021; Alsubhi et al. 2023) — but no published study has isolated the premium for "dye-free" specifically. As dye-free transitions from differentiator to expected baseline, the central question:

**What remains of the dye-free price premium during this regulatory transition — and does claiming "no artificial colors" generate value independent of composition?**

Early evidence suggests: very little. PepsiCo's Doritos Simply NKD retails at the same price as regular Doritos at Walmart (Food Institute, February 2026). Walmart's private-brand reformulation promises parity pricing (Walmart Corporate, October 2025). This makes mid-2026 a uniquely revealing measurement window.

**OPALS Bridge.** OPALS students in the food dye toxicity track study cellular effects of synthetic dyes — the same compounds the FDA is phasing out. This project traces the downstream impact: **Lab Finding → Regulatory Action → Industry Reformulation → Consumer Market Response** (Wang et al. 2025, *Biomedical Engineering Education*).

---

## Novel Angle

Most WTP studies use stated-preference methods during stable regulatory periods. This study measures **observed retail prices** during an active voluntary phase-out — a regulatory situation without precedent in US food policy. The novelty is **situational**, not methodological.

No published study has isolated WTP for "dye-free" as a standalone attribute. This study provides the first observed-price estimate for this specific attribute during the transition. We also test whether the **act of claiming** "no artificial colors" — independent of compositional difference — is associated with higher prices.

---

## Theoretical Framework

**1. Signaling Theory (Spence 1973).** "No artificial dyes" is a voluntary quality signal. As adoption spreads, the signal's informational value decreases. *Prediction:* premiums lower where most competitors have adopted.

**2. Anticipated-Mandate Expectations.** The phase-out is legally voluntary as of June 2026 — no formal rulemaking has revoked Red 40's authorization (PolicyCanary 2026; Haynes Boone 2025). But market participants price in the anticipated mandate. Firms reformulate pre-emptively; consumers discount the signal.

**3. Competitive Price Convergence (Tirole 1988).** Simultaneous industry reformulation prevents any firm from sustaining a premium — consumers substitute to identically-positioned competitors. PepsiCo's parity pricing for Doritos NKD illustrates this.

**4. Cost Pass-Through.** Observed price differentials reflect BOTH supply-side cost increases (natural colorants cost 2–10× more; per-SKU reformulation costs $50K–$200K per Haskell 2026, PolicyCanary 2026) and demand-side WTP. We cannot fully disentangle these with observational data, but design specific comparisons to bound each component (RQ3).

The three demand-side mechanisms are **complementary, not competing** — all predict premium compression. The null-result framework (below) partially distinguishes them via data patterns.

## Research Questions

| # | Question | Prediction | Challenged If |
|---|----------|------------|---------------|
| **RQ1** | Do dye-free products command a measurable price premium over comparable dyed products in mid-2026? | Premium ≥ 5% in paired comparisons | Premium < 5% or non-significant. A null result is substantively meaningful — see Interpreting Null Results. |
| **RQ2** | Is the premium smaller where reformulation is more advanced? | Premium in high-reformulation categories ≤ half the premium in low-reformulation categories | Premiums uniform across categories — would suggest cost/brand effects, not signal value. *Exploratory:* ~25–30 pairs per category limits statistical power to detect moderate differences. |
| **RQ3** | Among compositionally similar dye-free products, are those with "no artificial colors" claims priced higher than those without? | Claimed > unclaimed | No difference — consumers respond to ingredients, not marketing. *Note:* This is an association, not a causal test. Brands that claim are systematically different (see Limitations). |

**Meta-analytic context (Discussion only, not a formal test):** We compare findings against published WTP estimates for broader health claims: 29.5% for sustainable food (Li & Kallas 2021, *Appetite*) and 30.7% for healthier food (Alsubhi et al. 2023, *Obesity Reviews*). These aggregate across organic, fair trade, reduced-fat — not "dye-free" specifically. Dye-free is a narrower attribute that would command a smaller premium even absent any transition.

## Methodology

### Pre-Camp Preparation (Mentor, 25–30 hours)

| # | Task | Details |
|---|------|---------|
| 1 | **FDC data pull** | Query USDA FDC Branded Foods API for products containing synthetic dye names in ingredient list. Filter to target categories. Record coverage. |
| 2 | **OFF data pull** | Download OFF CSV, filter to US. Quantify products with additive tags for E129/E102/E110/E133/E132/E143. |
| 3 | **Data quality spot-check** | Select 30 products from FDC (primary) and 30 from OFF (supplementary). Verify ingredient lists against Walmart.com. Record discrepancy rate per source. For OFF: record `last_modified_t` distribution. |
| 4 | **Matched-pair identification** | Pre-identify ≥85 candidate pairs from Walmart.com across 3 categories. Document: matching rationale, match type (within-brand / cross-brand), subcategory, comparable size (±25%). Target ≥15 within-brand pairs. |
|   | | **(a) Candy/confections** (~30 pairs) — low reformulation |
|   | | **(b) Cereals/baked goods** (~30 pairs) — medium-high reformulation |
|   | | **(c) Beverages** (~25 pairs) — medium reformulation |
| 5 | **RQ3 products** | Identify ≥30 dye-free products WITH "no artificial colors" front-of-pack claim and ≥30 WITHOUT. Same category, comparable brand tier. Record: brand tier, bundled claims (Organic, Non-GMO, "clean ingredients"). |
| 6 | **Geographic pilot** | Check 5 products at zip 92093 (San Diego) and 77001 (Houston) on Walmart.com. If prices identical for all 5 → reduce geographic check to 10 pairs. If prices differ → proceed with 20-pair check. |
| 7 | **Template spreadsheet** | Build Google Sheets template with all required columns, data validation rules, and 10 pre-filled example rows. Build JASP analysis template with one example pair. |
| 8 | **Feasibility gate** | If <50 valid pairs across all categories → pivot to reformulation-landscape-only study. |
| 9 | **Progress check (2 weeks pre-camp)** | If <40 pairs → trigger feasibility gate early. If <25 pairs → commit to landscape-only study. |

### Price Collection Protocol

1. All prices from **Walmart.com**, primary zip code **92093** (UCSD campus)
2. **Geographic sensitivity check:** For a subsample (10–20 pairs per pre-camp pilot), collect prices using zip **77001** (Houston, TX)
3. Prices collected within a **3-day window** (Week 1, Days 3–5)
4. Record the **regular shelf price** per these rules:
   - No promotional badge/strikethrough → displayed price is regular price
   - "Rollback" badge WITH strikethrough → record BOTH prices; use strikethrough (original) for primary analysis
   - "Rollback" badge WITHOUT strikethrough → displayed price is regular price (permanent rollback)
   - "Was $X.XX" shown → record both; use "Was" price as regular
   - Do NOT use Walmart+ member pricing
5. Record **date of collection** for each product
6. **Screenshot every 10th product** as audit trail
7. **Inter-collector reliability:** Two students independently look up 10% overlap subsample. If discrepancy >5% → review and reconcile

### Week 1: Data Assembly & Familiarization (5 days)

**Days 1–2: Learning**
- "Ingredient detective" exercise: classify 10 familiar products (Froot Loops, Goldfish, Gatorade, etc.) into Groups A/B/C using Walmart.com ingredient lists
- FD&C naming conventions, E-numbers, natural colorant names
- Regulatory timeline and February 2026 labeling change
- "Price pair" demo: look up 3 pre-selected pairs, record all fields, discuss discrepancies

**Days 3–5: Data collection**

Record per product: name, brand, category, dye status (A/B/C), **dye count** (distinct synthetic dyes), front-of-pack claims, **brand tier** (premium/natural, mainstream, value/store), price, **package size (total oz)**, **unit price** (price/oz for solids, price/fl oz for liquids), **match type** (within-brand / cross-brand)

**End of Week 1 go/no-go:**
- ≥60 valid pairs with complete data across ≥2 categories → proceed to RQ1/RQ2
- ≥15 within-brand pairs → report within-brand premium as primary estimate
- <15 within-brand pairs → proceed with caveat; cross-brand comparisons carry brand-equity confounds
- <60 valid pairs → drop pricing; focus on reformulation landscape (Phase 3)

### Week 2: Statistical Analysis (5 days)

**RQ1 — Primary:**
- Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test (JASP: Frequencies → Paired Samples)
- Report: median premium %, IQR, p-value
- **Stratify by match type.** Within-brand pairs are the clean comparison. If within-brand ≈ 0% but cross-brand > 0%, interpret the latter as brand positioning, not dye-free attribute value.
- **Package-size check:** If paired products differ systematically in size, report sensitivity analysis restricted to pairs within ±25% size.
- Subset by category and dye status group (A vs. B, A vs. C)

**RQ2 — Exploratory:**
- Compare premium distributions across three categories
- Kruskal-Wallis test; pairwise Mann-Whitney U with Bonferroni correction (3 comparisons)
- **Power note:** ~25–30 pairs per category. Report effect sizes (rank-biserial r) alongside p-values. Non-significant results may reflect limited power, not absence of effect.

**RQ3 — Exploratory:**
- Compare unit prices: claimed-dye-free vs. unclaimed-dye-free, within same category
- Mann-Whitney U test
- **Sensitivity check:** Restrict to same-brand-tier comparisons
- **Power note:** 30 per group; power ~0.50–0.60 for small effects at α = 0.05. Report as exploratory.

### Week 3: Reformulation Landscape + Review Coding (5 days)

**Phase 3 — Reformulation Landscape (all students, primary deliverable):**

Using FDC + OFF + FDA Tracker + CSPI Tracker:

- **Dye prevalence:** What % of products per category still contain synthetic dyes? Which dyes are most prevalent?
- **Dye complexity:** Distribution of distinct synthetic dyes per product, by category. Products with 3+ dyes face higher reformulation costs.
- **Pledge-vs-product-data gap analysis:** Cross-reference company pledges (FDA/CSPI) with product-level ingredient data (FDC primary, OFF supplementary). Have companies that pledged reformulation by mid-2026 actually removed dyes?
- **Staleness validation:** For any apparent gap (company pledged but product data still shows dyes), manually verify ≥10 products against Walmart.com ingredient lists. Report proportion of **confirmed gaps** (product still contains dyes on shelf) vs. **database lag** (reformulated but FDC/OFF not yet updated). FDC's monthly manufacturer updates make confirmed gaps more credible than they would be with OFF alone.
- **State regulatory exposure:** Which products still containing dyes are sold in states with active bans? California (AB 418, Red No. 3, 2023); West Virginia (all 7 dyes in school meals, 2025); >70 food dye bills introduced across states in 2025, >15 enacted (EWG, Mar 2026).
- **Visualize:** Stacked bar charts of dye status by category; brand compliance timeline; dye prevalence heat map; pledge-vs-reality comparison.

**This analysis is independently publishable regardless of pricing outcomes.** No existing tracker cross-references company pledges with product-level ingredient data.

**Phase 4 — Review Content Analysis (stretch goal, subset of students):**

- 30–40 products (15–20 dye-free, 15–20 conventional) + 5–10 neutral-category controls (plain pasta, rice)
- 10 most recent reviews per product, ≥2 sentences (source: Amazon or Walmart product pages, manual reading only)
- Code: dye/health mentions, taste/quality, switching behavior, price sensitivity
- **Inter-coder reliability:** 20% overlap sample, Cohen's κ. If κ < 0.6 → refine scheme.

### Week 4: Synthesis & Communication (5 days)

- Integrate findings: landscape × premium estimates × review themes
- IMRAD-format research poster
- Oral presentations
- **Stretch:** 2-page conference abstract

## Interpreting Null or Near-Zero Premium Results

If observed premium < 5% or non-significant:

| Interpretation | Mechanism | Supporting Data Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Transition completion** | Market has priced in the baseline shift | Premium ≈ 0% in high-reformulation categories, > 0% in low-reformulation |
| **Competitive convergence** | Simultaneous reformulation prevents any firm from sustaining a premium | Premium ≈ 0% uniformly across all categories |
| **Cost absorption** | Firms absorb higher costs to maintain share | Premium ≈ 0% for major brands, > 0% for small brands |
| **Attribute non-salience** | Dye-free was never independently valued | Premium ≈ 0% overall, but RQ3 claim association is positive |
| **Attribute inversion** | Dye-free is now mainstream; remaining dyed products are niche/legacy at lower price points | Negative overall premium, driven by cross-brand pairs; within-brand ≈ 0% |

A null result is not a failed study. Documenting the absence of a premium during this regulatory moment establishes an observed-price baseline for future work and tests theoretical predictions about premium erosion during industry transitions.

## Scope Boundaries

This proposal:
- Takes a **cross-sectional snapshot**. No longitudinal claims.
- Reports **descriptive/correlational** premiums. No causal identification. Cost pass-through and brand positioning are acknowledged confounds, not solved.
- Treats RQ3 as an **association**, not a causal test.
- Reports **Walmart-specific** findings, not market-wide estimates.
- Does not study PFAS, Reddit, or use any scraping/API-based price collection.
- Does not assume a positive premium. Null and negative findings are substantively meaningful.

---

## Limitations

1. **Cost pass-through confound.** Observed price differentials include manufacturer cost increases from natural colorant reformulation (2–10× raw material cost; Haskell 2026). Cannot fully disentangle supply and demand. RQ3 partially addresses.

2. **Selection bias in matched pairs.** Within-brand pairs exist disproportionately for brands that have NOT yet reformulated — a non-random sample.

3. **Ingredient data coverage.** FDC Branded Foods is manufacturer-submitted and more systematic than OFF, but may not cover all products in target categories. OFF is supplementary but European-origin and crowdsourced. Findings from the landscape analysis describe products in the database, not all US products.

4. **Single time point.** Cannot measure premium change over time. Meta-analytic comparisons are suggestive context, not formal benchmarks.

5. **Voluntary transition.** The phase-out is guidance-based. Red 40 remains lawfully authorized. We study an anticipated-mandate environment, not a post-mandate one.

6. **RQ3 endogeneity.** Brands claiming "no artificial colors" are systematically premium-positioned. The comparison controls for composition but not brand positioning, marketing investment, or bundled claims. Brand-tier sensitivity check mitigates but cannot resolve.

7. **RQ3 statistical power.** 30 per group; power ~0.50–0.60 for small effects. Exploratory. Non-significance may reflect power, not absence.

8. **Single retailer, single geography.** Walmart EDLP compresses price variation. California (AB 418, 2023) is the most regulated state for food dyes. Premium estimates are Walmart-in-California-specific. Geographic sensitivity check (second zip code) partially addresses. Griffith & Nesheim (2013): organic premiums 0%–30% across retailer types.

9. **Database staleness.** Even FDC has update lags for recently reformulated products. Staleness validation (manual verification of ≥10 apparent gaps) partially addresses.
