**How the proposal evolved:**

The original draft cast a wide net — seven claim types (dye-free, PFAS-free, organic, sweetener-free, non-toxic, clean ingredients, sustainable packaging) across six product categories, five methodological phases including predictive modeling, data collection from four retailers (Amazon, Walmart, Target, Whole Foods), Reddit sentiment analysis across seven subreddits, and a target of 500–2,000 products. It was ambitious and well-motivated. But through several rounds of critical review, three structural issues kept surfacing:

1. **Scope vs. time.** Five phases, six categories, and four data sources would stretch even a graduate student over a full semester. In a four-week OPALS window, the risk was producing shallow analysis across many fronts rather than deep, defensible analysis on one.

2. **The dye-free angle is uniquely timely.** No published study has measured a "dye-free" price premium specifically. The FDA's synthetic dye phase-out (announced April 2025) creates a once-in-a-generation measurement window: we can observe prices mid-transition, as "dye-free" shifts from voluntary differentiator to expected baseline. Every other health claim in the original — organic, non-GMO, clean label — has been studied extensively. Dye-free in mid-2026 is a genuine gap in the literature. This gives students something to say that nobody else has said yet — which matters for applications far more than a broader but less novel survey.

3. **Data foundation.** The original listed NielsenIQ, McKinsey, and Statista as sources — all paywalled or commercially licensed and inaccessible to students. It also relied on multi-retailer data collection (including Amazon, which raises terms-of-service concerns for systematic scraping). The revised proposal uses two publicly accessible sources: USDA FoodData Central (the US government's official database — 400,000+ branded products, updated monthly by manufacturers) for ingredient composition, and Walmart.com manual price lookup for pricing. This makes the core deliverable genuinely credible with zero access barriers.

**Key design decisions in the final proposal:**

- **One focused question** (dye-free premium during the FDA transition) with a clear regulatory hook — instead of six questions across six categories
- **Tiered deliverables** so something ships no matter what. The reformulation landscape analysis (which companies pledged to remove dyes vs. which products actually still contain them) requires zero pricing data and is novel — no existing tracker cross-references company pledges with product-level ingredient data
- **Honest statistical design** appropriate for high school students: non-parametric paired tests in JASP (free, GUI-based), not the predictive models in the original Phase 5 that would require programming skills and much larger datasets
- **A concrete execution plan** — down to which zip codes, how to handle Walmart rollback pricing, what to do if matched pairs fall short. The original's methodology was organized by phase but left execution details open; the revised version specifies exact thresholds and decision rules
- **A sophisticated null-result framework.** The study is designed to be informative whether premiums are positive, zero, or even negative. "We found no premium during this regulatory moment" is itself a publishable finding — it documents the market absorbing a major transition in real time
