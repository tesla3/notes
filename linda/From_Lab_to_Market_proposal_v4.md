# What's Left of the Dye-Free Premium? Price Signals in the Middle of America's Food-Color Transition

## Project Overview

In April 2025, the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services and the FDA announced the largest food-additive phase-out in American history: voluntary elimination of all six remaining petroleum-based synthetic dyes (Red 40, Yellow 5, Yellow 6, Blue 1, Blue 2, Green 3) from the nation's food supply, with a voluntary compliance target of October 2027. Red No. 3 was formally revoked in January 2025 (enforcement: January 2027). As of June 2026, the FDA's industry tracker shows most major manufacturers — General Mills, Nestlé, PepsiCo, Kraft Heinz, Mars, Hershey, Walmart — have pledged reformulation, though the majority are still "In Progress."

Before this regulatory shift, products marketed as "dye-free" or "no artificial colors" commanded price premiums over conventional alternatives. Meta-analyses estimated average willingness-to-pay premiums of ~30% for health and sustainability claims broadly — organic, fair trade, reduced-fat, and similar attributes (Li & Kallas 2021; Alsubhi et al. 2023). No published study has isolated the premium specifically for "dye-free" or "no artificial colors" as a standalone attribute. But as dye-free status transitions from voluntary differentiator to expected industry baseline, a critical question emerges:

**What remains of the dye-free price premium in the middle of this regulatory transition — and does the mere act of *claiming* "no artificial colors" generate value independent of the product's actual composition?**

Early market evidence suggests the answer may be: very little. PepsiCo's Doritos Simply NKD — a dye-free reformulation launched December 2025 — retails at the **same price** as regular Doritos at Walmart (Food Institute, February 2026; Daily Mail, February 2026). Walmart's own private-brand reformulation explicitly promises "affordable food" at parity pricing (Walmart, October 2025). This raises the possibility that the premium has already collapsed to zero in some categories, making the mid-2026 measurement window a uniquely revealing snapshot — whether premiums persist, are eroding, or have already vanished.

This project creates a concrete bridge between OPALS laboratory research and real-world market outcomes. OPALS students in the food dye toxicity track study the cellular and molecular effects of synthetic dyes — the same compounds the FDA is now phasing out. This project traces the downstream impact:

**Lab Finding → Regulatory Action → Industry Reformulation → Consumer Market Response**

## Novel Angle

Most willingness-to-pay studies for health claims use stated-preference methods (surveys, choice experiments) and are cross-sectional snapshots during stable regulatory periods. This study measures **observed retail prices** during an active voluntary phase-out — a regulatory situation without precedent in US food policy. The novelty is **situational**, not methodological: we observe the premium at a moment when the "dye-free" claim is transitioning from voluntary differentiator to expected baseline.

No published study has isolated willingness-to-pay specifically for "dye-free" or "no artificial colors" as a standalone product attribute. Existing meta-analyses aggregate across broader claim bundles — organic, sustainability, nutritional improvement — making it impossible to establish a pre-transition baseline for the dye-free premium specifically. This study provides the first observed-price estimate for this specific attribute during the transition.

We also test whether the **act of claiming "no artificial colors"** — independent of compositional difference — is associated with higher prices. This partially separates the marketing signal from the product attribute itself, though brand-level confounders remain (see Limitations).

## Theoretical Framework

### 1. Signaling Theory (Spence 1973)

"No artificial dyes" is a voluntary signal of product quality. As more firms adopt the signal — driven by FDA pressure, state legislation, and consumer demand — the signal becomes less differentiating. Its informational value decreases.

**Prediction:** Premiums should be lower in categories where most competitors have already adopted the signal.

### 2. Anticipated-Mandate Expectations

The FDA phase-out is legally voluntary as of June 2026 — no formal rulemaking has revoked Red 40's authorization (PolicyCanary, March 2026; Haynes Boone, April 2025). But market participants price in the anticipated mandate. Firms reformulate pre-emptively; consumers discount the signal because they expect it to become universal. This is expectations-based erosion, not mandate-based.

### 3. Competitive Price Convergence

In addition to signal erosion on the demand side, simultaneous industry reformulation creates competitive convergence on the supply side. When multiple firms adopt the same attribute concurrently, no individual firm can sustain a premium because consumers can substitute to identically-positioned competitors (Tirole 1988). This predicts premium compression independent of changes in consumer valuation.

PepsiCo's parity pricing for Doritos NKD illustrates this mechanism: the dye-free variant costs the same as the original, not because "no artificial colors" is a weak signal, but because competitors are reformulating concurrently and charging more would lose market share.

### 4. Cost Pass-Through vs. WTP Decomposition

Observed price differentials reflect BOTH supply-side cost increases and demand-side willingness to pay. Natural colorants cost 2–10x more than synthetic dyes on a raw-material basis (Haskell 2026; Elchemy 2025; NewFoodMagazine 2025), and per-SKU reformulation costs range from $50,000–$200,000 (PolicyCanary 2026). We cannot fully disentangle supply and demand with observational data, but we design specific comparisons to bound each component (see RQ3).

## Research Questions

| # | Question | Prediction | Challenged If |
|---|----------|------------|---------------|
| **RQ1** | Do dye-free food products command a measurable price premium over comparable dyed products in mid-2026? | Premium ≥ 5% in paired comparisons. | Premium < 5% or not significant (p > 0.05). A null result is not uninformative — it would indicate the market has already absorbed the transition. See *Interpreting Null Results* below for four distinguishable interpretations. |
| **RQ2** | Is the premium smaller in categories where reformulation is more advanced vs. categories where synthetic dyes remain common? | Premium in high-reformulation categories ≤ half the premium in low-reformulation categories. | Premiums uniform across categories — would suggest the premium reflects cost structure or brand positioning, not signal value. |
| **RQ3** | Among compositionally similar dye-free products, are those with front-of-pack "no artificial colors" claims associated with higher prices than those without the claim? | Claimed dye-free products priced higher than unclaimed dye-free products. | No difference — would suggest consumers respond to ingredients, not marketing signals. |

RQ3 is the most theoretically interesting comparison in this proposal. By holding composition constant (both groups are dye-free), it isolates the association between the label claim and price. However, this is not a clean causal test: brands that make the claim are systematically different from those that don't (see Limitation #6). The comparison controls for composition but not for brand positioning, marketing investment, or bundled claims.

### Meta-Analytic Context (Discussion, Not a Formal Test)

We contextualize our findings against published WTP estimates for broader health and sustainability claims: 29.5% average for sustainable food products (Li & Kallas 2021, *Appetite*) and 30.7% mean for healthier food products (Alsubhi et al. 2023, *Obesity Reviews*). These baselines aggregate across organic, fair trade, reduced-fat, and other multi-attribute bundles — not "dye-free" specifically. Dye-free is a narrower attribute that would be expected to command a smaller premium even absent any regulatory transition. We report this comparison as indicative context in the Discussion section, not as a formal benchmark test.

### February 2026 Labeling Change

The FDA's February 5, 2026 enforcement discretion policy is critical for this study's design:

> *"Under the updated policy, companies may now state that products contain 'no artificial colors' as long as the product does not contain petroleum-based synthetic dyes, even if it uses FDA-approved, non-petroleum-based color additives derived from natural sources."* — Barley.com, February 2026

This means products labeled "no artificial colors" in 2026 may contain beet juice, spirulina, or other natural colorants. Our coding scheme accounts for this (see Product Classification below).

## Product Classification

All products are classified into three groups based on ingredient composition and the February 2026 FDA labeling policy:

| Group | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **A — Synthetic Dyes** | Contains one or more FD&C-certified petroleum-based color additives | Froot Loops (contains Red 40, Yellow 6, Blue 1) |
| **B — Natural Colorants** | Dye-free (no synthetic dyes) but contains natural colorants (beet juice, turmeric, spirulina, etc.). Can legally claim "no artificial colors" under Feb 2026 policy. | Annie's Bunny Grahams (colored with beet juice concentrate) |
| **C — No Colorants** | Contains no added colorants of any kind | Plain Cheerios |

This three-group scheme is essential because Groups B and C have different cost structures (Group B uses expensive natural colorants; Group C does not) and different signaling profiles.

## Methodology

### Pre-Camp Preparation (Mentor, 14–18 hours)

1. **Open Food Facts validation.** Download OFF CSV. Filter to `countries_en` containing "United States." Quantify: how many products have complete ingredient lists with additive tags? Flag products containing E129 (Red 40), E102 (Yellow 5), E110 (Yellow 6), E133 (Blue 1), E132 (Blue 2), E143 (Green 3).

2. **Data quality spot-check.** Select 30 US products from OFF that are tagged with synthetic dye additives. Manually verify against the product's actual ingredient list (from Walmart.com or manufacturer website). Record discrepancy rate. If >10% of OFF tags are incorrect → plan to supplement with manual ingredient-list review during Week 1.

3. **Coverage check.** Cross-reference with PlainFoodSafe if OFF US coverage < 200 relevant products.

4. **Matched-pair identification.** Pre-identify ≥ 85 candidate matched pairs from Walmart.com across **three categories**:
   - **(a) Candy/confections** (~30 pairs) — low reformulation. National Confectioners Association members have not pledged voluntary removal. Mars, McKee Foods targeting end of 2027 at earliest. (CSPI, April 2026)
   - **(b) Cereals/baked goods** (~30 pairs) — medium-high reformulation. WK Kellogg (end 2027), Post (end 2027) still in progress; General Mills targeting mid-2027. Some brands may have already completed.
   - **(c) Beverages** (~25 pairs) — medium reformulation. Kraft Heinz/Kool-Aid (end 2026), Coca-Cola (end 2027). Sports drinks, flavored waters moving faster.
   - Document each pair's matching rationale (same brand where possible; same subcategory, comparable size).

5. **RQ3 pairs.** Identify ≥ 30 products that are dye-free (Group B or C) AND make a "no artificial colors" claim on the label, and ≥ 30 dye-free products that don't make the claim. Same category, comparable brand tier (premium/natural, mainstream, value/store brand). Record for each product: brand tier, presence of other health claims (USDA Organic, Non-GMO Project Verified, "clean ingredients"), and any visible front-of-pack design cues.

6. **Feasibility gate.** If < 50 valid pairs exist across all three categories → plan pivots to reformulation-landscape-only study (Phase 3 becomes the entire project; still publishable with pledge-vs-reality analysis).

### Price Collection Protocol

All price data follows a standardized protocol to ensure consistency and reproducibility:

1. All prices collected from **Walmart.com** using zip code **92093** (UCSD campus) for geographic consistency
2. All prices collected within a **3-day window** during Week 1 (Days 3–5)
3. Record the **regular shelf price**, not sale/rollback price. If a product is on sale, record both prices; use regular price for analysis
4. Record **date of collection** for each product
5. **Screenshot every 10th product** as a spot-check audit trail
6. **Inter-collector reliability:** Two students independently look up prices for a 10% overlap subsample. Report concordance rate. If discrepancy rate >5% → review and reconcile

---

### Review Selection Protocol

For the Phase 4 review content analysis:

1. **10 most recent reviews** per product as of the data collection date
2. Exclude reviews shorter than **2 sentences** (filters uninformative one-word ratings)
3. Record the **date range** of sampled reviews for each product
4. Source: Amazon or Walmart product pages (publicly visible, manual reading only — no scraping)

### Week 1: Data Assembly & Familiarization (5 days)

**Day 1–2:**
- Students explore OFF data; learn to read ingredient lists
- Understand FD&C color additive names vs. E-numbers vs. natural colorant names
- Classify products into Groups A / B / C (see Product Classification above)
- Introduction to the regulatory timeline and the FDA's February 2026 labeling change

**Day 3–5:**
- Students manually look up current Walmart.com prices for pre-selected matched pairs following the Price Collection Protocol
- Record per product: name, brand, category, dye status (A/B/C), **dye count** (number of distinct synthetic dyes), specific front-of-pack claims ("no artificial colors," "USDA Organic," "Non-GMO Project Verified," "clean ingredients," or none), **brand tier** (premium/natural, mainstream, value/store brand), price, package size, unit price (per oz or per serving)

**End of Week 1 go/no-go:**
- ≥ 60 valid pairs with complete data across ≥ 2 categories? → Proceed to RQ1/RQ2 analysis
- < 60 valid pairs? → Drop pricing analysis; focus on reformulation landscape (Phase 3) + RQ3 if enough claim-vs-no-claim pairs exist

---

### Week 2: Statistical Analysis (5 days)

**RQ1 — Primary analysis:**
- Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric, robust to small samples and non-normal price distributions)
- Report: median premium %, interquartile range, p-value
- Subset by category and by dye status group (A vs. B, A vs. C)

**RQ2 — Category comparison:**
- Compare premium distributions across three categories: candy/confections, cereals/baked goods, beverages
- Kruskal-Wallis test for overall difference across all three categories
- Pairwise Mann-Whitney U tests between each category pair (with Bonferroni correction for 3 comparisons)

**RQ3 — Claim association:**
- Compare unit prices of claimed-dye-free vs. unclaimed-dye-free products within same category
- Mann-Whitney U test
- **Sensitivity check:** Restrict to same-brand-tier comparisons (e.g., premium vs. premium only) and report whether the association persists

**All students:** Calculate descriptive statistics, create box plots, learn to interpret p-values and confidence intervals.

---

### Week 3: Reformulation Landscape + Review Coding (5 days)

**Phase 3 — Reformulation Landscape Analysis (all students):**

Using OFF data + PlainFoodSafe + FDA Industry Tracker + CSPI Tracker:

- What % of products in each category still contain synthetic dyes?
- Which dyes are most prevalent? (Red 40 dominates per PlainFoodSafe: 21,595 products)
- **Dye complexity:** How many distinct synthetic dyes does each product contain? Map the distribution by category. Products with 3+ dyes face higher reformulation costs and complexity.
- **Pledge-vs-reality gap analysis:** Cross-reference company-level pledges (FDA/CSPI) with product-level data (OFF). Have companies that pledged reformulation by mid-2026 actually removed dyes from their products in the OFF database? If OFF shows General Mills cereals still containing Red 40 despite a mid-2027 target, what proportion of their product line has been reformulated so far?
- **State regulatory exposure:** Which products still containing dyes are sold in states with active bans or restrictions? California banned Red No. 3 (AB 418, effective 2023). West Virginia banned all seven dyes in school meals (Phase 1, August 2025). Per EWG (March 2026), more than 70 food dye-related bills were introduced across US states in 2025, with more than 15 enacted.
- **Visualize:** stacked bar charts of dye status by category; brand compliance timeline chart; dye prevalence heat map; pledge-vs-reality comparison chart

**This is the descriptive backbone of the study. The pledge-vs-reality gap analysis is independently publishable regardless of pricing analysis outcomes, because no existing tracker cross-references company pledges with product-level ingredient data.**

**Phase 4 — Review Content Analysis (subset of students, 3 days):**

- Select 30–40 products (15–20 dye-free, 15–20 conventional) across all three categories
- Follow the Review Selection Protocol: 10 most recent reviews per product, ≥2 sentences (300–400 total reviews)
- Code each review for:
  - Explicit dye/health-concern mentions (dyes, children, allergies, cancer, hyperactivity)
  - Taste/quality comments
  - Switching-behavior mentions ("I bought this because…," "switched from…")
  - Price-sensitivity mentions
- **Inter-coder reliability:** Two students independently code a 20% overlap sample. Report Cohen's κ. If κ < 0.6 → refine coding scheme and re-code.

## What This Proposal Does NOT Do

- **Does not claim to measure temporal change.** We take a cross-sectional snapshot. Longitudinal claims are explicitly disavowed.
- **Does not claim causal identification.** Premiums are descriptive/correlational. Cost pass-through and brand positioning are acknowledged as confounds, not solved.
- **Does not treat RQ3 as a clean causal test.** The claim-vs-no-claim comparison controls for composition but not for brand positioning, marketing investment, or bundled claims. We report associations with caveats, not causal effects.
- **Does not study PFAS.** PFAS claims are packaging-level, not consumer-facing product labels. Too few "PFAS-free" labeled retail products exist for statistical analysis.
- **Does not use Reddit data.** The disconnect between Reddit discourse and product-level pricing is unresolvable within this design.
- **Does not scrape any website.** Manual lookup of publicly displayed prices only.
- **Does not assume OFF data is comprehensive for US products.** Pre-camp validation required; quality spot-check required; fallback plan specified.
- **Does not assume a positive premium.** The study is designed to be informative whether premiums are positive, zero, or negative. A null finding is substantively meaningful (see Interpreting Null Results).

## Limitations

1. **Cost pass-through confound.** Observed price differentials include both consumer WTP and manufacturer cost increases from natural colorant reformulation (natural colorants cost 2–10x more than synthetic dyes per Haskell 2026, NewFoodMagazine 2025). We cannot fully disentangle these. RQ3 (claim effect on compositionally similar products) partially addresses this.

2. **Selection bias in matched pairs.** Within-brand pairs that still exist in summer 2026 are disproportionately from brands that have NOT yet reformulated — a non-random sample. Categories with completed reformulation cannot provide within-brand pairs.

3. **Open Food Facts coverage and quality.** Crowdsourced, European-origin database. US coverage may be incomplete. US food labels do not use E-numbers; OFF's mapping of "Red 40" / "FD&C Red No. 40" to standardized additive tags is of unknown accuracy. Findings from the reformulation landscape analysis should be interpreted as "of the products in the database," not "of all US products." Pre-camp spot-check partially addresses data quality; coverage limitations remain.

4. **Single time point.** We cannot measure premium change over time. Comparisons to meta-analytic baselines are suggestive, not definitive — those baselines used different methods (stated preference vs. our observed prices), measured different attributes (organic/sustainability broadly vs. dye-free specifically), and covered different time periods.

5. **Voluntary transition.** The FDA dye phase-out is guidance-based, not a legal mandate as of June 2026. Red 40 remains a lawfully authorized color additive (PolicyCanary 2026; Haynes Boone 2025). Signaling theory predictions about mandate effects are theoretical projections, not observed phenomena. We study an anticipated-mandate environment, not a post-mandate one.

6. **Endogeneity in RQ3.** Brands that make "no artificial colors" claims are not randomly assigned. They tend to be premium-positioned brands with higher prices across their product lines. The RQ3 comparison controls for composition (both groups are dye-free) but not for brand positioning, other health claims, or marketing investment. Any observed claim-price association may reflect brand equity rather than the signaling value of the specific claim. We mitigate this by recording brand tier and bundled claims and reporting sensitivity checks, but cannot fully resolve the confound without within-brand comparisons (which are likely too rare to achieve adequate sample size).

7. **Statistical power for RQ3.** With 30 products per group and a Mann-Whitney U test, statistical power to detect a small effect (e.g., 3% price difference) is limited — approximately 0.50–0.60 at α = 0.05, depending on variance. RQ3 is exploratory. Failure to detect a significant claim-price association may reflect insufficient power rather than absence of effect.

## Expected Results

### H1
Products marketed as dye-free command a statistically significant price premium (≥ 5%) over comparable dyed products, but the premium is likely modest given the pace of the industry transition and emerging evidence of parity pricing in reformulated product lines.

### H2
The premium is smaller in high-reformulation categories (cereals) than in low-reformulation categories (candy), with beverages intermediate, consistent with signal-erosion and competitive-convergence theories.

### H3
Among compositionally similar dye-free products, those with front-of-pack "no artificial colors" claims are associated with higher prices than those without the claim. This association may reflect the signaling value of the claim, brand-level positioning, or both.

### H4
Consumer reviews for dye-free products more frequently reference health concerns (children's health, hyperactivity, cancer risk) and switching behavior than reviews for conventional products.

## Interpreting Null or Near-Zero Premium Results

If the observed premium is < 5% or not statistically significant, four interpretations are possible, each with distinct data signatures:

| Interpretation | Mechanism | Data Pattern That Would Support It |
|---------------|-----------|-----------------------------------|
| **1. Transition completion** | The market has already priced in the expected baseline shift; dye-free no longer differentiates. | Premium ≈ 0% in high-reformulation categories but > 0% in low-reformulation categories (where the transition is less advanced). |
| **2. Competitive convergence** | Simultaneous industry reformulation prevents any single firm from sustaining a premium — consumers substitute to equally dye-free competitors. | Premium ≈ 0% uniformly across all categories, including those where dyes are still common (firms preemptively match competitors). |
| **3. Cost absorption** | Firms absorb higher natural-colorant costs rather than pass them through, to maintain market share during the transition. | Premium ≈ 0% for major brands (who can absorb costs) but > 0% for small/independent brands (who cannot). |
| **4. Attribute non-salience** | Dye-free status was never independently valued by consumers; historical "premiums" reflected organic/natural brand positioning, not dye absence per se. | Premium ≈ 0% overall, but RQ3 claim-price association is positive — indicating consumers respond to marketing positioning, not to dye-free composition itself. |

A null result is not a failed study. Documenting the absence of a premium during this specific regulatory moment is itself a contribution: it establishes an observed-price baseline for future longitudinal work and tests whether the theoretical prediction of premium erosion during industry transitions is empirically supported.
