# UCSD MAP (Mentor Assistance Program) — Deep Dive

**Research Date:** March 8, 2026
**Source:** SDSC StudentTech website, UCSD Today press releases, MAP application page, MAP mentors page, Triple V listing, July 2024 showcase press release. All verified against primary sources.
**Related:** [Claire STEM decision brief](claire-stem-internships-2026-decision.md)

---

## What It Is

An 8-month mentorship program pairing San Diego high school students with UCSD faculty, postdocs, and staff researchers. Students work 2–4 hours/week on a real research project under their mentor's guidance. Culminates in a poster + lightning talk at the annual MAP Research Symposium (held at SDSC Auditorium, typically late April/early May).

**Not** a summer program. Not a class. Not paid. It's a structured volunteer research apprenticeship during the school year.

**Run by:** San Diego Supercomputer Center (SDSC), UC San Diego. Co-founded in 2015 by Ange Mason (SDSC Education Manager) and Dr. Kellie Church (School of Medicine). Now in its 10th year.

**Scale:** ~50–60 students per cohort, ~13 mentors across ~8 STEM fields (2024–25 numbers). Has grown steadily since launching with a smaller cohort in 2015.

---

## How It Works

### The Structure

1. **Application period:** Opens April 15, 2026. Deadline: May 31, 2026.
2. **Mentor selection:** Applications go to mentors by third week of June. Mentors choose their own students using their own criteria. MAP coordinators have no influence on selections.
3. **Notification:** August 1–15, 2026 (for fall start). Some mentors select for summer start (early June decisions).
4. **Program runs:** October 1, 2026 – May 31, 2027.
5. **Time commitment:** 2–4 hours/week. Flexible scheduling around mentor's availability.
6. **On-campus activities:** Every 8–10 weeks, Friday evenings or Saturday mornings.
7. **Finale:** Poster session + 1-minute lightning talk + 3-slide presentation at annual MAP Symposium (SDSC Auditorium). Hundreds of attendees — parents, students, UCSD community.

### The Mentorship Model

This is the program's key design insight. From Ange Mason (co-founder):

> "We have designed this program so that UC San Diego faculty and postdoc mentors can decide their own level of involvement with the students, from mentoring over email and phone regarding students' science fair or independent study projects, to mentoring involving laboratory and worksite visits."

In practice, involvement varies by mentor. Some offer:
- **Email-only:** Providing scholarly articles, guiding independent study or science fair projects remotely.
- **Lab/worksite visits:** Student comes to campus to observe or participate in data gathering.
- **Active research collaboration:** Student contributes to ongoing research projects.

The key line from the founding press release: **"After six months, the mentor may offer the student a summer internship if he or she desires."** This is the pipeline to summer research that makes MAP strategically valuable.

### Students Are Hired as Volunteers

All MAP participants are formally registered as **volunteers through SDSC at UC San Diego**. This gives them institutional affiliation — important for listing on applications and resumes.

## Application Requirements

From the application page (verified):

1. **Cover letter** addressed to your first-choice mentor (by name — use Dr. when appropriate)
2. **Resume** — well-crafted
3. **Essay** — why you wish to participate (original, not reused from school)
4. **Recommendation letters** — must be submitted by deadline; cannot be submitted separately/late
5. **Transcript** (implied from recommendation form which asks evaluator to assess grades)

The recommendation form asks evaluators to rate the student on: energy/initiative, ability to work independently, ability to work in a group, fulfilling goals, working to capacity, oral expression, written expression, originality, social maturity, self-confidence, dependability, disciplined work habits. Plus an attached letter addressing benefits, personal qualities, awards, and explanation of any grade discrepancies.

**Student-driven ethos:** The program explicitly states parents should NOT call on the student's behalf. The student contacts coordinators directly. This is a test of initiative in itself.

## Critical Assessment

### What's Genuinely Good

1. **Low barrier, high ceiling.** The eligibility requirements are minimal (2.75 GPA, age 16). But the output — 8 months of faculty mentorship and a research poster — is a serious credential. The program is designed so the effort you put in determines the return.

2. **The pipeline effect.** The founding design includes the explicit possibility of a summer internship offer after 6 months. This is how students without connections get lab access. MAP → summer research → elite program applications is a real pathway.

3. **Mentor quality is real.** These aren't grad students or TAs. The current list includes an NSF CAREER Award winner, a DOE Early Career Award winner, Stanford/IIT PhDs, faculty publishing in Nature/Science/NEJM. This is research university faculty mentoring high schoolers — rare access.

4. **Manageable time commitment.** 2–4 hours/week during the school year is sustainable alongside honors coursework. It doesn't compete with summer programs — it complements them.

5. **Student-driven model.** The program explicitly tests initiative — students contact coordinators themselves, write their own essays, address mentors correctly. This filters for the kind of student who will benefit.

### What's Less Great

1. **Variable mentor engagement.** Some mentors only offer email correspondence and articles. Others offer active lab work. The "level of involvement" listed on the mentors page shows the range. A student matched with an email-only mentor gets a fundamentally different experience. The student has some control over this by choosing which mentor to apply to, but final selection is the mentor's.

2. **Unpaid.** 2–4 hours/week for 8 months is 64–128 hours of volunteer work. For a family where the student needs to earn money, this is a real cost.

3. **No guaranteed outcome.** The mentor may not offer a summer internship. The research project may not lead to anything publishable. The value depends heavily on the specific mentor-student match.

4. **Modest scale.** ~13 mentors for ~60 students means ~4-5 students per mentor. Competition for the best mentors (Silberman, Berti, Duarte) is likely higher.

5. **Application timing quirk.** Applications open April 15, deadline May 31, but notifications don't come until August 1–15. That's a 2.5-month wait with no status information (parents are told explicitly not to call for status updates). If Claire is planning her junior year around this, she needs to treat it as uncertain until August.

## Strategic Value in Claire's Overall Plan

MAP is not a standalone play. It's the **second leg** of a two-part 2026–27 strategy:

**Summer 2026:** Get any research/engineering experience (Cooper Union, SRTI, or volunteer lab work).

**October 2026 – May 2027 (MAP):** Build sustained research experience with UCSD faculty. This transforms Claire's profile from "strong student, no research" to "student with demonstrated research experience and a faculty recommendation from a UC San Diego researcher."

**October – December 2026:** Apply to elite summer 2027 programs (SEAP, NASA, COSMOS, Salk) with a fundamentally different application:
- ✅ Prior summer program experience
- ✅ Ongoing research mentorship with UCSD faculty
- ✅ Python + computational skills applied to real research
- ✅ Biology coursework (if she takes it junior year)
- ✅ Science fair entry (if she uses MAP project as basis)

**The insight:** MAP is the cheapest, lowest-barrier way to get verifiable UC San Diego research experience with a named faculty member. That single credential — "conducted research under Dr. X at UCSD for 8 months" — is what separates Claire's 2027 applications from her 2026 applications.
