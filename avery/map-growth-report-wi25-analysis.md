## How to Read This Report

### RIT Score
- Rasch Unit — equal-interval scale (~100 to 300+). Not a percentage, not grade-locked.
- The three numbers (e.g., 269-**273**-277) are: low confidence bound, best estimate, high confidence bound. If retested, the true score is 95% likely within that range.
- Don't over-interpret small differences — a 3-4 point gap may be measurement noise.

### Percentile
- "77th percentile" = scored higher than 77% of 7th graders nationally.
- The three percentile numbers map directly to the three RIT numbers (low RIT → low percentile, etc.).

### Bar Chart
- Blue bar = Avery's actual score
- Yellow bar = national average for grade 7
- Hatched bar = projected score next winter

### Goals Performance (Sub-Scores)
- The RIT ranges represent the difficulty band of questions answered correctly in each strand.
- Most actionable part of the report — shows specific strengths and gaps.

### Lexile Range
- Converted from Reading RIT. Maps to real-world text difficulty.
- Sweet spot for growth reading: within this band. Below = too easy. Above = frustration.

### Growth Projection
- NWEA's statistical prediction based on students with similar starting scores. Not a goal or ceiling.
- Growth columns are blank here — likely because there is no prior winter score in the system to compare against. (This is an inference; the report doesn't explain why.)

### Vocabulary-Specific Strategy

Vocabulary was the weakest sub-score (RIT 218-230 vs. Literary Text 227-239). Direct actions:

1. **Read non-fiction regularly.** Non-fiction has ~3x the density of Tier 2 academic vocabulary compared to fiction. Even 20 minutes/day of WSJ, The Atlantic, or Smithsonian Magazine moves the needle.
2. **Context-based learning over flashcards.** Encountering words in real text builds deeper retention than memorizing definitions.
3. **If she'll do it:** keep a running list of unfamiliar words encountered while reading — look up after finishing the chapter, not mid-read (preserves flow).
4. **Lean into her math strength.** Books like *Freakonomics*, *Outliers*, and *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (1400L, stretch) combine analytical thinking she's strong at with the vocabulary exposure she needs. Lower friction than pure literary fiction.
