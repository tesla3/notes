# College Majors 2026: Critical Distillation

Source: Multi-document research set + independent verification against primary sources | Date: 2026-05-10

## I. The Structural Facts (Verified Against Primary Sources)

### 1. The entry-level job market is bad — but contextualize correctly

> "The underemployment rate of 42.5% was the highest level since 2020."
> — CNBC (citing NY Fed), Mar 13, 2026

> "The rate since 2008 has ranged from 38% to 48%."
> — Politifact, Mar 23, 2026

> "The unemployment rate for recent college graduates swelled to roughly 5.7% in the fourth quarter of 2025... The overall unemployment rate during that time was closer to 4.2%."
> — CNBC, Apr 6, 2026

> "In 2025, the share of unemployed Americans who are new workforce entrants hit a 37-year high, peaking at 13.3% in July before settling at 10.6% this February."
> — Fortune, Mar 21, 2026

**Correct interpretation:** 42.5% underemployment is within historical norms (38-48% since 2008) — structurally high for two decades, not a sudden crisis. What *is* genuinely new: the 37-year-high new-entrant unemployment share, combined with 5.7% graduate unemployment, in a labor market that's otherwise at 4.2%. The *composite* picture is bad; no single figure alone is catastrophic.

**Methodological caveat:** Georgetown CEW (Mar 2026) directly challenges the NY Fed's underemployment measurement:

> "The standard approach labels a college graduate as underemployed any time they work in a job that BLS designates as requiring less than a bachelor's degree for entry-level hiring."
> — Georgetown CEW, Mar 1, 2026

> "Conventional wisdom holds that recent college graduates have a harder time finding jobs today than in the past. But a new approach to assessing job suitability suggests otherwise."
> — Urban Institute, "Mismatch," Oct 2025

Georgetown CEW and the Urban Institute argue the standard approach overcounts underemployment. Even Georgetown acknowledges: "The primary issue is that underemployment is too high, whether measurements fall at the low end or the upper bound" (Mar 17, 2026). The number may be 35% or 42% depending on methodology — either way, it's too high.

### 2. White-collar employment is in structural contraction; healthcare and physical work are growing

> "Information services lost 13,000 jobs in the month [April 2026], adding to a steady decline for the sector since November 2022 (down 342,000 jobs) as AI has disrupted employers."
> — Forbes (citing BLS), May 8, 2026

> "White-collar employment across the S&P 500 fell by 400,000 in 2025 as major companies redirected spending towards AI. The first annual decline since 2016."
> — India Today, Apr 27, 2026

> "Financial activities employment fell by 15,000 in March 2026 alone... The sector is now down 77,000 jobs since May 2025."
> — Yahoo Finance, Apr 20, 2026

> "Health care saw the largest job gains with 37,000 jobs added to the economy [in April], followed by transportation and warehousing adding 30,000 jobs."
> — Forbes, May 8, 2026

**Critical caveat on the 342,000 figure:** "Information sector" (NAICS 51) includes publishing, motion pictures, broadcasting, telecommunications, and data processing — many of which were dying before AI. An unknown fraction of the 342,000 loss is legacy media collapse, not software employment specifically. Software developers are classified under SOC 15-xxxx and work across *all* sectors (healthcare IT, defense, finance). The sector-level number overstates the damage to CS career prospects specifically.

**Nevertheless:** The directional signal is clear. The two sectors that historically absorbed the most college graduates — finance and information — are contracting in aggregate. The growth is in healthcare, construction, and services.

### 3. CS enrollment declined 8.1% at four-year institutions

> "Undergraduate enrollment at four-year institutions fell by 8.1% to about 606,000 students. Graduate enrollment dropped sharply by 14.0%, with about 190,000 students."
> — National Student Clearinghouse, "The Changing Landscape of Postsecondary Education," Feb 10, 2026

The steepest one-year decline of any major since at least 2020.

### 4. AI displacement of entry-level workers is empirically measured

> "Early-career workers (ages 22-25) in the most AI-exposed occupations have experienced a 16 percent relative decline in employment even after controlling for firm-level shocks."
> — Stanford Digital Economy Lab, "Canaries in the Coal Mine?" Apr 28, 2026

Key findings from the study:
- Displacement strongest where AI *automates* tasks
- Where AI *augments* workers, entry-level employment holds up or grows
- Adjustments through headcount (fewer hires), not pay cuts
- Effect accelerated from 13% (Aug 2025 working paper) to 16% (Apr 2026 published version)

**Contradicting study:**

> "There is no empirical evidence that AI is reducing overall labor market demand — and it may be increasing opportunities for fresh graduates."
> — University of Maryland–LinkUp AI Maps Project, Apr 2026 (via Yahoo Finance)

**Resolution:** These measure different things. Stanford measures *employment outcomes* for workers in specific AI-exposed occupations. UMD/LinkUp measures *job postings* (demand signals) across the entire economy. Firms can post more jobs (UMD is right about aggregate demand) while filling fewer of them with junior people (Stanford is right about entry-level displacement in specific roles). Both are correct; they're not contradictory — they illuminate different layers of the same phenomenon.

Still current as of May 2026. No evidence of material change. The degree remains the baseline hiring filter.

### 7. The accounting/CPA shortage is acute and the pathway is being reformed

> "The accounting profession lost over 300,000 professionals between 2020 and 2024. The pipeline replacing them has shrunk by roughly 33% over the past decade."
> — Madras Accountancy, Apr 2026

> "In Kansas, Kentucky, and Maryland, prospective CPAs can now get the credential with a four-year degree and two years of work experience, plus passing the CPA exam."
> — CFO Brew, May 7, 2026

> "The 150-hour requirement for obtaining a CPA license has led to a 26 percent decline in entry of minority CPAs."
> — MIT/Journal of Accounting Research (Sutherland, Uckert & Vetter, 2024)

10+ states have reformed or are reforming the 150-hour rule (including OH, KS, KY, MD, IN, GA, VA, NM, UT, HI, WV, NE, RI, LA). This simultaneously *lowers barriers* and *may increase supply* — the shortage could partially self-correct faster than nursing (which has physical infrastructure constraints).

### 8. The demographic cliff begins NOW

> "WICHE projects that the total number of high school graduates will peak in 2025 before entering a period of steady decline through 2041... a 13% decline from the peak."
> — WICHE, Knocking at the College Door (11th Edition), 2026

Asymmetric: Northeast faces 20%+ declines; the South gains ~3%. Creates tuition leverage for students in declining regions.

### 9. The skilled trades gap is the most extreme supply-demand mismatch in the labor market

> "Across core skilled-trade jobs, there are about 2.9 million openings each year, but only about 1.25 million newly trained workers."
> — Forbes, Mar 2026

A 2.3:1 demand-to-supply ratio. On debt-adjusted early-career ROI:

> "A craft apprentice completing a four-year program earns a median journeyman wage of $63,456 with zero educational debt, while the average four-year college graduate starts at $47,000 carrying nearly $26,000 in loans."
> — VoteChed/Substack, citing NCCER 2024 report, May 7, 2026

**Critical counterpoint the trades boosters omit:**

> "Some skilled trades offer better income out of high school, but not necessarily in the long-term."
> — WOSU, May 8, 2026

BLS data consistently shows bachelor's degree holders earning ~$1.2M more over a lifetime. Trades dominate on debt-adjusted early-career ROI for students who *would not finish* a four-year degree. They do not dominate on lifetime earnings for students who would successfully complete degrees at selective institutions. The correct comparison is against the realistic alternative, not the ideal one.

## II. The AI & Jevons Paradox Question — What Evidence Actually Shows

The central unresolved tension:

> "The Anthropic CEO said publicly and repeatedly that AI could eliminate half of entry-level, white-collar knowledge work within years."
> — Fortune, May 5, 2026

Then reversed to invoke:

> "the 'Jevons Paradox' — the 19th-century observation that efficiency gains expand demand rather than contract it."
> — Fortune, May 5, 2026

**The formal constraint** (what determines whether Jevons activates):

An arxiv paper (Mar 2026) develops a formal VES (variable elasticity of substitution) framework showing Jevons Paradox only activates when the elasticity of substitution between AI and labor exceeds certain thresholds. It's not automatic — when AI is a near-perfect substitute for labor (rather than a complement), Jevons *fails* and displacement dominates.

> "We develop a formal economic framework to analyze whether neural scaling laws in artificial intelligence will activate Jevons' Paradox in labor markets, potentially leading to increased AI adoption and human labor substitution."
> — arxiv.org/abs/2503.05816, Mar 2026

**Empirical evidence Jevons IS working in augmentation contexts:**

> "The trend shows that more radiologists are being hired now as a result of AI."
> — NIH/PMC (PMC12959442), "Jevons Paradox: Rise of the Machines May Create More Work for Physicians," Jan 2026

> "As the cost of professional work falls as AI makes tasks more efficient, the market for those tasks will actually expand."
> — Torsten Slok, Apollo chief economist, Fortune, Apr 28, 2026

**Synthesis:** Jevons is empirically confirmed where AI *augments* human judgment (radiology, law, accounting advisory). It likely fails where AI is a near-perfect substitute for discrete separable tasks (data entry, basic code generation, bookkeeping). The Stanford study's finding — "where AI augments workers, entry-level employment holds up or grows" — is the empirical confirmation of this theoretical boundary.

**For students:** Choose work where AI augments rather than automates. The augmentation/automation distinction is the single most important variable for AI-era career durability.

## III. STEM Attrition — The Real Picture

> "Of students who started as a STEM major, 55% completed a STEM degree, 16% completed a non-STEM degree, and 29% did not complete any degree (as of 2021)."
> — NSF/NCSES, Feb 2026

The document must include the counterpoint:

> "The study finds that while many students who enter college intending to major in STEM fields leave those majors, an equal number move into STEM during college — resulting in graduating STEM cohorts that are larger than the original first-year STEM-intending classes."
> — Rutgers Heldrich Center, "New Research Finds College STEM Pathways Are a Two-Way Street, Not a 'Leaky Pipeline,'" Mar 1, 2026

**Correct interpretation:** At the *system level*, STEM isn't losing talent — it's churning it. Graduating cohorts are larger than entering ones. But at the *individual level*, a student who starts engineering at a low-support institution and doesn't finish faces a catastrophic personal outcome. Top engineering schools have 90%+ retention; non-selective schools have ~50% (ASEE). The advice "choose engineering" must be paired with "at a school where you'll finish" — this is about institutional fit, not systemic shortage.

## IV. BLS Projections — Use With Explicit Uncertainty

> "About 317,700 openings are projected each year, on average, in [computer and IT] occupations due to employment growth and the need to replace workers who leave."
> — BLS OOH, 2024-2034

This number is a *baseline scenario* that assumes pre-AI relationships between economic output and labor demand. If AI changes these relationships — which the Stanford 16% figure suggests is already happening — the BLS projection overstates future demand. Use it as an upper bound, not a certainty. The lower bound: if AI-driven productivity gains of 30-50% materialize, actual annual openings could be 160K-220K rather than 317K.

Similarly, Morgan Stanley's "$24B → $61B software development market by 2029 at 20% CAGR" is about revenue for AI coding *tools*, not headcount demand for developers. A growing market for productivity tools is consistent with *fewer* developers each doing more. Do not conflate tool market revenue with labor demand.

## V. Confidence-Rated Recommendations

### HIGH CONFIDENCE (multiple independent sources, consistent)

1. **Healthcare is structurally undersupplied through 2037.** BLS, HRSA, AACN, McKinsey all agree. Supply-constrained by faculty, not demand.

2. **The entry-level market is genuinely compressed — all majors.** The 37-year-high new-entrant unemployment share + 5.7% graduate unemployment matter more than the (historically normal) underemployment rate.

3. **Skills-based hiring will not save anyone.** 0.14% actual impact. Get the degree.

4. **Institution × major × internships = outcomes.** Georgetown's $80K spread for the same major is definitive.

5. **The demographic cliff creates leverage in the Northeast/Midwest.** WICHE data is authoritative. Students in shrinking-cohort states have bargaining power.

6. **The accounting shortage is real and the pathway is opening.** 300K lost, 10+ states reforming, $72K starting salary.

### MEDIUM CONFIDENCE (strong evidence, competing forces)

7. **CS is a reasonable contrarian bet for students enrolling now (graduating ~2030).** 8.1% enrollment decline + 14% grad decline + international exodus = likely supply tightening. BUT: AI may reduce per-unit labor demand, the information sector has lost 342K jobs (partially media/telecom, partially tech), and the historical CS trough lasted 6-7 years. Net: probably favorable by 2030 for domestic graduates with strong internships from good programs.

8. **Trades offer the best debt-adjusted early-career ROI for students who wouldn't complete four-year degrees.** 2.3:1 demand-supply, $63K with zero debt. BUT: lifetime earnings favor degree holders; physical toll is real; this is the right comparison only against the realistic alternative (no degree), not the ideal one (engineering degree from Tier 1).

9. **The Jevons Paradox will partially offset AI displacement — but only in augmentation roles.** Empirically confirmed in radiology. Theoretically bounded by elasticity of substitution. Not a blanket comfort for all knowledge work.

### LOW CONFIDENCE (contested, insufficient data, genuinely unknowable)

10. **Whether the white-collar contraction is cyclical or structural.** If structural, the traditional college → office pathway is impaired for a generation. If a one-time AI adjustment (like offshoring in the 2000s), it recovers in 3-5 years. Unknowable today.

11. **Whether CS supply tightening will outpace AI-driven demand reduction.** The key variable: does growing software *output* translate to growing *headcount*? If AI enables 50% productivity gains, 20% market growth = declining headcount. Genuinely unknowable.

12. **The Iran War's economic aftermath.** CBS News (Apr 27, 2026): "gas prices above $4 a gallon, inflation to its highest level in nearly two years." CNBC (May 5, 2026): "the war in Iran could halt that much needed progress in the labor market." If prolonged → recession → all projections invalid.

## VI. Decision Framework by Student Profile

### Profile A: High-achieving, selective institution

Any rigorous major + strong GPA + internships works. CS remains viable ($81.5K starting, $150K at elite schools). Engineering if interests align. Finance/econ for Wall Street/consulting track. Humanities defensible only with specific postgraduate plan (T14 law: 87.7% employment per ABA).

**Risk:** Complacency. Competition is 273 applications per internship slot at top companies (Handshake).

### Profile B: Mid-tier institution, wants stability

**Optimal:** Accounting (300K shortage, barrier dropping, $72K), nursing (structural demand, AI-resistant, $65K), or supply chain management (28-30% BLS growth, $75-85K from top programs). Engineering only with strong institutional retention support.

**Risk:** Prestige major without a plan. Biology without pre-med = weak employment for the bachelor's alone.

### Profile C: Non-selective institution, first-gen, budget-constrained

**Optimal:** Maximize P(completion) above all else. Nursing, accounting, IT/cybersecurity with stacked certifications. Consider CC → transfer (saves $40-80K, higher completion at destination). Trades genuinely competitive at this tier — $63K with zero debt vs. $55K with $50K debt for a general bachelor's at an open-admission school.

**Risk:** Starting but not finishing. 43.1 million Americans have "some college, no credential." At non-selective schools, this is the plurality outcome. High-structure, high-completion programs dominate high-ceiling/high-attrition ones.

### Profile D: Uncertain/undecided

**Optimal:** Business or economics (broad applicability, moderate difficulty, decent employment). Add quantitative skills regardless. Pursue internships from sophomore year. Specialize by junior year based on where you get traction.

**Risk:** Paralysis → generic degree → drift → underemployment. Any deliberate strategy beats optimization.

## VII. The Distilled Advisory (10 Sentences)

1. The entry-level market is at a 37-year worst for new workforce entrants; the composite of 5.7% unemployment + 42.5% underemployment + compressed hiring signals a structural compression regardless of major.

2. White-collar employment in finance and information has contracted for 31 months; the growth is in healthcare, construction, and services — the traditional college → office on-ramp is impaired.

3. Choose work where AI *augments* rather than *automates* — this is the single variable that predicts whether Jevons Paradox creates jobs (radiology, advisory) or displacement dominates (data entry, basic coding).

4. Healthcare is the one unambiguous structural winner: 8.4% sector growth, 10% RN shortage, 46% NP growth, 80K qualified applicants rejected annually due to faculty constraints.

5. Accounting is the hidden gem for students wanting security without STEM attrition: 300K professionals lost, 10+ states dropping the 150-hour barrier, $72K starting.

6. CS is a defensible contrarian bet for students enrolling now — 8.1% enrollment decline + international exodus creates a supply thinning — but only at programs with strong industry connections and internship pipelines.

7. Institution × internships × completion probability explain more outcome variance than major alone; the same CS degree pays $150K at Berkeley and $60K at a regional school.

8. Completion probability is the dominant risk at non-elite institutions: STEM attrition is ~45% nationally, and a degree you finish from a modest school beats a prestigious major you abandon.

9. Skilled trades represent the best debt-adjusted early-career ROI for students who would not complete four-year degrees (2.3:1 demand-supply, $63K with zero debt) — but lifetime earnings still favor completed bachelor's degrees.

10. The demographic cliff (13% decline through 2041), dead SAVE plan, and coming borrowing caps mean the financial strategy layer matters as much as the academic one.
