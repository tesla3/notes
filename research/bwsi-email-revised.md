## Why the Timing Is Perfect

BWSI's summer program is designed for **rising seniors** — students who've just finished junior year and are heading into 12th grade. That's exactly where you'll be in summer 2027. The FAQ says it plainly: "Accepted students are generally finishing their junior year."

This means:
- Summer 2026 (after 10th grade): too early — you'd be competing against older students
- **Summer 2027 (after 11th grade): your window** — you'll be the target audience
- Summer 2028 (after 12th grade): too late — they don't accept graduated seniors

You have exactly one shot at the summer program, and it's summer 2027.

## Why MAP + BWSI Work Together

- **MAP** = 1-on-1 with a UCSD mentor, ~2–4 hrs/week, Oct–May, deep dive into one field
- **BWSI** = cohort-based, structured online prereqs (Feb–Mar) + 4-week summer intensive at MIT

They tell two different stories — personal mentorship vs. structured technical credential — that strengthen each other. If your MAP mentor's field overlaps with a BWSI course (CS, bioenginetic, hardware, etc.), they reinforce each other directly.

## Framing: This Is Exploration, Not Commitment

You're still figuring out which corner of engineering grabs you. MAP lets you go deep with one expert in one area. BWSI lets you sample cutting-edge fields — autonomous vehicles, AI, robotics, quantum computing, medical analytics, cybersecurity — to see which one you don't want to put down. The point isn't to lock anything in. The point is to give yourself enough exposure to find what resonates.

## What BWSI Is

A rigorous, four-week MIT summer program for high school students, with 14 courses to choose from:

**In-person at MIT:**
- Autonomous RACECAR Grand Prix (programming robotic cars)
- Autonomous Air Vehicle Racing / UAV (drones)
- Autonomous Underwater Vehicles Challenge
- Embedded Security and Hardware Hacking
- E-Textiles and Wearable Technology
- Unmanned Air System-Synthetic Aperture Radar (UAS-SAR)

**Virtual (synchronous via Zoom):**
- Autonomous Cognitive Assistance / CogWorks (AI/ML across audio, vision, language)
- Quantum Software
- Medlytics (medical analytics / ML for healthcare)
- Remote Sensing for Disaster Response
- Serious Games Development with AI
- Cyber Operations
- Basics of ASICs (chip design)
- Microelectronics and Hardware Development

Schedule is intense: 9am–5pm (in-person) or 10am–6pm EDT (virtual), Monday–Friday, for four weeks in July.

**Cost:**
- Family income under $200,000: **free**
- Family income over $200,000: **$2,400**
- Housing not included for in-person courses (families must arrange their own)

## The Strategic Move: Fall 2026 "Girls Who Can" Saturday Program

BWSI runs Saturday programs in fall and spring to introduce students to STEM topics before the summer program. The **fall program is called "Girls Who Can"** — it's designed to encourage female students in STEM, though all BWSI programs are open to any gender identity.

**What it is:**
- Free, virtual, ~8 Saturdays (typically October–December)
- Taught by MIT faculty and Lincoln Laboratory staff
- Topics include robotics, AI, Python, drones, biotech, digital logic
- Open to students currently in 10th or 11th grade (that's you in Fall 2026)

**Why it matters:**
- Gives you prerequisite skills *before* the online courses open in February
- Connects you with an MIT instructor who could write your April recommendation
- Provides project work and context for your application essays
- Demonstrates commitment — exactly what the admissions criteria emphasize

**Timeline for "Girls Who Can":**
- Program announced in **August 2026** — registration opens shortly after
- **Open enrollment** (not competitive) — but capacity-limited, so register early
- Program runs **fall 2026**, Saturdays only
- Separate from the summer program application process

This isn't a guaranteed path into the summer program, but it's a smart way to build skills and relationships before the application window opens.

## Why I'm Emphasizing the Online Courses

The application page states it directly: **"If you don't complete the minimum amount of the online courses, you are unlikely to be accepted into the summer program."**

Your grades in the online prerequisite courses are tracked and weighed heavily. The FAQ confirms: "Enrollment for the summer program is competitive and score in the online courses are an important part in our enrollment decisions."

Most students register but don't take the online courses seriously. Starting day one and maintaining strong grades is the clearest differentiator.

## The One Timing Conflict to Plan For

IF you get into MAP (notification Aug 1–10, 2026), February and March 2027 will be your heaviest BWSI prerequisite period while MAP is still running. The mitigations:

- At your MAP orientation in fall 2026, tell your mentor about the Feb–March BWSI commitment and ask how to structure the project so milestones land outside that window
- Front-load heavier MAP work in October–January
- Finish strong in April–May after BWSI application is submitted
- Mentor flexibility varies — better to set expectations early than to go quiet mid-project

## Application Components

The summer application includes:
- **Three short essays** (500 words or less each)
- **Honors/awards** (200 character limit — be specific and concise)
- **Parent information and electronic signature**
- **Teacher or mentor recommendation** (can be a robotics coach, etc. — doesn't have to be a classroom teacher)

You'll select your top 3 course preferences. You only need ~50% completion in your preferred course before applying, but stronger progress = stronger application.

## Why This Is Worth It Regardless of the Outcome

Even if you're not accepted into the July program:
- The online courses remain open for independent learners
- Completing them earns certificates (portfolio material)
- The skills transfer directly to other summer program applications (RSI, MITES, SSP, COSMOS, etc.)
- Same recommender, same Python skills, same demonstrated initiative

And if you *are* accepted: BWSI sponsors (Raytheon, MITRE, Draper, Lincoln Laboratory) offer internships to alumni the following summer. BWSI also provides college recommendation letters through Common App and Coalition App — they have a dedicated process for this.

To say it plainly: both MAP and BWSI are exactly the kind of experiences that engineering programs notice on applications. But that's the bonus, not the reason. The reason is finding what you love and building real skills. The outcomes take care of themselves when the work is genuine.
