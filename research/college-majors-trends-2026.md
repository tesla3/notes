# Top College Majors: What US High Schoolers Are Choosing and Why (2026)

Source: Multi-source research synthesis | Date: 2026-05-10 (v4 distilled)

---

## The Big Picture: What Students Actually Choose

According to NCES data (through 2021-22) and National Student Clearinghouse enrollment data (through fall 2025), the top bachelor's degree fields by volume are:

1. **Business** — nearly always #1 since 1980. ~390,000 degrees/year.
2. **Health Professions & Nursing** — #2 and rising fast. BSN enrollment hit record 268,000 in 2024-25.
3. **Engineering** — surging. Up 7.3% in fall 2025.
4. **Biological/Biomedical Sciences** — strong growth. ~642,500 enrolled in fall 2025, up 3.7% YoY (STAT News, Jan 2026).
5. **Computer Science** — was exploding (170,000 grads in 2024, 2x from 2014), now *declining* sharply: down 8.1% at four-year schools in fall 2025 to ~606,000 enrolled (National Student Clearinghouse, Feb 2026).
6. **Psychology** — large but flat.
7. **Social Sciences** — declining.
8. **Communication/Journalism** — stable.
9. **Education** — long decline, slight recovery.

Overall postsecondary enrollment: **19.4 million** in fall 2025 (16.2M undergraduate, 3.2M graduate), up 1.0% from prior year. Community colleges saw the largest gain at +3%.

> "Across undergraduate and graduate programs, total enrollment reached 19.4 million students, growing 1.0% compared with the fall of 2024." — NPR, Jan 15, 2026

**International student decline masks domestic growth**: New international F-1 visas fell by 97,000 (Chronicle of Higher Education/Georgetown, Mar 2026). IIE reported a 17% drop in new international enrollments for 2025-26, with 96% of institutions citing visa concerns.

Sources: NCES Table 322.10; National Student Clearinghouse (Jan/Feb 2026); IIE (Nov 2025); Georgetown/Feed (Mar 2026).

## A Critical Frame Correction: Major Choice Matters — But Less Than You Think

Before diving into specific fields, the most important finding from the research deserves prominence:

> "First-year earnings for the same degree in the same major can vary by $80,000 at different colleges and universities." — Georgetown CEW, "Buyer Beware," Aug 2024

> "At $150,000, the median annual earnings two years after graduation for Berkeley computer science majors is significantly higher than those of their peers at UCLA ($130,000), Cal Poly San Luis Obispo ($114,000) and UC San Diego ($106,000)." — SF Chronicle, Mar 2025

The **institution-major-experience combination** predicts outcomes — not major alone. The note's own data shows a $44,000 spread for the same CS degree from the same state system. For students at non-elite institutions, major choice may matter less than internship quality, GPA, and network. For students at elite institutions, the network/signal may do more lifting than the major itself.

The right mental model: **Major is necessary but insufficient.** A strong major without internships, without institutional support, without real-world application — is just a transcript.

## The Entry-Level Job Market: Two Stories That Don't Add Up

The most important unresolved tension in 2026 hiring data:

### The Optimistic Signal (NACE — surveys employer *intentions*)
- Employers expect to hire **5.6% more** new graduates in 2026 vs. 2025 (NACE, Apr 2026)
- **77% of 2025 grads** found jobs within 3 months (up from 63% for 2024 grads) (CNBC, Apr 2026)

### The Pessimistic Signal (Handshake — measures *actual postings*)
> "Early talent job postings declined throughout 2025: full-time jobs are down 15% year-over-year, extending a downward shift we've been tracking since the 2021–2022 hiring surge." — Handshake, Jan 2026

> "Job postings are 2% down from last year and 12% below pre-pandemic levels. Senior pessimism has climbed from 46% to 62% from two years ago — a 16-point rise." — Handshake, Class of 2026 Graduation Report

> "Handshake reports that the average internship now attracts nearly twice as many applicants as the year before, with tech internships drawing 273 applications per posting." — Stanford Review, Apr 10, 2026

> "The class of 2026 could face the highest unemployment in years — even without a recession." — Fortune (BlackRock CEO Larry Fink), Mar 18, 2026

> "Notably, the sharpest pullback in postings last year came from government employers, driven by hiring freezes." — Handshake/CWU Career Services, Feb 2026

**Reconciliation**: NACE surveys employer *intent* months ahead. Handshake measures real *behavior*. Both can be true: companies plan to hire more but post fewer roles amid AI uncertainty, tariff disruption, and government freezes. The net effect for students: **competition per slot is intensifying** regardless of whether total jobs grow. Applications per role up 26-30% means even "in-demand" majors face brutal competition at the entry level.

## The Five Mega-Trends Reshaping Major Selection

### 1. The Computer Science Bubble Is Bursting — But the Story Is Nuanced

**The definitive number**:

> "Undergraduate enrollment at four-year institutions fell by 8.1% to about 606,000 students. Graduate enrollment dropped sharply by 14.0%, with about 190,000 students." — National Student Clearinghouse, Feb 10, 2026

> "A whopping 170,000 undergraduates majored in the field in 2024—more than double the number in 2014. And now, recent computer science graduates face some of the highest unemployment rates, at 6.1%." — Forbes, Dec 2025

> "Fresh computer science graduates are facing unemployment rates of 6.1% to 7.5%, more than double that of biology and art history majors." — Forbes, Apr 27, 2026

#### Decomposing the Decline: International Students + Sentiment + Reality

International students constitute ~20-25% of CS undergraduate enrollment and >50% at the graduate level. The 14% graduate decline and a substantial portion of the undergraduate decline are driven by immigration policy:

> "US International Master's Enrolments [are] Set to Fall 15% in 2025–26 Amid Visa and Work Uncertainty." — Collegedunia, Jan 2026

**Estimate**: If 40-50% of the CS decline is international-driven, the *domestic* CS flight is ~4-5%, not 8.1%. This strengthens the contrarian thesis: fewer international graduates = less domestic labor market competition.

#### Sub-Discipline Divergence

> "While traditional computer science, software engineering, and information systems were the most likely to report undergraduate enrollment declines, several other computing disciplines saw growth — including computer engineering, cybersecurity, and artificial intelligence (AI)." — CRA CERP Pulse Survey, Oct 2025

#### Where CS Students Are Going

> "The 8 percent decline in computer science majors last fall was nearly mirrored by a 7.3 percent increase in engineering majors." — Slashdot/National Student Clearinghouse, Apr 2026

Students pivot to: **engineering** (mechanical, electrical), **data science**, **cybersecurity/AI programs**, **nursing**, and **skilled trades**.

#### The BLS Math Still Favors CS — With Caveats

> "Overall employment in computer and information technology occupations is projected to grow much faster than the average... About 317,700 openings are projected each year, on average, in these occupations." — BLS, Aug 2025

That's **317,700 annual openings** against a shrinking graduating class of ~170,000. But BLS projects from *historical* output-to-employment relationships. If AI changes that relationship:

> "Corporate America is increasingly signaling a 'decoupling' between revenue growth and headcount growth." — Fortune/Morgan Stanley, Mar 2026

**Honest assessment**: BLS projects 317,700 annual IT openings assuming pre-AI productivity. If AI enables 30-50% productivity gains, actual openings could be 200,000-250,000. Still above shrinking supply, but thinner margins than BLS suggests.

#### The Coding Bootcamp Collapse — The "Alternative Path" Is Dying Too

The primary "alternative to a CS degree" for a decade is collapsing alongside CS enrollment:

> "Tech hiring cooled in 2022, cooled again in 2023, and has not meaningfully recovered at the entry level. The Fortune 500 companies that once ran robust junior-dev pipelines are hiring more selectively, and the late-stage startups that absorbed the overflow are either gone or running lean." — PicturingToLearn.org, Apr 2026

> "Employers are more selective. Regulators are paying attention. And AI has changed everything, not by fully replacing developers (not yet, at least), but by rewriting the rulebook on what job-ready actually means." — Forbes, "The Tech Boot Camp Shakeout," Jul 2025

**Implication**: Both the degree path (CS enrollment -8.1%) and the non-degree path (bootcamps collapsing) are contracting simultaneously. This validates that the *demand* side is genuinely compressing at entry level — not just sentiment.

#### The Underemployment Reframe

> "Computer engineering and computer science had underemployment rates below 20%, while anthropology, fine arts, and performing arts had rates above 50%." — Business Insider, Feb 2026

CS unemployment is high (~7%), but underemployment is only ~19%. Those who get jobs get *good* jobs. The problem is distributional, not structural.

#### Historical Precedent

> "Computer-science enrollments started dropping in 2001, reaching 50% of the 2000 level in 2005, and then staying flat through 2007." — ACM Communications, Nov 2009

That was a 6-7 year trough. The current decline started in earnest in fall 2024. **Why this cycle might differ**: AI code-generation is genuinely new (41% of code now AI-written, per Index.dev 2026). Stanford's empirical finding of 16% relative employment decline for young workers in AI-exposed roles is observed data, not projection. But Morgan Stanley projects the software market growing at 20% CAGR to $61B by 2029 — Jevons Paradox may expand total demand even as per-project headcount shrinks.

**Net assessment**: Students enrolling in CS now, during the panic, have a reasonable chance of graduating into a recovering market (~2029-2031). But "SWE at FAANG" may not be the default outcome it was in 2019. CS + domain expertise (healthcare, finance, defense, energy) remains powerful.

### 2. Healthcare/Nursing Is the Structural Winner

> "BSN enrollment jumped 4.9% (12,000+ students)." — Nurse.org, Apr 2026

> "More than 193,000 openings for registered nurses are projected each year through 2032." — BLS via RegisteredNursing.org

> "For 2026, HRSA projected demand for 3,393,590 RN full-time equivalents against supply of 3,043,050... roughly a 10% RN shortage." — ProLink Works, Apr 2026

> "AI has reached the nursing floor — but mostly as an add-on, not a disruptor." — McKinsey, May 5, 2026

The math: guaranteed employment, AI-resistant work, aging population, structural shortage. **But**: over 80,000 qualified nursing applicants were rejected in 2024 due to faculty shortages. Getting in is competitive.

Healthcare and social assistance is projected to have the **largest job growth** from 2024-2034: +8.4%, adding 1.7 million jobs — fully one-third of all projected growth. (BLS, Aug 2025)

#### The Physician Assistant Pathway (Underappreciated)

> "With the workforce expected to expand by nearly 30% over the next decade—adding 12,000 new positions annually—PAs are stepping into the void left by physician shortages." — HIT Consultant, Dec 2025

> "HRSA projects a total shortage of 124,180, 167,030, and 187,130 physicians in 2027, 2032, and 2037, respectively." — Journal of General Internal Medicine (Springer), May 2025

PA programs: 27 months post-bachelor's, median salary $130,020 (BLS). The new federal loan caps threaten to shrink the PA pipeline → fewer PAs → worse shortage → higher wages for completers. Students who find alternative financing face *less* competition and *better* outcomes.

#### Pharmacy: The Parallel Cautionary-Contrarian Tale

> "A 24% decline in graduates between 2018 and 2024 followed, making the pharmacist workforce pipeline a serious national problem." — UW-Madison School of Pharmacy, Apr 2026

> "The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects about 13,400 job openings for pharmacists each year. In 2026, the number of people graduating with a Doctor of Pharmacy across the U.S. will be about 8,000 — only 60% of what we need." — KSMU, Feb 2025

> "Pharmacy school applications increased 6% from fall 2024 to fall 2025." — Becker's Hospital Review, Jan 2026

Same structural pattern as accounting: massive pipeline decline → shortage → market correction. Pharmacist median salary: $136,030 (BLS). But PharmD requires 4 additional years + $150K+ debt. The EAB warns of "the rise of online prescription services, stagnant wage growth, and a slower-than-average employment projection." This is a **higher-risk** contrarian bet than nursing or accounting.

### 3. Engineering: Surging — But Not Monolithic and Not Guaranteed

The 7.3% surge is real, but requires decomposition:

#### By Employer Demand (NACE 2026)
- **Mechanical Engineering**: 61.3% of firms hiring (tied #1 with Finance)
- **Electrical Engineering**: ~55% of firms hiring (#6)
- **Computer Engineering**: Growing enrollment but 7.8% unemployment — faces same headwinds as CS

#### By AI Exposure
- **Low risk**: Civil, mechanical, aerospace, biomedical (physical systems)
- **Higher risk**: Computer engineering, software engineering (task overlap with CS)

#### The Clean Energy Demand Driver (Missing from Most Guides)

> "The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects that wind turbine service technicians and solar photovoltaic installers will be among the fastest-growing careers in the country from now through 2034." — Forbes, Sep 2025

> "Driven by increased investment, rising energy demand and government policies, clean energy jobs grew by nearly 12%, going from 3.2 million workers in 2021 to 3.[6 million]." — WRI, Dec 2025

> "According to some estimates, 336,000 manufacturing jobs will be created per year over the lifetime of the IRA, CHIPS, and Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act." — EESI, Feb 2025

Clean energy intersects with electrical engineering, mechanical engineering, and trades. Grid modernization, EV infrastructure, and renewable deployment are major demand drivers for EE specifically. **But political risk applies** — IRA tax credits face repeal pressure.

#### The Manufacturing Reality Check

The engineering narrative assumes reshoring = jobs. Reality is more complicated:

> "U.S. manufacturing employment declined for eight consecutive months in the second half of 2025. The sector shed roughly 90,000 jobs over the full year, marking the third straight year of net losses." — The Interview Guys (citing BLS data), Apr 2026

> "US employment, measured as hours worked, would decline in sectors most exposed to trade, with the biggest drops in durable goods manufacturing, mining, and agriculture." — PIIE, May 2026

> "In manufacturing, the real bottleneck is usually not the machine on the shop floor: it is the person running it." — Fortune, May 6, 2026

**Distinction**: There's a *construction boom* for new factories (CHIPS Act, IRA) but not necessarily a *manufacturing employment boom*. These are different. ME grads going into infrastructure/construction face different dynamics than those targeting factory operations.

#### The Attrition Problem

> "The American Society of Mechanical Engineers reports that approximately half of the students who begin engineering studies do not complete their degrees." — ASME (via zyBooks, 2025)

> "Of these students, 38% entered postsecondary education with a declared STEM major. 55% completed a STEM degree, 16% completed a non-STEM degree, and 29% did not complete any degree." — NSF/NCSES, Feb 2026

> "Engineering students tend to fall more into emotional exhaustion, digital boredom, incoherent peer interaction, and a very ambiguous personal and academic life." — Frontiers in Psychology, Apr 2026

Mental health is a primary *non-academic* driver of engineering attrition that most guides ignore. Schools with strong retention (Georgia Tech, Purdue: 90%+) invest in peer mentoring and support structures. At regional schools, 4-year graduation rates for EE and ME can be 37-42% (UNF data, Mar 2026). **Students should evaluate not just "can I handle the math?" but "does this school support students who struggle?"**

#### Race/Ethnicity and STEM Attrition

> "About 52 percent of Black applicants expressed interest in a STEMM education... Less Than a Third of Black Students Interested in STEMM Majors Earn STEMM Degrees Within Six Years." — JBHE, Mar 2025

> "Despite this growth, Hispanic Americans remain underrepresented among workers in STEM jobs. This is the case even though demand for STEM workers is on the rise." — Pew Research, Oct 2024

> "The proportion of future public high school graduating classes who come from underrepresented racial and ethnic backgrounds — particularly Hispanic and Multiracial graduates — will continue to increase." — WICHE, 2026

If the future college population is increasingly Hispanic and Black (per WICHE), and these students complete STEM at dramatically lower rates, the "engineering supply will grow" assumption is shakier than enrollment numbers suggest. The shrinking STEM pipeline isn't just about sentiment — it's about who enters and who completes.

### 4. The Accounting/CPA Crisis — A Hidden Opportunity

> "The accounting profession lost over 300,000 professionals between 2020 and 2024. The pipeline replacing them has shrunk by roughly 33% over the past decade." — Madras Accountancy, Apr 2026

> "The number of people sitting for the CPA exam has declined by more than 30 percent since 2016." — Wall Street Journal, 2025

> "83 percent of financial leaders said they could not find qualified accounting talent." — CFO Pulse Survey 2024

> "At least seven states — Indiana, Georgia, Ohio, Virginia, New Mexico, Utah, and Hawaii — have passed or enacted laws in 2025 that would allow aspiring accountants to trade some tuition costs for a working paycheck on their paths to obtaining a CPA license." — Empower, May 4, 2026

**Why this is the best risk-adjusted opportunity in 2026**: Same structural dynamics as nursing (boomer retirements + declining pipeline + rising demand) but with higher early-career pay ($72K starting per NACE), lower attrition (no calculus/physics gauntlet), more geographic flexibility, and states actively reducing the 150-credit-hour barrier. NACE ranks Finance #1 and Accounting #4 in employer demand (~61% and ~58% of firms hiring).

### 5. The Humanities Collapse — And Its Escape Valves

> "Degrees in English (-37%), history (-38%), and philosophy (-34%) have seen steep declines." — Enrollify, Jul 2024

> "That decline correlates to about 35,000 fewer graduates per year in the humanities." — Marketplace.org, May 8, 2026

Universities are actively *closing* these programs. But satisfaction data complicates the story: a May 2026 Frontiers in Education study found humanities/social science majors reported "higher job relevance and stronger career satisfaction scores" despite lower salaries.

#### The Law School Escape Valve (Entirely Underappreciated)

Law school — historically the primary graduate pathway for humanities, poli sci, and social science majors — is experiencing a massive boom:

> "As of today, the number of individuals applying to law school is 10.3% higher than last year at this time, and almost 32% higher than two years ago." — LSAC, Apr 2026

> "The incoming 2025 class was the largest since 2012, an 8% increase from 2024." — LSAC Knowledge Report

> "Overall, 87.7% of class of 2025 graduates from Council-accredited law schools were reported to be employed in full-time, long-term Bar Admission Required/Anticipated or J.D. Advantage jobs." — ABA, Apr 22, 2026

**The catch**: Applications surging 35% while class sizes grow only 8% means acceptance rates are plummeting. Another "oversupply into a bottleneck" story. And the bimodal salary distribution (BigLaw $235K vs. public interest $60K) means median outcomes are misleading. Debt averages $150K+.

A history or philosophy major from a strong school targeting T14 law school has a completely different outcome trajectory than the note's low salary figures suggest. But the path is getting brutally competitive.

### Skills-Based Hiring: The Emperor Has No Clothes

The "degrees are dying" narrative is empirically false:

> "While 85% of companies talk about skills-based hiring, only 0.14% of hires are actually impacted by degree requirement removal." — The Interview Guys (citing Harvard/Burning Glass research), Apr 2026

> "Mercer research published in early 2026 found that only 1 per cent of organisations have fully implemented a skills-based talent model." — InspireAmbitions, Mar 2026

> "In a 2026 Lumina Foundation-Gallup survey of 2,000 U.S. employers, 23% said they had removed degree requirements from some roles over the past three years." — BestColleges, Apr 2026

> "For every 100 job postings that drop degree requirements, fewer than 4 additional non-degree candidates are actually hired." — The Interview Guys, Apr 2026

**Bottom line**: Despite a decade of rhetoric, 99% of organizations haven't actually changed. The degree remains the baseline filter. Microcredentials and certifications are supplements, not substitutes.

### The Internship Pipeline: The Actual Hiring Mechanism

> "NACE's annual survey has consistently found that 50% to 60% of eligible interns convert to full-time employees." — NACE Position Statement

> "Internship competition has significantly increased. By January 2025, 41% of the Class of 2025 had already applied to at least one internship through Handshake; compared to just 34% of the Class of 2023 at the end of their senior year." — College Media Network

**Major + internships = outcome.** This is the actual unit of career success. Evaluate schools partly on co-op/internship infrastructure, employer recruiting relationships, and geographic proximity to industry.

## The AI Disruption Factor: Empirical Evidence (May 2026)

### The Displacement Evidence (Real, Not Hypothetical)

> "We find that since the widespread adoption of generative AI, early-career workers (ages 22-25) in the most AI-exposed occupations have experienced a **16 percent relative decline in employment** even after controlling for firm-level shocks." — Stanford Digital Economy Lab, Apr 28, 2026

> "Workers in positions like analyst, accountant, and judicial clerk between the ages of 22 and 27 have seen a somewhat greater increase [in unemployment]." — Morgan Stanley AI and Jobs Report, May 8, 2026

> "In 2026, 84% of developers use AI tools that now write 41% of all code." — Index.dev, 2026

### The Demand-Creation Evidence (Also Real — Jevons Paradox)

> "Morgan Stanley Research projects the software development market will grow at a 20% compound annual rate, expanding from $24 billion in 2024 to $61 billion by 2029." — AInvest/Yahoo Finance, Apr 2026

> "There is no empirical evidence that AI is reducing overall labor market demand — and it may be increasing opportunities for fresh graduates." — University of Maryland–LinkUp AI Maps Project (via Yahoo Finance), Apr 2026

> Dario Amodei invoked "the 'Jevons Paradox' — the 19th-century observation that efficiency gains expand demand rather than contract it." — Fortune, May 5, 2026

### The "Job Bundling" Framework (Most Sophisticated)

> "AI doesn't eliminate 'jobs' — it eliminates *tasks*. Whether a job disappears depends on whether the remaining human tasks in the bundle can be profitably separated." — CEPR Discussion Paper 21453, May 7, 2026

Nursing (many tightly-bundled physical+cognitive+emotional tasks) = resistant. Entry-level coding (loosely-bundled, separable tasks) = vulnerable.

### Synthesis for Students

Both displacement AND demand-creation have empirical support. They're not mutually exclusive:
- **Displacement is real for entry-level, task-separable work** (Stanford: -16% employment)
- **Demand creation is real at aggregate/market level** (Morgan Stanley: 20% CAGR; UMD: no reduction in overall demand)
- **Net effect depends on time horizon**: painful transition for new grads *today*, potential boom for experienced practitioners in 3-5 years

The "AI-proof" framing is wrong in both directions. The right question: **Will this major teach me to leverage AI to become more valuable, or leave me competing with AI on tasks it does better?**

### The "AI-Proof" Trap

> "Gen Z graduates who majored in 'AI-proof' careers like pharmacy, biology, and education are making less than $50,000 after graduation." — Fortune, Mar 6, 2026

"AI-proof" ≠ "well-paid." Nursing and trades are AI-resistant — but education majors earn $45K, social services $43K. The highest-paid workers in *any* field will leverage AI, not avoid it.

## The Retention Reality: Enrolling ≠ Graduating

> "Of students who started as a STEM major: 55% completed a STEM degree, 16% completed a non-STEM degree, and 29% did not complete any degree." — NSF/NCSES, Feb 2026

> "The study finds that while many students who enter college intending to major in STEM leave those majors, an equal number move into STEM during college—resulting in graduating STEM cohorts that are larger than the original first-year STEM-intending classes." — Rutgers/Heldrich Center, Mar 2026

**National average** for engineering attrition: ~50% (ASME/ASEE). At elite schools: 90%+ complete. At regional schools: 37-50% in 4 years.

**The racial dimension**: 52% of Black students express STEM interest, but less than a third earn STEMM degrees within 6 years (JBHE, Mar 2025). Structural barriers to completion differ dramatically by demographic — advice to "choose engineering" lands differently for different populations.

## The Graduate School Question

For biology, psychology, and public health, the bachelor's is essentially a prerequisite, not a terminal credential.

### The Pre-Med Pipeline

- Medical school acceptance rate: ~41-44% (AAMC)
- 55-60% of "pre-med" students never make it to medical school
- Non-science majors who complete pre-med requirements have *slightly higher* acceptance rates (AAMC/Residency Advisor)
- Students who wash out are left with a $45K biology bachelor's

**Key graduate pathways**:
- **Biology → MD**: 8+ years, $200K+ debt, $200K+ eventual salary
- **Biology → PhD**: 5-6 years funded at ~$35K stipend, then $70-100K postdoc → faculty/industry
- **Biology → PA**: 27 months, $126K+ median salary — arguably best risk-adjusted path
- **Psychology → PhD/PsyD**: 5-7 years, often funded (PhD) or not (PsyD)

### The Law School Surge

> "The number of individuals applying to law school is 10.3% higher than last year and almost 32% higher than two years ago." — LSAC, Apr 2026

> "The incoming 2025 class was the largest since 2012, an 8% increase from 2024." — LSAC Knowledge Report

> "87.7% of class of 2025 graduates were employed in full-time, long-term JD-required/advantage jobs." — ABA, Apr 2026

Law school is the primary professionalization pathway for humanities/social science majors. Employment outcomes are strong. But: applications surging 35% while seats grow 8% = plummeting acceptance. Bimodal salary (BigLaw $235K vs. public interest $60K). Average debt $150K+.

## Public Health: The Political Risk Cautionary Tale

> "The number of undergraduate public health majors has skyrocketed over the past two decades" growing by "more than 1,000 percent." — Inside Higher Ed, Jan 2023

> "'The class of 2025 graduated into one of the worst public health job markets I've seen.' Since President Donald Trump reassumed office, the United States has endured a flurry of governmental blows to public health." — Think Global Health, Dec 2025

**Lesson**: Political risk is a real factor in major choice. Government policy changes can crater a field's job market within months. Public health, environmental science, and education are disproportionately exposed to public-sector funding.

## The Financial Strategy Layer

### Tuition Discounting: Sticker Price Is Fiction

> "Tuition discount rates hit another record high... first-time, full-time undergraduates receiving a **56.3 percent** break." — Inside Higher Ed/NACUBO, Jun 2025

At private nonprofits, the average freshman pays **44 cents** per dollar of sticker price.

### The Demographic Cliff Creates Leverage — Regionally

> "WICHE projects that the total number of high school graduates will peak in 2025 before entering a period of steady decline through 2041... a 13% decline from the peak." — WICHE, 2026

**Critical nuance**: The cliff is geographically asymmetric. The **Northeast** faces 20%+ declines. The **South gains 3% overall**. Students in Massachusetts have dramatically more bargaining power than those in Texas.

### The Federal Student Loan Overhaul

> "On March 10, 2026, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit officially eliminated the SAVE plan." — StudentChoice.org, Apr 2026

> "Beginning this summer, the Department will implement commonsense loan limits on how much students and parents can borrow." — U.S. Dept. of Education, Apr 30, 2026

**What this means**:
1. New borrowing caps mean some students can't finance expensive programs through federal loans alone
2. The death of SAVE removes the most generous safety net for low-earning majors
3. The "pursue your passion and use income-driven repayment" strategy is dead
4. ROI-focused major choices are now even more critical for heavy borrowers

Healthcare pipeline specifically: loan caps → fewer PA/nursing students → worse shortage → higher wages for completers.

### Dual Enrollment: The 2.8 Million Student Strategic Weapon

> "More than 2.8 million students enrolled in DE courses in 2023-24. From 2013 to 2023, the number nearly doubled." — Columbia University CCRC, Mar 2026

> "About 1 in 10 college undergrads are in high school." — Washington Post, Feb 2026

Strategic value: 15-30 credits before graduation = a semester or year saved ($20-60K), plus more bandwidth for hard technical courses.

## The Microcredential Reality (Tempered)

> "Among professionals with microcredentials, 28% received a pay raise and 21% earned a promotion after completing their program." — The Interview Guys, Feb 2026

**What actually works**: Industry certifications from major employers (AWS Solutions Architect, CompTIA Security+, PMP) > University-affiliated certificates > Platform credentials (Coursera, LinkedIn).

**Critical reality check**: "90% of employers prefer microcredentials" likely means *in addition to a degree*. With only 0.14% of hires actually affected by dropped degree requirements (Harvard/Burning Glass), credentials supplement — they don't substitute.

## Forward Look: 2030-2032 (When Today's Freshmen Graduate)

### BLS Projections 2024-2034
- **Total**: 5.2 million new jobs, 175.2 million total employment (+3.1%)
- **Healthcare**: +8.4% (1.7M jobs, one-third of all growth)
- **IT**: 317,700 annual openings
- **Construction**: +4.4%
- **19 million openings/year** from replacements (retirements dwarf growth)

### What This Means for Today's Freshmen

1. **Healthcare demand is structural.** PA and NP roles face acute shortages through 2037.
2. **CS labor market will likely tighten** — the enrollment decline reduces supply. But AI may reduce demand. Net: probably favorable for domestic grads with strong internship experience.
3. **Trades shortage worsens** — the 2.3:1 gap isn't closing.
4. **Accounting shortage deepens** — 8 consecutive years of declining graduates + boomer retirements.
5. **The demographic cliff accelerates** — fewer graduates entering the workforce means better leverage for those who do (especially Northeast/Midwest).
6. **Clean energy employment continues growing** — but faces political risk from policy reversals.
7. **AI capabilities will be far beyond today** — any prediction about which jobs AI can/can't do is unreliable on a 4-year horizon.

## Practical Tips

### For All Students

1. **Major ≠ Career.** The unit of career success is major + institution + internships + network. Any one alone is insufficient.

2. **Don't chase yesterday's gold rush.** CS was the "safe bet" from 2012-2022. By the time everyone piled in, the market flipped.

3. **The entry-level market is tight for EVERYONE.** Postings down 15%, applications per role up 26-30%. This isn't a CS problem — it's a structural compression. Differentiation matters more than ever.

4. **The demographic cliff is your leverage — regionally.** Powerful in the Northeast (20%+ declines), less so in the Sun Belt. Private colleges already discount 56.3% of tuition.

5. **Test scores still matter.** Even at test-optional schools, 70-80% of admits submitted scores. Prepare regardless.

6. **Dual enrollment is free money.** 2.8 million students are doing this. 15-30 credits = $20-60K saved.

7. **Understand the new loan landscape.** SAVE is dead. Low-earning majors + heavy debt = financial distress, period.

### For STEM-Targeting Students

8. **Engineering specificity matters.** ME and EE are surging because physical systems resist AI. Computer engineering (7.8% unemployment) faces CS-like headwinds.

9. **Factor in attrition honestly.** 50% national average don't finish. Mental health matters as much as math ability. Ask: does this school have support structures?

10. **CS is still strong — just not a guarantee.** $81.5K starting, 19% underemployment. CS + domain expertise (healthcare, finance, defense, energy) is the smart play. The contrarian case is sound — fewer students + fewer international grads + structural demand = tighter market by 2029-2031.

11. **Data Science is promising but immature.** 36% BLS growth. But a statistics major with programming achieves the same outcome with more portability.

### For Stability-Seeking Students

12. **Nursing is structurally undersupplied.** 193K openings/year, 10% shortage. But 80K rejected applicants annually. Apply early and broadly.

13. **Accounting is the hidden gem.** 300K+ lost since 2020. CPA candidates down 30%. Seven states lowering barriers. $72K starting with no physics gauntlet.

14. **Supply chain management is the other hidden gem.** 28-30% BLS growth, $80K+ starting from top programs, AI-leveraged not AI-threatened.

15. **The PA pathway deserves serious consideration.** 30% growth, $126K+ salary, 27 months post-bachelor's.

### For First-Gen and Low-Income Students

16. **The ROI calculus is non-negotiable.** With less financial cushion, every dollar of debt matters. Prioritize: nursing, accounting/finance, engineering (if you can handle it), IT/cybersecurity, supply chain.

17. **Skills-based hiring won't save you.** Despite the rhetoric, only 0.14% of hires actually changed. The degree remains the baseline filter. Get the degree.

18. **CC → transfer is a power move.** Transfer enrollment grew 4.4% in 2024. Save $40-80K, transfer with a clear plan.

19. **Trades are high-ROI, not consolation.** $60K+ starting with minimal debt > $45K with $80K in loans.

20. **Stack certifications alongside your degree.** AWS, CompTIA, Google Career Certificates are cheap/free and differentiate in IT, cybersecurity, data analytics, and cloud roles.

### The Contrarian Views

**The humanities contrarian**: When everyone flees, remaining students get smaller classes, more mentorship, less competition for scholarships. The $125M gift to Case Western for humanities (Forbes, Apr 2026) signals smart money sees value. **But**: this path requires financial cushion, genuine excellence, and a plan (law school, policy, consulting, UX). Low-income students cannot afford this bet.

**The CS contrarian** (stronger): 8.1% enrollment decline + international student exodus + 317,700 annual openings = likely labor tightening by 2029-2031. Enrolling now means less internship competition, more faculty attention, and graduating into a recovering market. But the downside risk is real: if AI genuinely reduces headcount needs by 30-50%, the trough could last 7+ years (longer than the dot-com bust).

**The most important contrarian insight**: "AI-proof" ≠ "well-paid." Choose AI-resistant work, then add AI skills — not the other way around. The highest earners in every field will be those who *leverage* AI.
