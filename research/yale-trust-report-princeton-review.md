# Critical Review: "A Yale report blamed universities for declining public trust. Princeton professors are divided."

**Daily Princetonian, May 1, 2026 | By Devon Rudolph**

---

## I. What This Article Is

This is a thoughtful piece of campus journalism that uses the Yale "Report of the Committee on Trust in Higher Education" (released April 10, 2026) as a lens to examine Princeton's own vulnerabilities on admissions, grading, governance, and speech. Rudolph interviews four Princeton faculty — politics professor **Jan-Werner Müller**, Dean of the College **Michael Gordin**, economics professor **Owen Zidar**, and politics professor **Charles Beitz** — and also draws on Yale committee co-chair **Beverly Gage** and committee member **Sarath Sanga**.

The article is structurally competent. It does something valuable: it forces Princeton to respond on the record to a peer institution's self-criticism. But the piece — and the Yale report it covers — merit deeper scrutiny on several fronts.

---

## II. The Yale Report: Context the Article Underplays

The 58-page Yale report was commissioned by President Maurie McInnis in April 2025, co-chaired by sociology professor **Julia Adams** and history professor **Beverly Gage**, and involved 10 faculty members over a year of work. McInnis stated: **"I accept this judgment fully."**

What the *Prince* article barely touches is the **political context of the report's genesis**. Yale commissioned this self-examination *during* the Trump administration's systematic defunding campaign against elite universities. By April 2025, the administration had:

- Suspended **$210 million** in research grants to Princeton
- Cut **$400 million** from Columbia
- Put **~$9 billion** at Harvard under review
- Frozen **$175 million** at Penn

The Yale report was, in part, a strategic document — an institution publicly flagellating itself to signal reform-readiness to a hostile federal government. Müller's complaint that the report overlooked **"the most serious attack on universities in the history of this country"** is the sharpest observation in the entire article, yet Rudolph treats it as one voice among many rather than the structural critique it is.

Beverly Gage's response — that the political environment **"really wasn't the focus"** — is both technically true and politically revealing. The committee deliberately bracketed the external assault to focus on internal failings, which is either intellectual honesty or strategic capitulation, depending on your reading.

---

## III. The Trust Data: What the Numbers Actually Show

The article cites Gallup data showing confidence decline. The full picture:

| Year | % Confident in Higher Ed |
|------|--------------------------|
| 2015 | 57% |
| 2024 | 36% (all-time low) |
| 2025 | 42% (recovery) |

The **partisan decomposition** is critical and the article doesn't mention it. According to Gallup's own reporting:
- **Democrats**: 61% confident (2025)
- **Independents**: 41% confident (2025)
- **Republicans**: 26% confident (2025, up from 20% in 2024)

In 2015, Republican confidence was **56%**. The entire "crisis of trust" is overwhelmingly a story of **conservative disaffiliation from higher education**, driven by decades of political messaging. Gallup itself notes that among those lacking confidence, **38% cite "political stances or agendas"** (up from 28% the prior year).

Beitz makes a crucial observation the article underplays: issues like legacy admissions and financial aid complexity are **"constants" not "changes"** — they cannot explain a trend that accelerated after 2015. The causal logic of the Yale report is weak here. As critic **John K. Wilson** wrote in *Inside Higher Ed*: the report **"offers zero empirical evidence to connect any particular issues to 'trust,' and few logical arguments."**

Even more damning: trust **rose** in 2025 — **"the worst year for campus free speech in recorded history"** per Wilson — which directly undermines the report's thesis that campus climate drives the numbers.

---

## IV. Grade Inflation: The Real Scandal Behind the Numbers

The article notes two-thirds of Princeton grades are A-range. The fuller data is striking:

- **2002–03**: 47.9% A-range
- **2010–13** (under deflation policy): 41.8% A-range
- **2024–25**: **66.7% A-range** (record high)
- **A and A+ specifically**: 45.5% of all grades (up ~20 percentage points in a decade)
- **17 departments** gave no thesis grade below B in 2025

The COVID pandemic drove a ~10 percentage point jump between 2019–2021, and it never reverted. Princeton's 2004 deflation policy (35% cap on A's) worked — grades dropped from 47% to 41.8% — but was repealed in 2014 because students perceived it as a "de facto quota" creating competitive anxiety.

Gordin's position — **"No grade deflation panel impaneled in secret. We're not having those discussions."** — is an extraordinary admission of institutional paralysis. The university acknowledges the problem, has historical proof that policy intervention works, and yet refuses to act. The Yale report's recommendation of a 3.0 GPA mean is at least a concrete proposal.

Sanga's claim that **"rigor is established through distinguishing correct derivation from mere adequacy"** is the intellectual justification for grading as a meaningful signal. When 45.5% of grades are A or A+, that distinction has collapsed.

---

## V. The 40:1 Ratio: Gordin's Non-Sequitur

The article reports a **40:1 Democratic-to-Republican faculty registration ratio** at Princeton (from a 2020 survey). Gordin's response — **"I have not seen evidence that persuades me that students are not getting the full spectrum of intellectual views in class"** — conflates two distinct questions:

1. Do professors teach partisan content? (Müller rightly notes: **"The assumption that people teach their own beliefs, from my experience, it's just not right."**)
2. Does ideological homogeneity produce institutional blind spots in what gets researched, who gets hired, and which questions get asked?

The second question is the one that matters for trust, and no one in the article addresses it. Research on confirmation bias and group polarization suggests that a 40:1 ratio inevitably affects which paradigms dominate, which hiring committees green-light, and which intellectual traditions flourish — even if no individual professor "teaches their beliefs."

---

## VI. Admissions Transparency: The Hardest Question

The most substantive policy debate in the article concerns admissions. Key data points:

- Dean Karen Richardson's 2023 estimate: **~half of rejected applicants would qualify academically**
- Princeton's own 2024 statement: **"all but about 30 a year"** of legacy admits would have been admitted anyway
- Yale's report recommends reducing preferences for athletes, legacies, and donor-affiliated applicants

Zidar calls admissions a **"mysterious process"** and supports more transparency. Beitz notes that prestigious institutions without legacy admissions **"don't seem to suffer for it."** Both are correct.

But the deeper issue the article doesn't probe: Princeton's claim that only ~30 legacy admits per year benefit from their status is unverifiable precisely *because* of the opacity Zidar criticizes. "Holistic admissions" is structurally unfalsifiable — any individual decision can be rationalized post-hoc. The Yale report's call for transparency is not just about public trust; it's about institutional accountability.

---

## VII. Governance: Zidar's Radical Proposal

Zidar's call for faculty representation on the Board of Trustees is the most structurally consequential proposal in the article. It challenges a governance model where:

- Trustees (largely wealthy donors and alumni) set strategy
- Administrators execute
- Faculty advise (optionally)

His framing: **"As all these big strategic funding cuts and decisions are being made, I think having some views of people who are actually in the classroom, doing teaching, research is really essential."**

Beitz's counter — that trustees **"hire administrations to make recommendations"** and don't operate at detail level — actually strengthens Zidar's case. If trustees set *direction* and faculty are excluded from that level, then the institution's academic mission has no structural voice at the decision-making apex. The administrative bloat the Yale report identifies is partly a consequence of this gap.

---

## VIII. What the Article Misses

1. **The strategic context**: This report exists because Yale (and by implication all Ivies) face an existential funding threat from a hostile federal government. The "trust" framing is at least partly a survival strategy — showing willingness to reform to protect billions in grants. The article treats the report at face value rather than as a political document.

2. **The class dimension**: The report and article focus on *public* trust but never ask: trust from *whom*? The institutions serve primarily upper-middle-class and wealthy families. Working-class Americans' "distrust" of Princeton is partly rational — these institutions don't serve them and barely pretend to.

3. **The counterfactual**: If Princeton implemented all 20 Yale recommendations tomorrow, would Republican confidence in higher education rise? Almost certainly not. The trust decline is driven by a political ecosystem (Fox News, conservative media, Republican politicians) that uses universities as a cultural foil. No amount of grade reform or admissions transparency addresses that.

4. **Princeton's silence**: The article buries the lede in its final note: **"Princeton has not announced plans for a similar investigation."** After losing $210 million in federal funding, with two-thirds of grades being A's, a 40:1 partisan faculty ratio, and opaque admissions — Princeton's response is to do nothing. That silence is the story.

---

## IX. Overall Assessment

The *Daily Princetonian* piece is solid campus journalism — well-sourced, balanced in voice, and structurally clear. But it remains largely within the frame the Yale report sets, rather than interrogating that frame. The fundamental tension it doesn't resolve:

**Is the "trust crisis" a legitimate institutional failing that universities can fix through reform? Or is it primarily a manufactured political narrative that structural changes cannot appease?**

Müller seems to believe the latter. Zidar the former. Gordin hedges. The truth is likely both — genuine institutional dysfunction (grade inflation, admissions opacity, administrative bloat) exists alongside a political campaign that would attack these institutions regardless of reform.

The Yale report's deepest flaw, which the *Prince* article reproduces, is treating these as separable problems when they are symbiotic. Institutional complacency gives political attackers real ammunition; political attacks give institutions an excuse not to self-examine ("it's just partisanship"). Breaking that cycle requires naming both forces simultaneously — which neither the Yale committee nor the Princeton faculty interviewed here quite manage to do.
