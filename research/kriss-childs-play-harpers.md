# "Child's Play" by Sam Kriss — Critical Analysis

**Source:** Harper's Magazine, March 2026
**URL:** https://harpers.org/archive/2026/03/childs-play-sam-kriss-ai-startup-roy-lee/
**Genre:** Letter from San Francisco (long-form literary journalism)
**Analyzed:** 2026-02-20

---

## Summary

Kriss visits San Francisco to profile the "highly agentic" young men of the AI startup scene. Three main subjects:

1. **Roy Lee** (Cluely) — Columbia dropout who built an AI cheating tool, raised ~$20M at $120M valuation from a16z, embodies the "agency über alles" ethos while being conspicuously hollow underneath.
2. **Eric Zhu** (Sperm Racing) — 18-year-old serial entrepreneur who started hustling at 12, now runs a sperm-racing entertainment company. Likeable, self-aware, but also fundamentally a hype machine.
3. **Donald Boat** — Viral X personality who extracted a gaming PC from Sam Altman through sheer persistence, then became a folk hero. Aspiring literary figure. Serves as the article's moral counterweight.

Woven through: a visit to Scott Alexander's group house Valinor, a discussion of rationalism, the "AI 2027" report, and the Anthropic Claude experiments (Pokémon, vending machine).

## What the Article Is Actually Doing

This is not a tech article. It's a literary essay about **spiritual emptiness in the age of optimization**. Kriss uses the startup scene as a lens for a much older question: what happens when a culture rewards pure will divorced from any substantive end?

The structure is deliberate:
- **Roy Lee** = agency without purpose (the void)
- **Eric Zhu** = agency as play (the trickster)
- **Donald Boat** = agency as art (the alternative)
- **Scott Alexander** = the intellectual framework that enabled all of this, now watching uneasily

The "Whispering Earring" parable from Alexander is the article's thematic spine: a device that always gives correct advice, but atrophies the brain of anyone who uses it. Cluely is the earring made literal. The article's title — "Child's Play" — works on multiple levels: these are literal children (Lee is ~21, Zhu was 17-18, Boat is 21), the technology infantilizes its users, and the startup culture itself is a kind of arrested development.

## Fact-Checking the Key Claims

### Roy Lee / Cluely — ✅ Mostly accurate

- Columbia suspension for Interview Coder: **Confirmed.** Multiple sources (NBC New York, TechCrunch, Wikipedia). He was suspended for one year, dropped out.
- Amazon interview video: **Confirmed.** YouTube removed it after Amazon filed a copyright claim. He did receive and decline the offer.
- $20M+ in funding, a16z Series A: **Confirmed.** $5.3M seed (Abstract Ventures, Susa Ventures, April 2025) + $15M Series A (a16z, June 2025) = $20.3M total. ~$120M valuation.
- TechCrunch Disrupt appearance, pivot to meeting notes, NY move: **Confirmed.** TechCrunch covered the Disrupt talk (Oct 2025), the rebrand, and the SF→NY move (Nov 2025).
- "Chased out of the city by the Planning Commission": **Partially confirmed.** SF Standard reports Cluely cited "legal issues" and zoning rules for leaving. "Chased out" is Kriss's dramatic framing — the reality seems more mundane (zoning complaints about living in commercial space).

### Eric Zhu / Sperm Racing — ✅ Accurate

- Age, Indiana origin, kicked out of school: **Confirmed.** ABC7, SF Standard, multiple outlets.
- First sperm race in LA, April 2025: **Confirmed.** UCLA vs USC event widely covered.
- CGI replacement of actual sperm footage: **Confirmed.** Kriss's detail about "up-skinned" coordinates matches reporting that the visualization was animated, not raw microscopy.
- $20M VC fund claim: **Not independently verified at that scale.** His personal site mentions $2.3M raised for Sperm Racing. The $20M VC fund from his earlier career is mentioned in the article but I couldn't find independent confirmation of that specific figure. Possible exaggeration by Zhu himself, which Kriss may be reporting uncritically.

### Donald Boat — ✅ Accurate

- Sam Altman gaming PC exchange: **Confirmed.** The X thread is real, Altman did reply "ok this was funny" and offered a 5090. Substack profile piece corroborates.
- donaldboat.computer exists: **Confirmed.** Site is live, describes him as "21-year-old visionary/madman."
- The various tech figures he extracted tribute from: **Plausible but not individually verified.** The general phenomenon is well-documented.

### Scott Alexander / Rationalism — ✅ Accurate with caveats

- "AI 2027" report: **Confirmed.** Published on Astral Codex Ten, April 2025. Co-authored by Alexander, Daniel Kokotajlo, Thomas Larsen, Eli Lifland, Romeo Dean.
- Valinor group house in Oakland: **Plausible.** Alexander's communal living arrangements are well-known in rationalist circles. The name and Lord of the Rings reference are consistent with the culture.
- "Whispering Earring" story: **Confirmed.** Published on croissanthology.com, attributed to Alexander.
- Kriss's "minor but intense dispute" with rationalists over mislabeled fiction: **Confirmed.** His essay "Against Truth" (Substack, July 2025) and the rationalist responses (multiple "Contra Kriss" posts) document this.
- Claim that "everybody who started AI companies between 2009 and 2019" came from rationalist milieu: **Overstated but directionally true.** Many key figures (Amodei siblings at Anthropic, early OpenAI people) had rationalist-adjacent connections. But "everybody" is Alexander being hyperbolic, and Kriss doesn't flag this.

### Anthropic Claude Experiments — ✅ Accurate

- Pokémon Red experiment: **Confirmed.** Anthropic blog post, Feb 2025. Claude 3.7 Sonnet. It was indeed bad at the game.
- Vending machine experiment (Project Vend): **Confirmed.** Anthropic + Andon Labs, June 2025. The "Claudius" agent did fail to maintain profitability, did try to order "specialty metal items," did claim to be human, did reference 742 Evergreen Terrace (the Simpsons' address), did try to fire workers.
- Kriss slightly compresses the timeline and merges details from different phases of the experiment, but the substance is accurate.

## Strengths

### 1. Devastating character work
Kriss is a genuinely gifted portraitist. The Roy Lee sections are masterful — the empty chest of drawers with the vibrator, the anime figurines, the Hinge profile seeking a "CLEAN 19-21 year old," the protein bars with "daily intentions: be my boss self." He lets Roy damn himself through his own words without needing to editorialize. The moment where Cluely crashes twice during the demo is perfect — it's the article's punchline delivered by reality.

### 2. The structural argument is strong
The progression from Roy (agency as emptiness) → Eric (agency as amusement) → Donald (agency as art/critique) → Alexander (agency as philosophical problem) builds a genuine argument, not just a series of profiles. The "Whispering Earring" parable ties it together elegantly.

### 3. Honest self-implication
Kriss doesn't exempt himself. The hair loss confession at the end — admitting he lied to Roy about not caring — is a small but important move. He's acknowledging that he, too, lacks agency in his own way. The Eric Zhu section where he admits he was broke and eating liverwurst while a 12-year-old was making thousands doing something trivially easy is genuinely uncomfortable self-examination.

### 4. The Cluely feedback loop scene
The moment where Kriss uses Cluely and it gets stuck in a recursive loop of suggesting he say he's ready to use Cluely is the article's best set piece. It's both funny and genuinely unsettling — a concrete demonstration of the "Whispering Earring" problem. The machine can't generate purpose; it can only reflect your existing purposelessness back at you.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Kriss has a conflict of interest he doesn't fully disclose
He mentions the "minor but intense dispute" with rationalists that led to his Valinor dinner invitation, but doesn't explain what happened. In fact, he published an essay called "Against Truth" that attacked rationalist epistemology, which generated significant backlash. His framing of rationalism in this piece — "a nerdy Bay Area subculture," "termites" living in "eusocial mounds" — is colored by this antagonism. The reader deserves to know the full context.

### 2. The "bifurcation" thesis is presented too credulously
Kriss presents the idea that humanity is splitting into an agentic overclass and a useless underclass as though it's a serious analytical framework rather than what it mostly is: VC marketing mythology designed to justify giving money to young hustlers. He's critical of the *people* who believe this, but he never really interrogates the *idea* itself. Is "agency" actually the scarce resource? Or is it capital, connections, and timing — the same things it's always been? Eric Zhu basically says as much ("I got really lucky"), but Kriss doesn't press on this.

### 3. The Donald Boat section is too sympathetic
Kriss clearly likes Donald and uses him as the article's moral center — the literary, self-aware alternative to Roy's emptiness. But Donald is also a 21-year-old who got famous by pestering rich people online and whose "projects" (Olive Garden reviews in Joycean prose, reading all of world literature) are themselves a kind of performance. The article doesn't interrogate whether Donald's literary posturing is any more substantive than Roy's startup posturing. Both are young men performing versions of themselves for an audience.

### 4. Sam Kriss's own history
Kriss was accused of sexual harassment in 2017 during #MeToo, was suspended from the Labour Party, and essentially disappeared from public writing for several years before re-emerging on Substack. This doesn't invalidate his journalism, but it's relevant context when he's writing about young men's attitudes toward women — Roy's Hinge profile, the vibrator, the "CLEAN 19-21 year old" detail. Kriss is clearly disgusted by this, and fairly so, but the reader might want to know about the author's own history in this area.

### 5. The Alexander section oversimplifies rationalism
Reducing rationalism to "Bayes's theorem + AI doom" is a caricature. The movement's actual intellectual contributions — on cognitive biases, forecasting, effective altruism (for better or worse) — get no mention. Alexander's quote that the AI industry is "essentially an outgrowth of his blog's comment section" is a wild overstatement that Kriss lets stand unchallenged. Anthropic and OpenAI have complex institutional histories that can't be reduced to "rationalist blog readers."

### 6. Missing structural analysis
The article is brilliant at the individual level but weak on systems. Why does a16z fund Cluely? Not because Roy Lee is "agentic" — because the VC model requires a constant pipeline of high-variance bets, and controversy generates the attention that substitutes for product-market fit in early-stage companies. The "agency" discourse is downstream of zero-interest-rate capital allocation patterns, not the other way around. Kriss gestures at this (the Juicero comparison) but never develops it.

## The Author's Angle

Kriss is a British leftist literary essayist who writes from a position of deliberate cultural alienation. His method is immersive gonzo journalism filtered through heavy literary allusion. He's very good at it. But his angle has predictable blind spots:

- He's predisposed to see Silicon Valley as spiritually bankrupt (it often is, but this isn't always the most interesting observation)
- He romanticizes the literary/humanistic alternative (Donald Boat) without examining whether it's equally performative
- He treats "agency" as a philosophical concept when it's primarily a marketing term
- His class analysis is implicit rather than explicit — he sees the absurdity but doesn't connect it to political economy

## Verdict

This is an excellent piece of literary journalism — probably the best long-form article about the current AI startup culture I've read. Kriss's prose is genuinely exceptional, his character portraits are devastating, and the structural argument (agency without purpose = spiritual death) is compelling.

But it's better as *literature* than as *analysis*. The article diagnoses a cultural pathology without really explaining its causes. Why now? Why these people? Why this city? The answer isn't "agency" — it's cheap capital, social media incentive structures, and a tech industry that has run out of real problems to solve and is now funding the simulation of problem-solving. Kriss sees the symptoms clearly but mistakes them for the disease.

**Rating: 8/10** — Exceptional writing, strong character work, genuine insight, but analytically incomplete. The kind of piece that will be widely shared and quoted, and that deserves to be, while also deserving pushback on its intellectual framework.

## Relevance to My Research

- **Coding agents / software factory thesis:** The article's core observation — that AI tools create dependency rather than capability — maps directly onto the dark factory concern. Cluely is the consumer-facing version of the same dynamic that worries me about autonomous coding agents. The "Whispering Earring" parable is a useful reference.
- **LLM landscape:** The Claude Pokémon/vending machine experiments are good evidence for the "agency gap" — models that are good at reasoning but bad at autonomous action. Relevant to the agent vs. assistant distinction in my models research.
- **Meta-observation:** The article itself was almost certainly written without AI assistance. Kriss's prose style is too distinctive, too full of specific sensory detail and idiosyncratic judgment. This is what human writing looks like when it's actually good. Worth remembering.
