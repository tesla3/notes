# ISEF Preparation Guide

Source: web research (Society for Science, yriscience.com, embarkchina.org, collegebase.org) | May 2026

## What ISEF Is

Regeneron ISEF (International Science and Engineering Fair) — world's largest pre-college science competition. ~1,800 finalists from 80+ countries. $9M+ in annual prizes. Arguably the single strongest STEM signal on a college application (85%+ of finalists admitted to top-30 US universities; ~40% to Ivy/MIT/Stanford).

**Pipeline:** School Fair → Regional/County → State → ISEF

21 categories: Animal Sciences, Behavioral/Social Sciences, Biochemistry, Biomedical/Health, Biomedical Engineering, Cellular/Molecular Biology, Chemistry, Computational Biology/Bioinformatics, Earth/Environmental Sciences, Embedded Systems, Energy, Engineering Technology, Environmental Engineering, Materials Science, Mathematics, Microbiology, Physics/Astronomy, Plant Sciences, Robotics/Intelligent Machines, Systems Software, Translational Medical Science.

## How Projects Are Judged (Official Criteria)

100-point scale:

| Category | Points | What They're Looking For |
|----------|--------|--------------------------|
| Research Question | 10 | Clear, focused, novel? Addresses a gap? |
| Methodology/Design | 15 | Rigorous? Controls defined? Reproducible? |
| Execution/Data | 20 | Thoroughness of data collection & skill |
| Creativity & Innovation | 20 | Imagination, new perspectives, potential impact |
| Analysis & Conclusions | 15 | Sound interpretation? Limitations acknowledged? |
| Presentation/Interview | 20 | Understanding of basic science, ability to discuss limitations, independence |

**Two areas get outsized emphasis** per official Society for Science guidance:
1. **Creativity & Potential Impact** — does it open new possibilities?
2. **Presentation/Interview** — can the student independently explain the science, limitations, and next steps?

### What Judges Probe in the Interview

- Degree of **independence** (especially if done at a university lab — Form 1C)
- Understanding of **why** each methodological choice was made
- Honest discussion of **limitations and failures**
- Vision for **future work and real-world impact**
- Only the **current year's work** matters for multi-year projects (Form 7)

## Three Characteristics of 2025 Winners

From analysis of ISEF 2025 award-winning projects:

1. **Deep AI + Interdisciplinary Integration** — not "I used a neural network" but genuine cross-domain application (e.g., EEG-controlled prosthesis combining neuroscience + embedded systems + ML)
2. **Long-term Project Continuity** — multi-year projects showing deepening sophistication
3. **Innovation × Real-World Applicability** — not just novel, but demonstrably useful

2025 top winners: brain-controlled bionic prosthetic leg ($50K Moore Award), sustainable plastics engineering, air quality detection systems. Top prize ($100K): Adam Kovalčík of Slovakia.

## Top Strategy to Win

### 1. Get a PhD-Level Mentor (Non-Negotiable)

"Almost every top ISEF project has PhD-level mentorship behind it. Judges can instantly tell when a project is backed by advanced expertise."

How to find one:
- Cold-email professors at nearby universities. Target labs doing relevant work.
- Template: show you've read their papers, propose a specific question, ask to contribute.
- Target PhD students or postdocs (more available than PIs).
- Alternative: formal programs (RSI, PRIMES, university summer research programs).
- Parent leverage: use your professional network to get introductions.

### 2. Pick the Right Problem (Novelty > Complexity)

A simple project done brilliantly beats a complex project done poorly.

- Find a **gap** in existing literature. Read review papers' "future work" sections.
- Favor problems with access to **unique data** others can't easily replicate.
- Cross-disciplinary = competitive advantage (computational biology, environmental AI, biomedical engineering).
- Avoid oversaturated topics.

### 3. Design for the Scoresheet

Structure everything around the 100-point rubric:
- Write the research question before starting experiments (10 pts)
- Document methodology obsessively in a lab notebook (15 pts)
- Collect more data than needed; show statistical rigor (20 pts)
- Articulate what's new about the approach explicitly (20 pts)
- Analyze honestly, including null results (15 pts)
- Practice the interview until she can explain everything without notes (20 pts)

### 4. Multi-Year Strategy

If starting in 9th/10th grade:
- **Year 1:** Build foundation, compete at regionals, learn the system. May not reach ISEF.
- **Year 2:** Deepen the same project with more sophisticated methods. Much stronger chance.
- **Year 3:** Peak competitiveness. Continuation projects with compounding results dominate.

### 5. Presentation Excellence

- **Board:** Clean, professional, tells a story in 30 seconds of scanning.
- **Interview:** Practice with mock judges (teachers, scientists).
- **Key move:** Be honest about what didn't work. Judges love students who discuss failures intelligently — it proves ownership.
- **Killer question prep:** "What would you do differently?" "What are the limitations?" "How does this extend?"

### 6. Nail the Paperwork Early

- Strict forms: IRB for human subjects, IACUC for animals, SRC approval, Form 1C for lab work.
- Missing forms = disqualification. Start paperwork before experiments.
- Register with school's Scientific Review Committee (SRC) in September.

## Parent's Role

| Your Job | Not Your Job |
|----------|-------------|
| Find and facilitate mentor access | Choose the topic |
| Drive to the lab, pay for supplies | Do the experiments |
| Help with logistics/deadlines/forms | Write the paper |
| Arrange mock interview practice | Present for her |
| Emotional support through failures | Pressure for a specific outcome |

Critical rule: Judges assess student independence. If it smells like a parent project, it scores poorly.

## Quick-Start Checklist

- [ ] Identify 3 broad areas of genuine curiosity
- [ ] Read 5–10 recent papers in each area (Google Scholar, "future work" sections)
- [ ] Identify 5 potential mentors at local universities
- [ ] Draft cold emails (student writes, parent reviews)
- [ ] Register for local/regional affiliated fair (societyforscience.org)
- [ ] Start a lab notebook from day one

## Awards Structure

- **Grand Awards:** 1st–4th place in each of 21 categories ($3,000 / $1,500 / $1,000 / $500)
- **Best of Category:** Top project in each category ($5,000 + sponsored awards, often $20K+ total)
- **Top Awards:** George D. Yancopoulos Innovator Award ($100K), Gordon E. Moore Award ($50K), Craig R. Barrett Award ($50K)
- **Special Awards:** From 100+ organizations (scholarships, internships, equipment)
