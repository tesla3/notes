# The "Addicted to Being Useful" Breakdown

**Personal educational reference.** Step-by-step walkthrough of findings from the [HN thread distillation](hn-addicted-to-being-useful.md) on Sean Goedecke's article ["I'm addicted to being useful"](https://www.seangoedecke.com/addicted-to-being-useful/).

---

## The Article

Sean Goedecke is a staff engineer at GitHub. He wrote a short blog post saying: I have this compulsion where I *need* to solve problems and be useful to people. It's almost physical — I see a problem, I feel discomfort until I fix it. And my job happens to perfectly match this compulsion, so I love my work even though the industry is rough right now.

He compares himself to a character from a Gogol short story — a guy named Akaky who has a terrible copyist job but loves it because his personality is perfectly suited to terrible copyist work. He also compares himself to a working dog that does the work for the work itself, not the treats.

Then he links to his other blog posts where he gives career advice that basically flows from this trait — how to protect your time from people who'd exploit your helpfulness, how to direct your usefulness toward your management chain (the people who control your reviews and promotions), not just toward whoever asks.

The post hit 600+ points on Hacker News with 309 comments. A lot of people saw themselves in it. But the thread revealed much more than the article intended.

---

## Finding 1: The thread acts out the very thing it's discussing

The top comment, by tclancy, says: "I'm like this too, but be careful — it took decades for my wife to get through to me that not every problem she voices needs a solution. Sometimes people just want to be heard."

Reasonable advice. But then something wild happens. Dozens of HN commenters — self-identified compulsive problem-solvers — launch into an enormous debate about what "validating someone's emotions" actually means. Two users (Aurornis and n4r9) go back and forth for *twelve exchanges* arguing about whether acknowledging someone's feelings counts as endorsing their reaction.

About 40% of the thread's depth is consumed by this one tangent.

The irony: a bunch of people who identify with "I can't stop solving problems" are demonstrating exactly that by being unable to walk past a conceptual disagreement about emotional support. They're problem-solving the concept of not problem-solving. Nobody in the thread notices this.

---

## Finding 2: The article treats one thing as if it's one thing. It's actually three.

Across 300 comments, people keep saying "I'm like this too!" — but if you read carefully, they're describing completely different experiences.

**Type A: Craft pleasure.** Some people just love puzzles. They'd be doing Factorio or crosswords or tinkering with electronics if they weren't coding. The work context is almost incidental. A commenter named niedbalski captures it: *"Computers were never a job to me. They were curiosity, play, obsession."* These people aren't addicted to being useful — they're addicted to the puzzle. Other people benefiting is a side effect.

**Type B: Identity-as-useful.** This is what the author actually describes — needing to *matter* to others. The discomfort when a problem exists that you could solve but haven't. A commenter named waltwalther is a perfect example: a sysadmin in his fifties who loves every minute of his job, including being on call, including talking to salespeople. He doesn't just like the puzzles — he likes that people depend on him.

**Type C: Trauma-driven compulsion.** A commenter named tombert shared something genuinely raw. He knew a minor internet celebrity named Lowtax. He saw warning signs before Lowtax's suicide, debated doing something, and chose not to. Now he's developed what he calls an "aggressively helpful" savior complex — he compulsively helps everyone around him, partly because he's terrified of feeling guilty again. He knows it's not healthy. He can't stop.

Here's why the distinction matters: **they have completely different failure modes.**

- Type A is basically fine. If you love puzzles, you love puzzles. Nobody gets hurt.
- Type B is exploitable. If your identity depends on being useful, anyone who controls which problems you see controls your emotional state. Your employer, your family, anyone.
- Type C is clinical. It needs therapy, not career advice.

The article lumps all three together under "addiction to being useful" as if they're the same trait at different intensities. The thread shows they're different traits entirely. And the advice that works for one can be actively harmful for another — telling a Type C person to "harness their compulsion more effectively" is like telling someone with an eating disorder to channel it into competitive eating.

## Finding 3: One commenter read the author's other blog posts and found the game

A user named keybored did something nobody else in the thread bothered to do — they went and read Goedecke's other articles. And what they found reframes the whole essay.

The "addicted to being useful" post presents itself as vulnerable self-reflection. "I'm a bit dysfunctional, my job matches my dysfunction, isn't that interesting?" Charming, relatable, a little self-deprecating.

But Goedecke's other posts tell a different story:

- **"Protecting your time from predators"** — identifies people who ask for your help as potential threats. Product managers from other teams, weaker engineers who want you to do their work. The advice: give low-effort answers to low-effort questions, don't help people who can't reward you in performance reviews.

- **"Crushing JIRA tickets is a party trick, not a path to impact"** — don't be useful to the ticket queue, be useful to your management chain. The people who decide your promotion.

- **"Good times are over"** — your interests now conflict with your company's interests. If you act against the company's interests, you'll be seen as unreliable and laid off.

- **"A little bit cynical"** — keybored's summary nails it: *"Cynical just meant merrily working within the gears of the professional system. Then having no complaints about it. No commentary beyond gaining both money and pleasure from aligning with optimizing shareholder value."*

See what's happening? The "addiction to being useful" isn't an uncontrolled compulsion at all. It's a carefully managed resource. Goedecke directs it *specifically* at the people who control his career outcomes. He actively defends it from anyone who'd consume it without compensating him. He's written multiple articles about how to do this efficiently.

What the essay frames as endearing dysfunction — "I just can't help myself!" — is actually a sophisticated career optimization strategy wrapped in self-deprecating language. The working dog doesn't just do the work for the work itself. This particular working dog has read the org chart very carefully and knows exactly which sheep to herd.

keybored saw through it. Almost nobody else in the thread did.

## Finding 4: Someone asked the question the article never asks — do the problems deserve solving?

A commenter named choonway wrote this:

> *"I was like the author, then I realized that I was solving problems that were created by other peoples' incompetence. Sure they were challenging, fun but they didn't bring anything positive overall. The incompetent people are still there — causing more problems."*

This is the sharpest challenge in the entire thread because it attacks the premise, not the execution.

The article's implicit logic goes: problems exist → I feel compelled to solve them → solving them feels great → therefore this trait serves me well. Goedecke then layers career advice on top: direct the compulsion toward your management chain, protect it from predators, etc.

But choonway asks: what if the problems are junk? What if you're spending your career on an endless treadmill of firefighting that exists because the organization is broken? The compulsion doesn't distinguish. A challenging bug caused by a bad architecture decision feels exactly as satisfying to fix as a challenging bug in a well-designed system. The dopamine hit is the same. Your compulsion treats all problems as fungible sources of satisfaction.

Another commenter, jebarker, hit the same nerve from a different angle: *"99% of them feel like they shouldn't even exist and solving them doesn't really lead to any meaningful progress beyond providing me job security and money."* He asked how people deal with this.

And here's the depressing part. giraffe_lady replied that the only real answer is moving into management — because that's where you get to decide *which* problems are worth solving in the first place. As an individual contributor, no matter how senior, you're fundamentally a problem-solver. The problem-*chooser* sits above you.

jebarker's response was honest: *"Well that's not what I wanted to hear."*

This connects back to Finding 3. Goedecke's career advice is about directing your compulsion efficiently within the system. choonway is asking whether the system itself deserves your compulsion. The article has no answer for that because it never considers the question.

## Finding 5: AI doesn't just threaten the job. It threatens the self.

A commenter named Ronsenshi, who clearly identifies with the author, wrote something that sounds like a small complaint but is actually a deep fear:

> *"I actually enjoy the process of writing code, understanding deeply the system I work on, finding elegant solutions... not just a list of checkboxes with features for a given sprint that agent churns in background."*

Read that again. He's not worried about unemployment. He's worried that the *thing that makes the work satisfying* is disappearing. The deep understanding. The elegance. The craft. If an AI agent churns out the features and your job becomes reviewing its output, the compulsion has nothing to latch onto.

Then a commenter named drekipus pushed back: "I feel more useful now more than anything. The amount of AI generated planning and fluffy workloads that I've been able to just delete from the team has saved the company many engineering hours."

Sounds reassuring, right? But look at what he's actually describing. His usefulness is now *quality-filtering AI output*. Deleting bad work. He's not creating — he's curating. He's useful, sure. But the specific kind of usefulness that Goedecke's article romanticizes — the deep problem-solving, the craftsmanship, the "I'm the only person who could solve this" feeling — that's gone. drekipus inadvertently confirms exactly what Ronsenshi fears.

Ronsenshi saw this and replied: *"Reviewing code that was written by somebody else is one of the least fun and enjoyable activities in my experience. I personally don't know any programmers who enjoy the process of doing PR reviews."*

Now connect this to the article's metaphor. Goedecke says he's like a working dog — does the work for the work itself, not the treats. Working dogs are bred for specific tasks. A border collie herds sheep. A retriever retrieves. The satisfaction is in the *doing*.

What happens to a working dog when there's no work? They don't retire peacefully. They become neurotic. They chew furniture. They pace. The drive doesn't go away — it just has nowhere to go.

If your identity is "the person who solves the hard problems," and a model starts solving them, you don't lose a paycheck. You lose a self. The thread touches this for about three comments and then moves on. Nobody follows it to its conclusion.

## Finding 6: This isn't about engineering. It's about mattering.

The thread keeps trying to frame this as a software engineering thing — a personality quirk of people who like computers. But several commenters accidentally reveal it's much bigger than that.

A commenter named bradley13 told a story. An African politician visited the UK and was given a tour of social housing — the government showing off how well it takes care of people. The politician saw residents sitting around with their housing and food paid for. His reaction? *"How horrible."*

Not because the conditions were bad. Because these people had no role. No purpose. Nothing to do. No reason to exist in the eyes of their community.

Then nolamark cited a book about retirement that introduced the concept of a "mattering span" — the idea that we plan for our financial health and physical health in retirement but never plan for how we'll continue to feel like we matter. Retirees who lose their sense of usefulness don't just get bored. They collapse psychologically.

Then pjc50 added a sharp structural observation from disability rights: there are people who *can* do some work some of the time, and would benefit from it. But the benefits system forces a binary — you're either fully capable or fully helpless. So people who could contribute are trapped performing helplessness to survive bureaucratically. The system designed to help them strips them of the very thing humans need most.

And then dkarl went all the way to Marx — the idea that productive work for the benefit of others is part of the "species-essence" of humans. Not a quirk. Not a dysfunction. The baseline. He argued Goedecke has it backwards: *"If you're working for the satisfaction of being useful to others, that's not dysfunction. That's you beating the odds and having a healthy relationship to your work despite the external social pressure to make it about the money."*

This reframes everything. The article says "I'm weird because I love being useful." These commenters are saying: no, *everyone* needs to matter. You're not weird — you're just in a position privileged enough to actually get that need met at work. Most people aren't.

## Finding 7: What nobody said.

Three blind spots the thread circles but never names.

**The class dimension.** Goedecke is a staff engineer at GitHub. That means compensation probably north of $400k, high autonomy, interesting problems, smart colleagues, job security relative to most of the industry. His "addiction to being useful" feels like a gift because the material conditions are excellent.

Now imagine the same personality trait in someone making $55k doing IT support at a regional insurance company. Same compulsion — can't see a problem without solving it. Same willingness to go above and beyond. What happens to that person? They become the one everyone dumps work on. They don't get promoted because the org doesn't have a promotion ladder. They burn out. They're not a working dog having a great time — they're a working dog being run to death.

The article frames this as a personality trait with career advice layered on top. But the trait is only an asset above a certain threshold of compensation, autonomy, and organizational health. Below that threshold, the exact same trait is a vulnerability. One commenter (elzbardico) brushed against this — *"a corporate job pays much more, and the work load is a tiny fraction of other jobs"* — but treated it as a point in favor of corporate work rather than recognizing what it reveals: the advice only works if you're already winning.

**Institutional selection.** Nobody asks the systems question: do large tech companies *select for* this trait on purpose? Think about it. The interview process rewards people who light up when given a hard problem. The promotion criteria reward people who demonstrate "impact" — which is another word for being conspicuously useful. The performance review structure channels your usefulness toward your management chain specifically. The whole system is designed to attract people with this compulsion, direct it toward shareholder value, and reward just enough to keep the cycle going.

Goedecke writes as if he discovered a personality trait and then cleverly adapted it to his environment. But maybe the environment was engineered to find people like him and extract exactly this behavior. He's not a working dog who happened to find a farm. He was recruited by a farm that specifically breeds for his traits.

**The identity-fragility problem.** This connects back to Finding 5 but goes further. The thread had a couple of comments about AI and moved on. But if mattering is a core human need (Finding 6), and your way of mattering is solving hard technical problems (the article), and AI is hollowing out the craft that makes solving those problems satisfying (Finding 5) — then what's coming isn't a career disruption. It's an identity crisis at scale. Thousands of people who built their sense of self around being the person who can fix things that nobody else can fix, gradually discovering that the problems either don't need them or don't exist anymore.

The thread never connects these dots.

---

## Overall Takeaway

The article says: "I have this charming dysfunction where I need to be useful, and my job matches it perfectly."

The thread, without quite realizing it, dismantles every part of that sentence:

- It's not one dysfunction — it's three different things with different consequences (Finding 2)
- It's not charming — it can be a trauma response or a path to exploitation (Findings 2 and 7)
- It's not really a dysfunction at all — it's a universal human need that most people can't meet (Finding 6)
- The job doesn't just "match" it — the job was designed to capture and direct it (Finding 7)
- And the match may be temporary, because AI is eroding the craft that makes it work (Finding 5)

The author's own working-dog metaphor tells the real story better than he intended. Working dogs don't choose the task. They don't evaluate whether the sheep need herding. They don't question the farmer. They do the work because the work is who they are. That's fine — until there are no more sheep.
