## HN Thread Distillation: "Slop is everywhere for those with eyes to see"

**Source:** [fromjason.xyz](https://www.fromjason.xyz/p/notebook/slop-is-everywhere-for-those-with-eyes-to-see/) | [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46651443) (324 points, 137 comments, 112 unique authors)

**Article summary:** The For You Page created an illusion of infinite content, but human creativity is finite. Demand outstripped supply, so platforms fill the gap with AI slop — the same market dynamic that produces fake olive oil and adulterated honey. The author points to Vine's 2015 collapse (20 top creators walked away) as proof that platforms depend on human creators, then argues we should return to "surfing the web" as an antidote.

### Dominant Sentiment: Weary agreement, article skepticism

The thread largely agrees slop is a problem but finds the article's analysis shallow and its historical example wrong. The most engaged subthreads are about the creator-side dynamics the article barely touches.

### Key Insights

**1. The article about slop opens with slop**

Top comment (kaonwarb) catches that the very first sentence — "The size of your plate can influence how much food you eat" — cites the Wansink plate-size research, which [failed to replicate](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2129126/) in controlled studies. The irony isn't subtle: an article training readers to spot low-quality content leads with an unreplicated pop-psychology claim presented as fact. This isn't a gotcha — it demonstrates how deeply slop-like information structures have penetrated even anti-slop writing. nerdponx names it directly: *"It's certainly ironic if an article about slop leads with a tired old glob of pseudoscience slop and the author doesn't realize."*

**2. The Vine analogy is wrong, and the thread demolishes it thoroughly**

The article's central historical argument — that 20 Vine creators "took down" the platform — gets systematically dismantled. Anon1096 notes there's always a 15% bench of aspirants ready to fill any vacuum. Tanoc points out YouTube and Twitch have lost dozens of enormous channels without flinching. johnnyanmac delivers the sharpest reframe: *"'Killing a platform' is less interesting than 'birthing a competitor.' And the Mixer experiment of 2021 suggests that the top dozen creators 5 years ago still wasn't enough to even foster a new platform."* vineanddine adds the simplest explanation: Vine died because Twitter killed it, full stop. The article built its demand-exceeds-supply thesis on a misread of platform history.

**3. The algorithm doesn't just surface slop — it forces creators to produce it**

JohnMakin's first-person account as a small-scale creator (4-digit followers, 10 years streaming, <$100 earned) is the thread's star comment. The insight: *"The FYP is deciding which content it likes, which affects what creators put out, which to me destroys the entire creative process and makes slop necessary."* The most telling detail: Twitch drives more traffic to channels that show ads. The platform literally rewards you for degrading your own content. This is a supply-side mechanism the article completely ignores — slop isn't just filler for excess demand, it's what the algorithm selects for on the production side too.

**4. The economics are inverted: supply exceeded demand, not the other way around**

lotsofpulp challenges the article's core economic framing: *"I would have thought the opposite, the supply exceeded demand, driving the price so low so as to not be able to reward quality creators."* This is important because it changes the diagnosis entirely. If the article is right (demand > supply), slop is inevitable filler. If lotsofpulp is right (supply > demand), slop is what happens when abundant content competes for scarce attention and the cheapest content wins. martin-t synthesizes: *"If it takes 10x the effort to increase quality 2x, then it might be economical to produce 10x the number of videos with 1x the quality."* The thread never resolves this, but the supply-side explanation is more consistent with what we observe.

**5. "Pre-war steel" — the contamination metaphor the thread converges on**

alexpotato, who runs a niche Instagram reposting account, offers the thread's most evocative framing: finding pre-2022 content is like finding pre-nuclear-era steel uncontaminated by radiation. The metaphor works because it captures something the article's food-fraud analogy doesn't — contamination is irreversible and affects everything downstream. You can't "un-slop" a training corpus or a recommendation graph. samiwami adds data: Instagram's CEO publicly admitted the social aspect peaked around 2020. The contamination timeline maps to the generative AI timeline almost exactly.

**6. "Choosing" is doing a lot of work**

When Anon1096 argues slop is *"there because that's what humans are choosing to engage the most with,"* bandrami's one-liner lands perfectly: *"'Choosing' is doing a lot of work in that sentence."* This is the thread's sharpest philosophical moment. The entire consent framework around algorithmic feeds assumes meaningful choice, but the systems are specifically engineered to exploit attentional reflexes, not serve deliberate preferences. andai makes this concrete from the user side: *"I click on what catches my attention. Whatever stands out. Which tends to be the most clickbaity thumbnails."* Even self-aware users can't distinguish fighting the algorithm from fighting their own limbic system.

**7. The will-slop-break-the-addiction debate is unresolved and probably wrong**

OGEnthusiast hopes AI slop will be so bad it breaks screen addiction. andyfilms1 sees a plausible mechanism: the parasocial hook ("hanging out with a friend") disappears when content is obviously AI-generated. But wat10000 counters: *"A large number of extremely smart people are being paid ungodly amounts of money to enhance the addictiveness of AI output."* ares623 compresses the pessimist case to three words: *"AI slop could become digital fentanyl."* The optimist position assumes slop will remain recognizable as slop, which contradicts the trajectory of generative AI quality.

**8. The grammar subthread is the meta-irony nobody intended**

A 20+ comment chain erupts over the author's use of "exacerbated" vs. "exasperated" and spirals into "hone in" vs. "home in," "begs the question," "literally," and a full taxonomy of common errors. It's unintentionally perfect: a thread about the degradation of content quality spends its longest subthread policing surface-level language markers — the one quality dimension generative AI already excels at. AI produces flawless grammar. The deeper quality problems the article actually exhibits (non-replicating claims, bad historical analogies, unfalsifiable framing) go undetected by exactly this kind of scrutiny.

### What the Thread Misses

- **Nobody names the principal-agent problem.** Platforms are agents for users but principals for advertisers. "Engagement time" is the advertiser's metric, not the user's. Every discussion of "what the algorithm wants" is really about what advertisers pay for. The thread circles this endlessly but never states it.
- **The "open web" alternative has a discovery problem the article hand-waves.** Surfing the web requires knowing where to go. Curation was never democratic — it was always mediated by gatekeepers (blogrolls, Reddit, HN itself). The article romanticizes a past that was smaller and more exclusive, not inherently better.
- **Most human-made content was always slop.** Sturgeon's Law predates the internet. AI didn't create the problem of low-quality content; it collapsed the production cost to zero, making the ratio visible. The thread occasionally gestures at this (int_19h: *"This has already described much of pop culture for decades"*) but doesn't follow through.
- **Nobody discusses platform liability or regulation.** The entire thread operates within the frame of individual consumer choice. No one asks whether platforms should bear responsibility for the information environment they've optimized into dysfunction.

### Verdict

The article frames slop as a supply/demand problem — creativity can't scale, so platforms fill the gap with AI filler. The thread reveals this is backwards. The real mechanism is an optimization loop: platforms maximize engagement time → algorithms select for whatever holds attention → cheap, stimulating content wins → creators either produce it or disappear → the corpus degrades → AI trained on degraded corpus produces more of the same. JohnMakin's creator testimony is the thread's most important contribution because it shows the loop operating on the supply side, not just demand. The article asks "why is there so much slop?" The better question, which the thread circles but never states: *who benefits from the slop, and why does the system have no feedback mechanism for quality?* The answer — advertisers pay per impression regardless of content quality — is so obvious that nobody says it out loud.
