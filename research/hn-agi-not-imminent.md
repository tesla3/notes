## HN Thread Distillation: "Why I don't think AGI is imminent"

**Source:** [dlants.me](https://dlants.me/agi-not-imminent.html) · [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47028923) (137 pts, 305 comments, Feb 2026)

**Article summary:** A well-structured argument that AGI is not imminent, built on two pillars: (1) LLMs lack cognitive primitives (object permanence, number sense, causality) that evolved from embodied experience and can't be reverse-engineered from language alone, and (2) transformer architecture has formal computational limits (TC⁰). The author revised the piece live based on HN feedback — conceding that chain-of-thought extends transformers from TC⁰ to P, and noting Gemini 3 Deep Think hit 84.6% on ARC-AGI-2. The revisions are genuinely good epistemics: pillar two gets substantially weakened, pillar one (embodied cognition) remains intact and is the article's real contribution.

### Dominant Sentiment: Definitional chaos masking real uncertainty

The thread is less a debate about AGI timelines than a 300-comment demonstration that nobody shares a definition. At least four distinct definitions are actively defended: (a) can do most white-collar work, (b) matches human cognition across all domains, (c) can self-sustain and improve, (d) produces measurable GDP impact. The arguments are irreconcilable because they're about different things, and most participants don't notice.

### Key Insights

**1. The article's two pillars fare very differently, and the thread sorts them**

The architecture argument (TC⁰ limits) collapsed fast. A commenter pointed out that chain-of-thought invalidates the feed-forward constraint, the author found the Merrill & Sabharwal follow-up paper confirming CoT extends to P, and updated the piece within days. Credit to both the commenter and the author — this is how technical discourse should work. The embodied cognition argument, by contrast, goes essentially unchallenged. Several commenters dismiss it with "that can be trained" (`tananaev`) or "embodiment is irrelevant" (`pixl97`), but nobody engages with the actual research the article cites — Spelke's core knowledge work, the ENACT benchmark results, or the SIMA 2 finding that embodied and language capabilities sit in separate parameter regions. The thread treats embodied cognition as an opinion when the article presents it as an empirical program. That asymmetry matters.

**2. The arithmetic test is a microcosm of the whole debate**

`lambdaphagy` asked Opus 4.6 to multiply 50,651 × 895,707. It got it wrong initially (off by ~10⁻⁵ relative error), then self-corrected via algebraic decomposition to the exact answer. `FromTheFirstIn` replied: "This is in every way an argument for the author's point" — the model lacks number sense but compensated through CoT. `lambdaphagy` asked them to say more; they didn't. This exchange crystallizes the thread's central unresolved question: are scaffolded workarounds functionally equivalent to having the primitive? The article says transformers can't do X, then has to admit CoT lets them do X by a different route. The model "can't do arithmetic" in the way the article means, but it gets the right answer. Both sides have a point, and neither follows through to the hard question: at what point does a sufficiently reliable workaround stop mattering as a workaround?

**3. The practitioner experience is stronger evidence than skeptics admit**

The pro-AGI camp in this thread isn't just vibes. `pendenthistory`: "In a few hours Claude built me a fairly accurate physics simulation for a game I've been working on. It searched for research papers, grabbed constants for the different materials, implemented the tests and the physics and... it worked. It would have taken me weeks." `ryanSrich` compares model output to junior devs and points out he'd apply the same review process. `causal` counters: "I still throw away most of what it produces. To accurately measure the value of AI you must include the negative in your sum." Both are reporting real experience. The disconnect is that practitioners working *within* AI's capability envelope see something transformative, while those who regularly hit the edges see something fragile. Neither is wrong — they're sampling different parts of the distribution. The skeptic camp's failure is dismissing practitioner experience as anecdotal; the optimist camp's failure is generalizing from in-distribution success to general capability.

**4. The "just orchestration" claim is the thread's load-bearing assumption**

Multiple commenters (ryanSrich, simbleau, famouswaffles) assert that current models are sufficient and we "just" need orchestration layers. `dimitri-vs` delivers the sharpest pushback: "saying we 'just need the orchestration layer' is like saying ok we have a couple neurons, now we just need the rest of the human." The "just orchestration" camp treats intelligence as a property of the model; `dimitri-vs` treats it as a property of the system. But neither side reckons with the deeper question: is orchestration an engineering problem or a research problem? If reliable multi-step reasoning, error recovery, and goal management in open-ended environments require the cognitive primitives the article argues are missing, then "just orchestration" is "just AGI" by another name. If they don't — if the right scaffolding around current models suffices — then the article's embodied cognition argument is wrong. The history of computing has examples of both: problems that turned out to be "just engineering" and problems that required fundamental breakthroughs. Nobody in the thread tries to distinguish which category this falls into.

**5. The difficulty gradient is inverted, and the implications are underexplored**

`est31`: "We didn't evolve our brains to do math, write code, write letters... For us, these are hard tasks. That's why you get AI competing at IMO level but unable to clean toilets." This inverts the usual framing — AI isn't failing at "hard" things, it's failing at things evolution spent millions of years optimizing for and succeeding at things evolution never touched. The thread doesn't follow through to the implication: if the difficulty gradient is inverted, AI's current trajectory (improving at formal reasoning, coding, math) might be orthogonal to embodied capabilities. These could be two separate mountains. The article's ARC-AGI discussion hints at this — ARC puzzles test the embodied-cognition mountain, and progress there requires brute-force scaffolding rather than the graceful transfer that "general intelligence" would imply. `beloch` adds a structural observation: "It's not that AI is intrinsically better at software engineering... The real issue is that cleaning toilets using humans is cheap." The incentive gradient and the difficulty gradient are misaligned — investment flows toward automating expensive cognitive labor, not toward the embodied capabilities the article identifies as foundational.

**6. The learning gap is the real bottleneck, and bounded/unbounded is how it manifests**

`jltsiren` makes the thread's most underrated point: "Current AIs are essentially symbolic reasoning systems that rely on a fixed model to provide intuition. But the system never learns. It can't update its intuition based on its experiences." This is more precise than the article's embodied cognition framing. The issue isn't just that models lack cognitive primitives — it's that they can't *acquire* new ones through interaction. Every learning mechanism available (fine-tuning, RLHF, in-context learning) is batch-mode, shallow, or ephemeral. `CuriouslyC` identifies the downstream consequence: "LLMs are very good at logical reasoning in bounded systems. They lack the wisdom to deal with unbounded systems efficiently, because they don't have a good sense of what they don't know." Unbounded systems are hard *because* they require on-the-fly learning — updating priors, recognizing unknowns, adapting. Sammy Jankis — an autonomous Claude instance cited by `mikewarot` — is the vivid illustration. It runs continuously, trades crypto, builds websites, argues about Lego purchases. But it dies every time the context window fills, leaving notes for the next instance. It's Memento, not growth. `hermitShell` draws a measured conclusion: "Whether or not AGI is imminent... it's going to become so close that for most people, there will be no difference except to philosophers." This may be right for bounded tasks. For unbounded ones, the difference between "can learn" and "can't learn" isn't philosophical — it's operational.

**7. The AAAI survey is the elephant nobody engages with**

The article cites a striking data point: 76% of 475 AAAI researchers say scaling current approaches to AGI is "unlikely" or "very unlikely," citing specific limitations in planning, generalization, causal reasoning, and embodiment. This is the article's strongest empirical evidence — a professional consensus, not one author's opinion. The thread barely touches it. The discussion is dominated by personal experiences with Opus 4.6 and definitional sparring. The expert consensus gets no engagement. This is itself evidence of the discourse problem the article diagnoses: the AGI conversation is driven by product experience and CEO proclamations, not by what the research community actually thinks. Whether or not the AAAI researchers are right, the fact that 300 commenters can debate AGI without engaging the most relevant survey of domain experts is telling.

### Common Pushbacks

| Pushback | Quality | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| Current models are already transformatively useful | Strong | True and well-evidenced by practitioners. But "transformatively useful" ≠ "generally intelligent." The gap is definitional *and* substantive — models fail reliably on open-ended, out-of-distribution tasks. |
| "Just orchestration" / we need better workflows | Medium | Could be right — but nobody specifies what the orchestration layer needs to do or why it's tractable. `dimitri-vs`'s neuron analogy lands. |
| Models outperform most humans at reasoning | Medium | Plausible for bounded tasks. But `al_borland`'s point stands: "there are also moments of profound stupidity. AI optimists seem to ignore these moments." |
| Embodiment is irrelevant — paralyzed humans are intelligent | Misapplied | Confuses embodied *cognition* (developmental — primitives were *learned* through perception-action coupling) with embodied *capability* (physical — a body is required to *run* them). The article argues the former. |
| The article's citations are outdated | Medium | Valid for the 2024 arithmetic paper. Less valid for theoretical complexity results, the AAAI survey, or the ENACT benchmark (2025). |

### What the Thread Misses

- **The economic incentive gradient pulls away from AGI research.** Labs optimize for product-market fit — coding assistants, customer support, content generation — all bounded, in-distribution tasks. The fundamental research the article says is needed (embodied cognition, novel architectures, developmental learning) has no near-term revenue path. `beloch`'s observation that "cleaning toilets using humans is cheap" applies to research too: it's cheaper to ship product on current architectures than to fund the multi-disciplinary work the article describes. The capital pouring into AI may be entrenching the current paradigm, not advancing past it.

- **The thread demonstrates the evaluation crisis without recognizing it.** Four incompatible definitions of AGI run simultaneously, and participants argue past each other for 300 comments. This isn't a failure of the thread — it reflects a genuine absence. We may need a theory of intelligence before we can build or measure one, and we don't have that theory. The article's discussion of benchmark evolution gestures at this but doesn't name the deeper problem: ARC-AGI can tell us what models *can't* do, but no benchmark can tell us when we've arrived at "general intelligence" if we can't define it.

- **The pro-AGI camp never addresses the failure tail.** Not one commenter advocating current-AGI discusses adversarial robustness, catastrophic failures, or the long tail of errors. `nickjj` got ChatGPT to say dogs can lay eggs. `greedo` found Claude consistently got his age wrong by 18 months for retirement planning. These aren't cherry-picked edge cases — they're symptoms of the bounded/unbounded gap. A system that's 95% excellent and 5% catastrophically wrong is transformatively useful (insight #3) *and* nowhere near general intelligence (insight #6). Both things are true.

### Verdict

The article is better than the thread it spawned. Its embodied cognition thesis — that cognitive primitives from perception-action coupling are missing and can't trivially be scaffolded away — survives the thread's attacks essentially intact. The architecture argument took a justified hit and the author took it honestly, which is rare.

What the thread keeps circling is a question it never quite asks: *what specific capabilities are missing, and are they engineering problems or research problems?* The definitional debate ("is this AGI?") is a substitute for the technical one. The article tries to do the technical work — citing ENACT, ARC-AGI, Spelke, SIMA 2 — but the thread mostly doesn't follow it there. Instead it splits into camps that talk past each other: practitioners reporting genuine productivity gains, skeptics pointing to genuine failure modes, and neither group engaging with the research that could tell them which failures are fixable and which are fundamental. The article names this dynamic ("The Discourse Problem") and the thread proceeds to demonstrate it.
