## TL;DR

Both companies are private. Neither has a public ticker. Retail investors **cannot** buy shares directly. The landscape as of March 2026 offers five tiers of access: (1) secondary market platforms for accredited investors, (2) public venture capital funds, (3) ETFs with private holdings, (4) indirect exposure through public backers, and (5) waiting for the IPO. The right approach depends entirely on your accreditation status, risk tolerance, and how much you trust these valuations.

## II. Investment Approaches — Compared and Evaluated

### Approach 1: Secondary Market Platforms (Accredited Investors Only)

**Platforms:** Forge Global, EquityZen, Hiive, Notice

| Factor | Anthropic | OpenAI |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Availability** | Some listings; 88 live orders on Hiive (Feb 2026); demand-to-supply 17:1 | Extremely limited; 6-month+ waitlists |
| **Minimum** | $25,000–$100,000+ | $25,000–$100,000+ |
| **Fees** | 5–10% of transaction | 5–10% of transaction |
| **Share Price** | $259 (Forge), $417 (Hiive) — wide spread | Varies; opaque |
| **Transfer** | Requires company approval; indirect transfers only (SPV/forward contracts) | Requires company approval |
| **Settlement** | 45–60 days | Similar |

**Verdict:** This is the closest thing to "buying stock," but it is expensive, illiquid, opaque, and restricted. The Anthropic demand-to-supply ratio of 17:1 means you're competing aggressively for scarce shares. The $259-to-$417 price spread across platforms is a **60% variance** — a massive information asymmetry problem. For OpenAI, availability is even worse.

**Who this is for:** Accredited investors with $100K+ to deploy, high risk tolerance, and a 2–5 year hold horizon. You're paying a premium on a premium.

**Shrewd take:** The widening spread between Forge ($259) and Hiive ($417) on Anthropic reveals that "Anthropic secondary price" is not one number — it's a market with thin liquidity and wide bid-ask spreads. At $417/share on a $380B valuation, you're paying a **~10% premium to the last primary round**. At $259, you might be getting last round's price. Always verify which round's pricing the platform references. Insiders on Reddit report being offered shares at an **implied $450B valuation** for a $1M investment — that's a 18% premium over the last round. Don't confuse "access" with "deal."

**Verdict:** VCX is the **only vehicle** that gives non-accredited retail investors meaningful direct exposure to both Anthropic AND OpenAI in a single publicly traded product. 21% Anthropic + 10% OpenAI = **31% combined AI lab exposure**. No other public instrument comes close.

**Key risks and caveats:**
- **NAV vs. market price divergence.** As a new NYSE listing, VCX will trade at whatever the market will pay — which could be a significant premium or discount to its underlying NAV. Day-one euphoria could push it to a large premium.
- **Valuation methodology.** Private holdings are marked-to-model, not marked-to-market. The 63% return reflects internal fair value estimates that could be revised downward if IPO valuations disappoint.
- **Concentration risk.** 39% in two companies (Anthropic + Databricks) is aggressive. If either stumbles, the fund takes a heavy hit.
- **Redemption structure.** As a closed-end fund listing on NYSE, you can sell shares on the exchange, but the fund isn't required to buy them back at NAV. Liquidity depends on trading volume.
- **Fee drag.** Details not yet fully public; historically Fundrise charges management fees.

**Shrewd take:** This is the most accessible and potentially the smartest retail play. But **do not buy VCX at a significant premium to NAV on listing day.** The smart play is to wait for the initial hype to settle and buy at or near NAV. The underlying holdings are real and valuable — but you need to be buying the portfolio, not buying the FOMO.

You won't 10x if Anthropic IPOs at a premium, but you also won't get wiped out if the bubble deflates. The Microsoft-OpenAI relationship is the single most important corporate partnership in tech — MSFT is the best risk-adjusted way to play the OpenAI thesis.

### Approach 5: Wait for the IPO

**Anthropic:** Hired Wilson Sonsini (Dec 2025). No S-1 filed. Realistic timeline: late 2026 to 2027. Would be one of three mega-IPOs (alongside OpenAI and SpaceX) with combined ~$2.9T estimated market cap.

**OpenAI:** Hired CAO and IR lead (Jan 2026). Informal bank talks. S-1 expected mid-2026. Targeting Q4 2026 at $830B-$1T. Would be among the largest tech IPOs ever.

**Verdict:** If you're a retail investor, this is the **simplest and most rational** approach. You get:
- SEC-mandated disclosures (S-1 with actual financials)
- Transparent pricing
- Liquid markets
- Regulatory protection

**Key IPO risks:**
- Historically, most IPOs are flat-to-negative after the first day once the hype settles
- Lock-up period expirations (typically 90-180 days) often create selling pressure
- The Reddit consensus is blunt: "never invest in an IPO" and "pretty obvious this is gonna be one big pump and dump it all in the hands of retail bag holders" (top-voted comments)
- **The Musk lawsuit** (spring 2026, $79-134B damages) could disrupt OpenAI's IPO timeline

**Shrewd take:** The market has a term for buying IPO allocations: "the winner's curse." The shares allocated to retail are the ones institutional investors didn't want. The smart IPO play is to wait 6-12 months post-listing for price discovery, lock-up expiration selling pressure, and the first few quarterly earnings reports to reveal actual unit economics. You will miss the first-day pop (if any) but avoid the inevitable post-IPO correction that hits most mega-cap listings.

## IV. Risk Assessment

### Risks Common to Both

1. **No moat.** Zero switching costs. Models commoditizing rapidly. Open-source (Llama, DeepSeek, GLM-5) closing the gap within months. Benedict Evans's analysis is damning: the model is "just an API call."

2. **Inverted scaling economics.** Unlike SaaS, AI inference costs scale with usage. Free users are a cost center, not a monetization funnel. This is structurally different from every successful tech business model of the past 20 years.

3. **Circular financing.** Nvidia invests in OpenAI → OpenAI buys Nvidia GPUs → Nvidia revenue inflated → Nvidia stock rises → funds further investment. The same dynamic is emerging with Anthropic. The entire AI capex web has fragile interdependency.

4. **Regulatory and geopolitical.** EU AI Act, potential US regulation, China achieving full-stack independence (GLM-5 on Huawei Ascend). Bank of England warned valuations are "materially stretched" and near dot-com levels.

5. **Valuation gravity.** At $380B and $830B respectively, these are priced for perfection. Any stumble — regulatory, competitive, technical — triggers a correction that hits pre-IPO investors hardest because they can't sell.

### Anthropic-Specific Risks

- **Smaller scale** means less margin for error
- **Concentration of backers** who are also competitors (Google, Amazon)
- **IPO market absorption** — three mega-IPOs (SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic) in the same cycle is unprecedented; the market may not have enough capital for all three at target valuations

### OpenAI-Specific Risks

- **Musk lawsuit** ($79-134B damages, spring 2026 trial) — existential legal risk
- **Cash burn** ($115B cumulative through 2029) — HSBC projects a $207B funding shortfall by 2030
- **Key talent departures** (Sutskever, Schulman, others left for competitors)
- **Market share erosion** (ChatGPT web traffic share dropped from 86.7% to 64.5% in 12 months)
- **"Discussion of cash burn is taboo at the firm"** — The Economist, citing a VC. This is a red flag.

## V. The Shrewd Investor's Playbook

### If you have <$25K and are NOT accredited:

1. **Buy VCX on NYSE** (listing ~March 9) — but WAIT for the hype to settle. Don't buy day one. Target NAV or slight discount. This gives you 21% Anthropic + 10% OpenAI in one ticker.
2. **Buy Microsoft (MSFT)** for OpenAI proxy exposure.
3. **Research Zoom (ZM)** as a hidden Anthropic leverage play — $51M investment now worth $2-4B, representing up to 20% of ZM's market cap.

### If you are accredited with $100K+:

1. Everything above, plus:
2. **Register on Forge/Hiive/EquityZen** for secondary market access — but be extremely price-disciplined. Don't pay >10% premium to last primary round.
3. **Consider concentration carefully.** A $100K secondary share purchase in Anthropic at $380B valuation needs the company to reach $760B+ for a 2x. That's a higher market cap than all but the very top public companies. The upside math gets hard at these levels.

### If you want to be maximally shrewd:

1. **Don't buy either company directly at current valuations.** The risk/reward at $380B (Anthropic) and $830B (OpenAI) is worse than it was at $60B and $157B. You're not an early investor — you're buying the last round before IPO.
2. **Buy the infrastructure.** Nvidia, Broadcom, and the cloud hyperscalers capture AI revenue regardless of which lab wins.
3. **Buy the winners of the second derivative.** Companies that USE AI effectively (productivity gains, margin expansion) rather than companies that SELL AI (commodity pricing pressure, massive capex).
4. **If you MUST have direct exposure,** VCX at NAV is the most capital-efficient path. 31% combined Anthropic+OpenAI exposure, diversified across other private tech, publicly traded, daily liquidity.
5. **Set a calendar reminder for 6-12 months post-IPO** for either company. That's when the real price discovery happens. Lock-up expirations, first earnings reports, and the S-1 financial disclosures will tell you what private investors never saw.

## VI. Verdict

**Anthropic is the better investment thesis** — faster growth, cleaner governance, earlier path to profitability, more diversified strategic backing, stronger enterprise traction. It is cheaper on a price-to-sales basis and has fewer existential risks than OpenAI.

**OpenAI has the stronger brand** and consumer dominance, but its cost structure is terrifying, its governance history is messy, and the Musk lawsuit is a wildcard that could delay or destabilize the IPO.

**Neither is cheap.** At $380B and $830B, these are priced for a future where AI transforms every industry and these labs capture a significant share of the value. The base case for both is good — real products, real revenue, real growth. But the difference between "good company" and "good investment" is price. At these prices, the margin of safety is razor-thin.

**The most important insight from the research:** The Economist nails it — "Unlike conventional software companies, which generate more profit the more they scale, AI firms face higher costs the bigger they get." This inverted scaling dynamic is the structural bear case for every AI lab, and no amount of revenue growth fixes it until inference costs fall dramatically or someone finds a moat that sticks.

The shrewd play is patience, diversification, and buying the thing that's underpriced — which in March 2026 is almost certainly not the AI lab that just raised at $380B, but the boring public company sitting on a $2-4B Anthropic stake that the market hasn't noticed yet.
