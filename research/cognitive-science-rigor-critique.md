# Cognitive Science: Rigor Critique — Is It Soft Science with a CS Veneer?

> Critical evaluation of the argument that CogSci is mostly soft science, and that applied math / EE / rigorous CS provides more durable long-term training, especially in the post-AI era.

---

## The Argument, Steelmanned

1. CogSci is predominantly soft science (psychology, linguistics, philosophy, anthropology) with a thin computational layer on top
2. Mathematical/engineering training is more durable — the math doesn't get revised, the psychology findings do
3. In a post-AI world, the "synthesize across disciplines" skill that CogSci claims as unique is exactly what LLMs do well — that was CogSci's moat, and it's eroding
4. Therefore: applied math, EE, or rigorous CS provides better long-term ROI

---

## Where This Argument Is Correct

### 1. The general BA track IS mostly soft science. Full stop.

Look at what's actually required for a UCSD CogSci BA:
- Calculus (Math 10 series — the easier one, not the 20 series)
- One stats course
- One programming course
- COGS 1, COGS 10 (introductory)
- COGS 101A-B-C (sensation/perception, learning/memory, language — these are psychology courses)
- COGS 107A-B-C (neuroanatomy, systems neuro, cognitive neuro — biology-adjacent)
- COGS 100 (distributed cognition — theoretical/philosophical)
- COGS 108 (data science — one real computational course)
- Electives (wide range, can be soft or hard depending on choice)

**That's one calculus sequence, one stats class, one programming class, and then a psychology + neuroscience core.** Calling this "interdisciplinary with strong computational foundations" is generous. It's a psychology degree with extra steps and a more interesting intellectual framing.

### 2. Mathematical maturity IS more durable than domain knowledge

Real analysis doesn't get replicated out of existence. Maxwell's equations don't have a replication crisis. Linear algebra works the same way it did 50 years ago and will work the same way in 50 more.

CogSci's empirical content has a shelf life:
- The "magical number seven" (Miller, 1956) has been revised downward to ~4 chunks
- Many priming studies failed replication
- The "embodied cognition" findings are mixed and contested
- Even Chomsky's universal grammar has been significantly challenged

An applied math or EE graduate's core training doesn't depreciate this way. The foundations transfer across paradigm shifts. You learn optimization, you can optimize anything — today's neural nets, tomorrow's whatever-comes-next.

### 3. The post-AI point has teeth

CogSci's claimed distinctive skill is interdisciplinary synthesis — reading across fields and finding connections. This is genuinely what LLMs do well. An LLM can read a neuroscience paper, a philosophy paper, and a linguistics paper and produce a coherent synthesis faster than any human.

If your main value-add is "I can think across disciplinary boundaries," you're competing with tools that do this at machine speed. That's a shrinking moat.

### 4. Career data backs this up

Applied math, EE, and CS graduates have:
- Higher starting salaries
- More straightforward career paths
- Less need to explain their degree to employers
- Better positioning for the technical roles that command premium compensation

CogSci graduates frequently report: "nobody knows what my degree is" and "I need a master's to be competitive." That's real signal about market valuation.

---

## Where This Argument Is Wrong or Incomplete

### 1. It conflates the general BA with the entire field

The ML & Neural Computation track at UCSD requires:
- Full calculus series (Math 20A-B-C-E — the hard series)
- Linear algebra (Math 18)
- Probability theory (Math 180A)
- 6 programming courses
- Machine learning (COGS 118A/B)
- Neural networks and deep learning (COGS 181)
- Computational modeling (COGS 109)

That's **not** soft science with a CS veneer. That's a legitimate quantitative degree. Similarly, the PhD level — Tenenbaum at MIT doing Bayesian cognitive modeling, Voytek at UCSD doing neural data science — is as mathematically rigorous as anything in CS or statistics.

**The critique applies to the soft tracks. It does not apply to the entire discipline.**

### 2. "Soft science" is doing hidden work as a value judgment

Experimental psychology uses the same statistical machinery as any empirical science — hypothesis testing, effect sizes, confidence intervals, increasingly Bayesian methods. The replication crisis hit psychology hard, but:
- It hit medicine and biology too (Ioannidis, "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" — that was about medical research, not psychology)
- The crisis is a sign of self-correction, which is how science is supposed to work
- Physics has its own embarrassments (string theory: 40+ years, zero empirical predictions)

The difficulty of the subject matter (human cognition) doesn't mean the researchers are less rigorous. It means the phenomena are harder to isolate and measure. An EE student working with circuits has clean, repeatable signals. A cognitive scientist studying decision-making has a noisy, non-stationary biological system that lies to you on surveys. The latter is harder to study rigorously, not easier.

### 3. "Understanding how humans think" is not a depreciating asset

You argue that hard math/engineering training is more durable. True for the *formal tools*. But consider what becomes MORE valuable in an AI-saturated world:

- **Who decides what AI systems should optimize for?** Not the math. The understanding of human values, cognition, and error patterns.
- **Who designs human-AI interfaces?** People who understand both computation and human perception/attention/memory limits. Pure CS people are famously terrible at this.
- **Who evaluates whether an LLM "understands" language?** You need CogSci's framework (Marr's levels, the Chinese Room argument, theories of representation) to even formulate the question properly.
- **Who designs AI alignment strategies?** This requires deep understanding of human cognition, values, and decision-making — exactly what CogSci studies.
- **Who detects when AI systems are failing in cognitively dangerous ways?** Bias, hallucination, persuasion — these are cognitive phenomena.

The post-AI era doesn't just need people who can build AI. It needs people who understand the human side of the human-AI system. That knowledge is CogSci's domain.

### 4. The "AI replaces synthesis" argument cuts both ways

Yes, LLMs can synthesize across papers. But they can also:
- Write code (threatens CS)
- Solve differential equations (threatens applied math)
- Design circuits (threatens EE)
- Prove theorems (threatens pure math)

If "AI can do what you do" is the argument, it applies to EVERY field. The question isn't whether AI can replicate a skill — it's whether the *human understanding* behind that skill remains valuable for directing, evaluating, and correcting AI output. CogSci's understanding of how minds work is at least as durable as an engineer's understanding of how circuits work, in this framing.

### 5. Applied math / EE have their own depreciation problems

- Much of classical EE (analog circuit design, signal processing) is being automated
- Applied math without domain expertise is "I can solve equations but I don't know what to model" — not obviously more marketable than "I understand human cognition but need more math"
- EE enrollment has been declining for years; the field is increasingly absorbed into CS
- Pure math/CS training without understanding of human factors produces technically correct systems that humans hate using (see: every enterprise software product ever)

## The Nuanced Verdict

### Your argument is about 70% right — but for the wrong scope

**For the general CogSci BA**: Yes, it is mostly soft science with computational garnish. For pure long-term career ROI, applied math or rigorous CS is a better bet. You are correct.

**For the ML/Neural Computation track or PhD level**: No, it's a legitimate quantitative discipline that produces people who can do things CS graduates can't (understand human cognition deeply) and things psychology graduates can't (build computational models). The critique doesn't land here.

### The real question isn't "CogSci vs. applied math"

It's: **which CogSci track?**

| Choice | Long-term durability | Honest assessment |
|---|---|---|
| CogSci general BA | Low | You're right — soft science with computational garnish. Get a master's or pivot. |
| CogSci ML/Neural Computation | Medium-High | Legitimate quantitative training + domain expertise in cognition. Defensible. |
| CogSci Design & Interaction | Medium | UX is a real career but the ceiling is lower than engineering. AI tools are already eating entry-level UX. |
| CogSci PhD (computational) | High | These people get hired by DeepMind, Google Brain, top neuro labs. Serious training. |
| Applied Math BS | High | Strong formal foundations. But you still need domain expertise in *something*. |
| EE BS | Medium-High | Declining enrollment, but signal processing + ML is a strong combo. |
| Rigorous CS BS | High | Best raw career optionality. But produces people who don't understand users. |

### What I'd actually advise

**If the goal is durable training with maximum optionality:**

1. **CS or applied math as the primary degree** — you're right, the formal foundations are more durable
2. **CogSci as a minor or second major** — the intellectual perspective is genuinely valuable, but as *augmentation*, not as the foundation
3. **OR: CogSci ML track treated as a de facto CS degree** — take every hard math and CS course available, use the CogSci framing to add domain depth

The worst move: a general CogSci BA with no computational spine, hoping the "interdisciplinary perspective" alone will carry you. It won't.

### The post-AI twist you might be missing

Here's where I'll push back hardest on your framing:

The post-AI era doesn't just reward people who can build technical systems. It rewards people who understand **what the systems are for** — what humans need, how humans fail, how humans and AI interact. This is not a trivial skill and it's not something applied math or EE teaches you.

The most valuable person in 2030 isn't the pure engineer OR the pure cognitive scientist. It's the person who has **engineering-grade technical skills + deep understanding of human cognition**. That's the CogSci ML track done right, or it's a CS major with serious CogSci coursework.

Your framing of "hard science vs. soft science" is real but incomplete. The actual axis that matters is: **can you build AND can you understand the humans who use what you build?** The disciplines that teach only one of those are each incomplete.
