## HN Thread Distillation: "EU–INC – A new pan-European legal entity"

**Source:** [eu-inc.org](https://www.eu-inc.org/) / [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46703763) (765 points, 726 comments)

**Article summary:** EU-INC is a lobby/policy campaign (22,000+ signatories) pushing for a "28th regime" — a single, optional EU-wide legal entity for startups that sits alongside national company structures. Announced by von der Leyen at Davos in January 2026. Key features: central EU registry, 48-hour fully-digital incorporation, standardized investment docs and stock options. Local taxes and employment law remain national. Still pre-legislative: a public consultation opened July 2025, closes September 2025. Earliest implementation ~2027. Builds on existing 28th-regime precedents (Societas Europaea, unitary patent, etc.) but targets startups specifically, unlike the SE which requires €120k capital and can only be formed from existing public companies.

### Dominant Sentiment: Cautious support, deep structural skepticism

The thread broadly *wants* this to work but doesn't believe it will. The skepticism isn't anti-EU reflex — it's informed pattern-matching from people who've actually incorporated across multiple EU jurisdictions and know where the real friction lives.

### Key Insights

**1. The proposal's own bullet points undermine its thesis**

"Local taxes & employment" is listed as a feature — the idea being that EU-INC is *pragmatic*, not utopian. But multiple commenters identify this as the line that defeats the purpose. **egorfine**: *"Completely and totally defeats the purpose."* If you still face 27 different employment law regimes and tax codes, you haven't solved the cross-border operations problem. You've solved the *letterhead* problem. The incorporation friction the proposal targets (notaries, registries, capital requirements) is real but is the *smaller* half of the cost. The larger half — ongoing compliance, VAT, permanent establishment rules, CFC taxation — is explicitly carved out. **adamcharnock** illustrates: form an Irish company from France, and France will demand you file French corporation tax anyway because you have a "permanent establishment" there. EU-INC doesn't touch this.

**2. The thread's sharpest insight: dissolution is harder than formation, and nobody's talking about it**

**jandrewrogers**: *"The real, massive difference between the US and EU is how hard it is to shutdown a company and the legal implications of that... Many countries effectively treat it as a criminal proceeding."* **jan_Inkepa** adds that lawyers commonly advise re-seating a German company in the UK just to dissolve it — the extra step is *cheaper* than native dissolution. The EU-INC proposal focuses entirely on the birth of companies and says nothing about death. This is a classic policy blind spot: optimizing for the photogenic metric (speed of creation) while the painful metric (cost of failure) goes unaddressed. For a startup ecosystem, failure tolerance is the *more* important variable.

**3. Cross-border investment is the real bottleneck, not incorporation speed**

**bootsmann**: *"It's almost impossible for a fund from Germany to invest in a company from France and vice-versa. It is a significant problem!"* **tietjens** makes the same point to a skeptical Danish founder who doesn't see the value: the gain isn't faster incorporation (Denmark already has that), it's standardized investment documents that let capital flow across borders without bespoke legal work per jurisdiction. This is where EU-INC's "standardized investment documents" bullet is genuinely novel. The thread underweights this — most commenters fixate on incorporation speed (the flashy 48-hour claim) rather than the capital-flow plumbing. The real comparison isn't "Delaware formation in 3 days" — it's "any US VC can invest in any Delaware C-corp with a standard SAFE."

**4. Regulation vs. Directive is existential and under-discussed**

**rsynnott** and **MadsRC** flag the critical fork: if EU-INC is implemented as a Directive, each member state interprets and transposes it nationally — producing 27 flavors of EU-INC, which is exactly the current problem wearing a new hat. If it's a Regulation, it applies uniformly without national transposition. **MadsRC**: *"So 27 individual implementations of this, as opposed to the current 27 different implementations of how to incorporate and assign equity? Seems… silly?"* Politico already reported (July 2025) that "EU's dream of 1 startup regime risks shattering into 27 pieces." Von der Leyen's speech language suggests Regulation, but the legislative sausage-making hasn't started. This single variable — Regulation or Directive — will determine whether EU-INC is transformative or decorative.

**5. Societas Europaea already exists and its limitations are instructive**

Multiple commenters ask "how is this different from an SE?" — and the answer is revealing. The SE requires €120k capital, can only be formed from existing national public companies, and carries heavy governance overhead. It's used by Airbus, SAP, LVMH — not startups. **bux93** argues the fix is trivial: *"Needs like a few sentences changed in the existing regulation to extend that to private companies."* If that's true, it raises the question of why a whole new regime is needed rather than an amendment. The cynical answer: because "EU-INC" is a better campaign brand than "SE Reform Act." The charitable answer: the SE's governance model is fundamentally wrong for startups and a ground-up redesign is warranted. The truth likely sits in between — but the SE's 20-year track record of being ignored by startups is the strongest evidence that the *design* matters enormously, not just the existence of a pan-EU option.

**6. The "it's fine in my country" fallacy**

Nordic commenters (Denmark, Sweden) consistently express puzzlement — **oellegaard**: *"I'm based in Denmark and have incorporated multiple companies. It is very easy."* This is genuine but parochial. The proposal isn't primarily for Danes incorporating in Denmark. It's for a French founder with a German co-founder hiring in Spain and raising from a Dutch VC. The variance across EU jurisdictions is enormous: Estonia's e-Residency takes days, Germany's GmbH formation takes weeks and €25k. **adamcharnock** lived the gradient: UK (30 minutes, £20) → Portugal (15-20 days, notaries) → Germany ("definitely worse"). The proposal targets the worst-case jurisdictions and the cross-border use case, neither of which the Nordic founders experience.

**7. The cultural/investment climate problem is upstream and untouched**

**carlosjobim** delivers the thread's most developed contrarian argument: *"Americans love new things and new ideas and see them as opportunities. Europeans see them as threats."* The hostile environment isn't legal structure — it's banking relationships, regulatory attitude, and investment culture. **ThomPete** adds the structural argument: *"Since its inception in 1993, not a single global top 100 company has been created by any of the EU countries."* His three-point diagnosis — EU solves problems politically that startups solve better, extreme precautionary principle, impossibility of challenging regulation — positions EU-INC as treating a symptom while the disease is institutional. These arguments are partially valid but also partially self-defeating: you can't fix culture with a policy, but you can remove the structural barriers that reinforce the cultural dysfunction. Standardized investment docs won't make Europeans braver, but they'll reduce the penalty for trying.

### Common Pushbacks

| Pushback | Quality | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| "Local taxes & employment defeats the purpose" | **Strong** | The carved-out problems are bigger than the solved ones |
| XKCD 927: 28th standard just adds complexity | **Medium** | Valid concern, but 28th regime is *optional overlay*, not replacement — the mechanism differs from standards proliferation |
| "Registration is already easy" (Nordic/UK view) | **Misapplied** | True locally, misses the cross-border and worst-case-jurisdiction use cases |
| "Cultural problem, not structural" | **Medium** | Real but counsel of despair — structural fixes can shift cultural equilibria over time |
| "SE already exists, just fix it" | **Medium** | Underestimates how much SE's design assumptions (public company, €120k, heavy governance) are baked in |

### What the Thread Misses

- **Geopolitical timing is the real accelerant.** mrtksn briefly mentions that US-EU deterioration would create "instant trillion Euro market" for EU tech, but the thread doesn't develop this. The *reason* von der Leyen announced this at Davos in January 2026 is that EU strategic autonomy became urgent after the US political shift. EU-INC isn't primarily a startup policy — it's an economic sovereignty play dressed in startup language.

- **The employee equity problem is enormous and barely discussed.** Stock options are taxed wildly differently across EU member states — in some jurisdictions, employees owe tax on *grant* (before any liquidity event), making equity comp effectively unusable. This is the single biggest reason EU startups can't compete with US startups for talent: they can't offer meaningful equity packages. EU-INC's "standardized EU-wide stock options" bullet, if actually delivered, would be more transformative than the incorporation reform itself. The thread almost entirely ignores this.

- **Nobody asks who *opposes* this and why.** National registries, notary professions, local corporate lawyers, and member states that currently attract incorporation (Ireland, Estonia, Netherlands) all have structural incentives to water this down. jcfrei gestures at this (*"countries fear they will lose out on taxes"*) but there's no serious analysis of the political economy of opposition.

### Verdict

The thread correctly identifies that EU-INC optimizes for the wrong metric — speed of formation rather than cost of operation — but then mostly argues about whether *formation speed even matters*, missing the proposal's genuinely novel element: standardized investment infrastructure. The real bet here isn't that 48-hour incorporation transforms European startups. It's that uniform investment documents, a single registry, and standardized equity frameworks lower the friction for *capital allocation* across borders — turning the EU's fragmented €450M-person market into something a VC can actually treat as one market. Whether that bet pays off depends entirely on a single legislative choice (Regulation vs. Directive) that the thread mentions but doesn't sufficiently emphasize. If it's a Directive, this is GDPR cookie banners all over again — 27 national interpretations of a good idea. If it's a Regulation, it's the first serious structural reform to EU startup infrastructure in decades. The irony is that a proposal explicitly designed to reduce fragmentation will itself be shaped by the very fragmentation it aims to fix.
