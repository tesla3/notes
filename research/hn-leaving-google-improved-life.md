## HN Thread Distillation: "Leaving Google has actively improved my life"

**Source:** [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47184288) (465 pts, 257 comments) · [Article](https://pseudosingleton.com/leaving-google-improved-my-life/) · Feb 2026

**Article summary:** A personal blog post describing the author's experience switching from Google services (Gmail → Proton, Search → Brave/DDG). Claims are thin: inbox is cleaner, searching is "fun again," conscience improved. The author admits still using YouTube and several other big tech services. No career reflection — this is about being a *user*, not an employee, despite the clickbaity title.

### Dominant Sentiment: Skeptical but sympathetic with a Kagi infomercial

The thread partially agrees with the impulse to de-Google but ruthlessly dismantles the article's specific claims. The real energy goes into relitigating search engine quality, where Kagi emerges as the consensus winner in a way that borders on astroturf (though the accounts check out — sustained engagement, technical specificity, willingness to name tradeoffs).

### Key Insights

**1. The article's evidence undermines its own thesis**

The two concrete "improvements" — a cleaner inbox and more intentional browsing — have nothing to do with Google. A cleaner inbox comes from a fresh email address with disciplined signup hygiene, which you can do on Gmail. "Going directly to IMDB or Wikipedia" instead of searching is a behavioral change, not a platform change. Multiple commenters land this punch. **Aurornis**: *"Most of the improvements he cited were either unrelated to Google or things he could have turned off in Gmail."* The article is a post-hoc rationalization dressed as a product review.

**2. DDG is the wrong horse and the thread knows it**

The article claims Brave and DDG are "better than Google for >90% of searches." The thread's top comment (Aurornis, heavily upvoted) immediately demolishes this: DDG is fine as a bookmark engine but objectively worse for anything requiring depth. The pattern repeats across dozens of accounts: tried DDG → ended up `!g`-ing 15-80% of queries → either went back to Google or found Kagi. **bartread**: *"8 or 9 times out of 10 I switch to Google search… it reminds me of AltaVista."* The structural reason, which the article never mentions: Reddit has an exclusive indexing deal with Google. DDG literally *cannot* index Reddit, and Reddit is now a primary source for authentic human opinions on products, health, travel, etc. This isn't a quality gap — it's a market-structure moat.

**3. Kagi is the actual story the article should have written**

The thread converges overwhelmingly on Kagi as the real Google alternative. The enthusiasm is notable for its specificity: people cite years of usage, concrete comparison anecdotes, and willingness to pay ($10/mo). **TexanFeller**: *"Kagi is what Google would have been if they had kept improving instead of transitioning to enshitifying ~10yrs ago."* **drcongo**: *"It only took me a couple of weeks to go from 'I can't ever imagine paying for search' to 'I will never use free search again.'"* The Kagi consensus reveals something important: the demand for paid, ad-free search is real and growing, but it's still an enthusiast market. Nobody in the thread addresses whether Kagi's economics actually work at scale, or whether they survive Google deciding to offer an ad-free tier.

**4. The SEO attacker-defender asymmetry is the real search crisis**

The most structurally insightful comment comes from **Arainach** (Google employee): *"Around the world there are orders of magnitude more people trying to game the search results… those people have a combined budget dramatically larger."* Search quality degradation isn't Google being lazy — it's an arms race where the attackers have orders of magnitude more resources and personnel than the defenders. This frames the entire "Google sucks now" narrative differently: every search engine will converge toward the same failure mode as it scales, because scale is what attracts the SEO army. Kagi's small user base may be its moat, not just its limitation.

**5. De-Googling has a counter-narrative that doesn't get written up**

**neya** provides the most valuable counter-experience: switched to Proton two years ago, now stuck in limbo. Proton's email search is so bad they still forward everything to Gmail for searching. *"I didn't realise how important searching your email was until I was at a bank and they kept asking me some documents from years ago."* Phone clients are "unusably slow." Can't switch back because of accumulated Proton aliases. This is the de-Google experience nobody blogs about — the one where you realize Gmail's search was a core feature, not a nice-to-have, and now you're locked into a worse provider.

**6. The "free internet" debate reveals a false dichotomy**

**WarmWash** frames the core tension: people want free, ad-free, and privacy-respecting, which is a trilemma. But **simon666** offers the sharper take: *"we all pay for access to the internet already. The problem is a shit-ton of monied interests want to squeeze every possible dollar."* **bubblewand** adds the mechanism nobody discusses: actually-free services get *suppressed* by free-with-ads services because the latter have marketing budgets and cross-promotion. The ecosystem is structurally hostile to the hobbyist/volunteer alternative, not because people won't use it, but because they can't find it.

**7. The "big tech is bad" claim goes completely unexamined**

Multiple commenters (**arthurjj**, **elxr**, **jajuuka**) flag that the article drops "big tech is bad, and Google is one of the worst" as self-evident truth while still using Amazon, Spotify, Uber, DoorDash, and Apple. The article never explains *why* Google is worse than these others or what "bad" means specifically. **jajuuka** draws a sharp (if unflattering) parallel: *"These types of articles always remind me of conspiracy theorists. Being so excited to… view themselves as unique individuals and in control again."* Harsh, but the article does read more like identity performance than analysis.

**8. YouTube is the elephant that eats the room**

The article admits YouTube is the exception. The thread barely pushes on this, but it's the load-bearing wall of the whole argument. YouTube is where Google's actual consumer lock-in lives — network effects, creator economics, no credible alternative. If you're still on YouTube (logged in, feeding the recommendation algorithm), you haven't "left Google" in any meaningful data-extraction sense. You've just rearranged the deck chairs.

### Common Pushbacks

| Pushback | Quality | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| DDG/Brave are better than Google for 90% of searches | Weak | Thread overwhelmingly contradicts this; DDG-to-Kagi is the real pipeline |
| Gmail has no differentiating features | Medium | Spam filtering and search are genuinely best-in-class per multiple users, including Proton converts |
| "Just turn off the features" solves Gmail complaints | Strong | Aurornis and others show the article's complaints were about optional, toggleable settings |
| Google profits enormously from "free" users | Strong | Factually true but the article doesn't engage with what this means or why alternatives would be different |
| Paying for services solves the problem | Misapplied | **aaaronic** notes Apple/Amazon/Spotify all productize paying users too; payment ≠ alignment |

### What the Thread Misses

- **LLMs as the real de-Googling force.** A few comments mention Claude or ChatGPT replacing search, but nobody connects the dots: the de-Google movement's biggest accelerant isn't Kagi or DDG — it's LLMs making traditional search less necessary. The article is fighting the last war.
- **Email portability is the structural barrier, not habit.** The thread treats de-Googling as a willpower problem. It's actually an infrastructure problem: email is a primary key for digital identity, and switching providers means updating hundreds of accounts. Nobody proposes standards-level fixes (email forwarding doesn't solve the identity problem).
- **The Kagi sustainability question.** Dozens of enthusiastic Kagi endorsements, zero discussion of whether a paid search engine survives Google offering an ad-free tier, or whether Kagi's reliance on third-party indexes (including scraped Google results) is legally durable.
- **Google Workspace vs. consumer Google.** The thread conflates them. Enterprise users can't "just leave" — their employer chose Google. The article's framing of individual choice ignores that most Google usage is institutional.

### Verdict

The article is a genre piece — the "I left X and my life improved" personal essay that HN upvotes reflexively and then eviscerates in comments. The thread is more interesting than the article because it surfaces a structural tension the author never engages with: Google's search quality *has* declined, but the alternatives are worse for different reasons (smaller indexes, no Reddit access, weak local results), and the one alternative that actually works (Kagi) requires payment that most people won't make. The real de-Googling isn't happening through principled consumer switching — it's happening through LLMs quietly making search irrelevant for the queries that matter most. The article is celebrating leaving a building that's already being demolished.
