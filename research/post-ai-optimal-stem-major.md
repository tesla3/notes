# What STEM Major Best Prepares You for the Post-AI Era?

> First-principles analysis grounded in economics, technological evolution, and human nature.

---

## 1. The Core Framework: Two Independent Axes

Most analyses collapse two distinct dimensions into one. Separating them is the single most important move.

**Axis 1 — Knowledge Half-Life.** Some knowledge is permanent (calculus, thermodynamics, evolution, systems architecture thinking). Some depreciates in years (specific languages, frameworks, API knowledge, routine analytical procedures).

**Axis 2 — AI Vulnerability.** Some work is execution within a defined frame (pattern matching, code generation, textbook problem-solving, optimization over known objectives). Some is judgment about the frame itself (choosing the right problem, operating under genuine novelty, bearing accountability).

These axes are **independent**, which is why naive analyses fail:

| | AI-vulnerable (execution) | AI-resistant (judgment) |
|---|---|---|
| **Long half-life** | Solving textbook DiffEq — permanent knowledge, but AI does it | Deciding which physical model applies to a novel failure — permanent knowledge, AI can't |
| **Short half-life** | Writing React components — depreciating knowledge, AI does it | Choosing architecture for a novel system in today's tool landscape — depreciating knowledge, but the judgment is real |

**The optimal training maximizes both axes: long-half-life knowledge AND judgment capability.** Sitting at a "deeper layer of reality" (physics > chemistry > biology > CS) only helps on Axis 1. It says nothing about Axis 2. A physics grad solving textbook problems has durable knowledge but vulnerable skills. A CS architect making novel design trade-offs has less durable knowledge but more durable judgment.

## 2. What AI Actually Commoditizes — and the Economic Law That Follows

Every general-purpose technology commoditizes the current value layer and shifts value to what it can't reach. AI commoditizes **cognitive execution** — writing, coding, analysis, pattern matching, optimization.

**The critical correction:** AI does not eat reality "top-down" through an abstraction stack. It automates execution at ALL layers simultaneously. AlphaProof does mathematical reasoning. GNoME discovers 2.2M crystal structures. AlphaFold solves protein folding. The durable human advantage is judgment *at whatever layer you operate*, not sitting at a "deeper" layer.

### The scarce-complement principle

The most important economic law for career strategy: **value accrues to the scarce complement of whatever becomes abundant** (Hal Varian, chief economist at Google, generalizing from decades of GPT research). When AI makes cognitive execution abundant, value shifts to what execution *needs but can't self-provide*:

- **Correct problem framing** — execution is useless without the right problem
- **Real-world data and physical implementation** — AI generates plans; someone must act in atoms
- **Accountability and trust** — outputs must be owned, certified, defended
- **Evaluation and quality judgment** — someone must know when execution went wrong
- **Novel objective-setting** — what to optimize for in the first place

This is not speculation. It's the observed pattern of *every* prior GPT. Cheap steel made bridge *design* valuable, not bridge *building*. Cheap compute made software *architecture* valuable, not *coding*. Cheap cognitive execution will make framing, evaluation, physical implementation, and accountability valuable — not execution itself. The career question reduces to: **which training develops the scarce complements of abundant AI?**

### Two complications most analyses miss

**Speed changes strategy.** Previous technology transitions took 50–100 years. AI plays out in years. The direction of value migration is predictable; the speed is not. A 50-year transition lets you retrain mid-career. A 10-year transition doesn't. This makes career entry speed a first-order variable, not a footnote (Section 5).

**GPTs create new categories.** The steam engine created railroads and urbanization. The internet created social networks and the attention economy. AI will create valuable work categories that don't exist yet. Any framework that only asks "what survives?" is incomplete. The best hedge is maximizing learning velocity — the ability to recognize and enter new categories fast.

## 3. The Judgment Moat: Real but Narrower Than Assumed

AI is already performing routine judgment: medical diagnosis, risk assessment, strategic recommendations, code review, hypothesis generation within known paradigms. "AI can only execute, not judge" was approximately true in 2023. Less so in 2026.

**What remains durably human is four specific things, not "judgment" broadly:**

1. **Judgment under genuine novelty** — situations with no historical analog, where the distribution itself is unknown (Knightian uncertainty). AI trained on past data structurally struggles here.
2. **Judgment with accountability** — someone signs the certification, takes the liability, goes to jail. This is structural (legal/regulatory/institutional), not capability-based. It persists even if AI is technically better.
3. **Judgment involving taste and values** — what *should* we optimize for? What's worth building? These require choosing objectives, not optimizing given ones.
4. **Meta-judgment about AI** — knowing when AI output is wrong, when the training distribution doesn't match deployment, when the model is confidently hallucinating. Requires deep domain expertise.

**Critical distinction: structural moats vs. capability moats.** Types 2 and 3 are *structural* — they persist regardless of how capable AI becomes, because they depend on legal systems, human institutions, and the irreducible nature of values. Types 1 and 4 are *capability moats* — they're real today but narrow over time. Meta-judgment about AI (#4) is explicitly a **wasting asset**: as AI systems improve at self-evaluation, calibration, and uncertainty quantification, the human edge in "catching AI errors" diminishes. Train for the structural moats (accountability, taste), not just the capability ones. Deep domain expertise still matters for #4, but expect this moat to be substantially narrower in 2035 than in 2026.

These four are reinforced by structural human demands that don't depend on capability comparisons: humans require **trust with skin in the game** (a body, a reputation, something to lose), **meaning and status signals** (human-made, human-judged output becomes scarcer and more valued as AI floods every channel), **physical presence** (no AI builds bridges, performs surgery, fixes grids), and **someone to blame** (governance, regulation, and liability require human agents).

**Implication for training:** Developing "formal reasoning" is necessary but insufficient. You need training that puts you in genuinely novel situations with real consequences, builds domain expertise deep enough to catch AI errors, and develops taste. Generic "learn to think" is too vague. The target is these four specific capabilities — weighted toward the structural moats.

## 4. Where Capital Is Flowing

**Physical-world transformation (trillions in capital):**
- Energy — batteries, grid, solar, fusion, nuclear, hydrogen
- Biotech/pharma — drug discovery, gene therapy, synthetic biology, precision medicine, aging
- Advanced manufacturing — semiconductors, materials science, additive manufacturing
- Infrastructure — everything old needs rebuilding
- Robotics — bridging AI and the physical world
- Space — launch, satellites, resource extraction

**AI infrastructure:** alignment/safety, reliable deployment at scale, evaluation/monitoring/governance.

**Frontier science:** novel materials, quantum computing, neuroscience, climate modeling — AI accelerates within-paradigm; humans set agendas and recognize paradigm shifts.

### Honest mapping to workforce

| Sector | Who actually does this work | Physics relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Energy transition | EE, ChemE, materials science | Indirect |
| Biotech/pharma | Biology, chemistry, bioengineering | **Minimal** — you cannot derive pharmacology from the Hamiltonian |
| Semiconductors | EE, materials science, ChemE | Moderate |
| Robotics | EE, MechE, CS (controls/perception) | Indirect |
| Infrastructure | Civil, MechE | Minimal |
| AI systems | CS, applied math, EE (signals/ML) | Indirect |
| Fusion/nuclear | Physics directly | High |

Physics is *adjacent* to most high-value sectors but *directly optimal* for almost none. The "universal adapter" narrative is real but carries hidden retraining cost and career-entry delay.

**The biology blind spot.** Biotech is likely the single largest physical-world value creation opportunity of the coming decades. Biology has its own irreducible first principles — evolution, the central dogma, thermodynamic constraints on living systems — that are genuine foundations, not stamp-collecting. You cannot derive the Krebs cycle from quantum mechanics. Emergent complexity is real.

**Geographic constraint — the trade-off nobody mentions.** Physical-world work is geographically tethered. You build batteries in factories, not apartments. You operate power grids on-site. You do wet-lab biology in labs. Semiconductor fabs are in specific locations. CS/AI work can be done from anywhere. This is a real life-planning variable: physical-world careers offer AI-resistance but constrain where you live. For some students this is a feature (stable local demand, less global competition); for others a genuine constraint (limits optionality, concentrates in specific metros). The analysis doesn't resolve this trade-off — it depends on personal values — but ignoring it is a blind spot in most career frameworks.

## 5. What Develops the Skills That Matter — and When

### The six ingredients

1. **Formal reasoning depth** — math through at least multivariable calculus, linear algebra, DiffEq, probability/statistics; ideally one proof-based course. The compounding engine.
2. **One domain of genuine depth** — deep enough to evaluate AI output critically and know when it's wrong without being told.
3. **Physical-world fluency** — lab work, manufacturing, building with atoms. Where AI is weakest and capital flows.
4. **Serious computational fluency** — not "Python basics" (that's 2026 table stakes) but systems thinking: decomposing problems for AI agents, evaluating outputs, building reliable pipelines. A core competency, not an accessory. 4–6 serious CS courses or equivalent project work.
5. **Learning velocity** — the ability to rapidly acquire new domain knowledge. Developed through multi-domain exposure and deep-enough foundations that new knowledge has structure to attach to. Possibly the most important meta-skill in a fast-disruption world.
6. **Breadth** — problem-framing is cross-domain. Taste develops through confronting many types of problems with real stakes.

### The career-entry variable: why it's first-order, not a footnote

**Judgment develops through real problems with real consequences, not coursework.** Research, internships, co-ops, projects that ship — these build pattern recognition about what matters. Two years of real-world problem-solving builds more judgment than two years of additional theory.

This creates the central strategic tension:

**The transition period (2025–2045)** is when current students will build careers, networks, reputations, and compound judgment. Computational depth is at peak leverage. Sectors absorbing talent have specific skill demands. Getting started 2–3 years earlier compounds through the period of maximum disruption.

**The equilibrium (2050+)** is when mathematical depth and physical intuition pay their full compound returns. Foundations don't churn. Physical-world fluency becomes increasingly valuable as AI penetrates digital work.

**If you optimize for the equilibrium, you may not survive the transition.** A beautiful theoretical foundation doesn't help if you can't get hired for three years. But optimizing purely for the transition risks depreciation when the equilibrium arrives.

**Resolution:** This is a Pareto optimization, not single-axis maximization. The best training provides *enough* formal depth for long-term compounding *and* enough practical applicability for immediate career entry. This is why "physics is the best major" is overfit — it maximizes one axis. The engineering disciplines (EE, MechE, ChemE) sit closer to the Pareto frontier.

### Graduate school as strategic variable

In many physical-world sectors — energy, biotech, semiconductors, materials science — a graduate degree is effectively required for the judgment-level work this analysis values. A BS ChemE designs within existing processes; a PhD ChemE invents new ones. A BS biologist runs assays; a PhD biologist designs experiments and evaluates AI-generated hypotheses.

This changes the calculus significantly:

- **If grad school is likely:** undergraduate major matters more for foundational depth than career entry. Physics becomes more viable because the career-entry penalty is deferred. The 4-year ROI frame is wrong; it's a 6–10 year frame.
- **If terminal BS:** career entry weight increases. Engineering disciplines win because they provide the shortest path to judgment-building real-world work.
- **The hybrid path (MS, not PhD):** 1–2 year specialization after a strong BS. Increasingly the optimal path for physical-world sectors: BS in EE/MechE/ChemE → MS specialization in energy/robotics/materials. Shortest total investment for judgment-level positioning.

The analysis in Section 6 assumes terminal BS unless noted. Students planning graduate school should weight Axis 1 (foundations) more heavily and Axis 6 (career entry) less.

### Sub-field choice matters more than major choice

Within any major, the sub-field specialization is often the bigger decision. EE (embedded systems + ML) is a fundamentally different career from EE (power systems). CS (distributed systems + AI infrastructure) is fundamentally different from CS (web development). Physics (condensed matter experiment) leads to very different outcomes than physics (string theory).

**The implication:** the department you register in matters less than the specific courses, labs, projects, and research experiences you choose. A MechE student who takes 4 CS courses and does robotics research has a stronger profile than an EE student who takes the default curriculum. Evaluate *programs* (what does this specific university's EE department emphasize?) not *majors* in the abstract.

### The self-teach asymmetry: real, general, and frequently misapplied

The commonly cited version: "Physics is harder to self-teach than programming." True, but misidentifies what's special. The deeper principle:

**Domain-to-tools is easier than tools-to-domain, in every field.** A biologist can learn Python more easily than a programmer can learn molecular biology. A mechanical engineer can learn ML more easily than an ML researcher can learn thermodynamics. A physicist can learn programming more easily than a programmer can learn physics. The asymmetry isn't physics-specific — it's that *deep domain knowledge* is the harder-to-acquire complement, and *computational tools* are the easier supplement, in every field.

Specific implication: **major in your deep domain; supplement with computation.** This holds for physics, biology, ChemE, and MechE students equally. The "physics is hard to self-teach" argument is a special case of a general principle that applies just as strongly to every rigorous domain.

**Caveat 1 — computational depth is underestimated.** "Learn Python on the side" was adequate in 2015. In 2026, effective AI-augmented work requires genuine systems thinking — understanding architectures, evaluation pipelines, deployment constraints. This isn't weekend-project territory. Budget 4–6 serious courses or equivalent project depth.

**Caveat 2 — AI tutoring may weaken this asymmetry.** If AI makes self-teaching physics or biology dramatically more effective (personalized explanations, adaptive problem sets, instant feedback), the "hard to self-teach" argument weakens over time. Khan Academy + GPT-4 is already measurably better than Khan Academy alone. In 10 years, the self-teach asymmetry may be substantially smaller. Don't overweight it in long-term planning.

## 6. The Assessment

### Rating methodology

Rated for the **median graduate** of a solid-but-not-elite program, not the ceiling. Every major looks brilliant at its best; what matters is the typical outcome.

**Critical methodological note: training effects vs. selection effects.** Ratings reflect what the *curriculum develops*, not who *self-selects into* the major. This distinction matters enormously. Physics attracts high-aptitude students, inflating outcome data (GRE scores, LSAT scores, career earnings). CS (systems/AI) attracts practically ambitious students. EE "modern track" students self-select for cross-disciplinary thinking. Throughout this table, we attempt to isolate training effects — what does the curriculum itself build, holding student aptitude constant? Where selection effects are likely distorting common perceptions, we note it explicitly.

**Column definitions:**
- **Math** — depth of formal mathematical training (proof exposure, abstraction level, breadth of tools)
- **Physical** — exposure to physical-world systems (lab work, hardware, atoms-not-just-bits)
- **Compute** — computational fluency (programming depth, systems thinking, data pipelines, AI tool usage)
- **Judgment** — extent to which curriculum forces real trade-offs, novel problem-solving, and evaluation under uncertainty (lab debugging, design projects with physical constraints, open-ended research) — distinct from domain knowledge per se
- **Domain** — depth and specificity of domain knowledge applicable to high-value sectors in Section 4
- **Entry** — speed and ease of career entry for median graduate (time to first meaningful role)
- **Durability** — resilience of career positioning across AI-disruption scenarios over 20+ years

| Major | Math | Physical | Compute | Judgment | Domain | Entry | Durability |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **EE (modern)** | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★★ | **Highest** |
| **Physics + CS depth** | ★★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★ | **Highest** (requires supplement) |
| **CS (systems/AI)** | ★★★ | ★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | **High** |
| **MechE** | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | **High** |
| **ChemE** | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★★ | **High** |
| **Physics (pure BS)** | ★★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★ | High (entry friction) |
| **Applied Math** | ★★★★★ | ★★ | ★★★ | ★★★ | ★★ | ★★★ | High (needs domain) |
| **Comp. Biology** | ★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★★★ | ★★★★ | High (sector tailwind) |
| **CS (general/web)** | ★★★ | ★ | ★★★★★ | ★★ | ★★★ | ★★★★★ | **Medium** |
| **Biology** | ★★ | ★★★★ | ★★ | ★★★ | ★★★★ | ★★★ | Medium |
| **CogSci (ML track)** | ★★★ | ★ | ★★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★ | ★★★ | Medium |
| **CogSci (general BA)** | ★★ | ★ | ★★ | ★★ | ★★ | ★★ | Low |

### Notable rating decisions

- **Physics pure BS: judgment ★★★.** Most undergrads solve well-defined problems. The deep "which model applies to this unprecedented situation?" skill develops in research and grad school, not coursework. GRE/LSAT score data cited for physics reflects selection effects (smart students choose physics), not training effects (physics makes students smarter).
- **Applied Math: judgment ★★★ (corrected from initial ★★★★★).** Same logic as physics — applied math undergrads solve well-defined optimization, PDE, and numerical analysis problems. Model-selection in homework sets is not model-selection under genuine uncertainty with real stakes. The "choose the right mathematical framework" skill is real but develops through applied *work*, not coursework.
- **EE: judgment ★★★★.** Lab-heavy, project-heavy curriculum with tighter feedback loops between theory and practice. Design courses with physical constraints force real trade-offs (power vs. speed, cost vs. reliability, thermal vs. size). Not ★★★★★ because most undergrad projects are still well-scoped — the truly open-ended judgment develops on the job.
- **Comp. Biology: judgment ★★★★ (upgraded from ★★★).** Wet-lab + computational modeling forces constant evaluation: does this data make biological sense? Is the model artifact or biology? This is closer to the EE/CS judgment profile than to pure biology.
- **No single pure major achieves top marks on all axes.** EE comes closest because it inherently spans the physical-digital interface.

### Network and ecosystem effects — the hidden variable

Your major determines your professional network, which compounds independently of curriculum:

| Major cluster | Primary network/ecosystem | Relevance to post-AI value |
|---|---|---|
| CS | Tech industry, startups, VC ecosystem | Highest-paying, most AI-adjacent, strongest in transition period |
| EE | Hardware, semiconductors, defense, energy | Direct alignment with physical-world capital flows |
| MechE | Manufacturing, automotive, aerospace, robotics | Strong where atoms meet automation |
| ChemE | Process industry, energy, pharma | Aligned with energy transition and biotech |
| Physics | Academic, national labs, research institutes | Powerful for research careers; weak for industry without active networking |
| Biology/BioE | Biotech, pharma, clinical | Aligned with largest physical-world opportunity sector |

Network effects are hard to quantify but empirically large. Stanford CS → Silicon Valley is worth more than many curricula. The optimal major depends partly on the specific university's ecosystem. EE at a university with strong industry partnerships (Georgia Tech, Purdue, Michigan) delivers different network value than EE at a theory-focused department. **Evaluate the program, not just the subject.**

### Tiered summary

**Tier 1 — Strongest positioning:**

- **EE (modern: embedded ML, robotics, energy, signals/controls)** — Math, physical intuition, computation, and a concrete domain in one package. Sits at the physical-digital interface where the scarce complements of AI cluster (Section 2). Reasonable career entry. The strongest single-major choice for broadest resilience. **Honest caveat:** the "modern" qualifier is doing significant work. Traditional EE (analog design, power systems) is solid but less distinctively positioned. The median EE student's experience depends heavily on sub-field and program. At best, EE is the clear winner. At median, it's in a tight race with the other Tier 1 options. The recommendation is really: *EE, but choose your sub-field deliberately toward embedded systems, controls, signals/ML, or energy.*

- **Physics + serious CS supplement** — Deepest foundations. Powerful combination. But pure physics BS without computational depth faces real career-entry friction. **The honest case:** if grad school is planned, the career-entry penalty is deferred and the depth advantage compounds over decades. Physics + CS is plausibly the *highest-ceiling* path — the one that ages best over a 40-year career. Without the supplement or grad school pathway, drops to Tier 2.

- **CS (systems architecture, AI/ML internals, distributed systems)** — Understanding the technology reshaping everything is a massive transition-period advantage. Best professional network for the coming decade. **The critical split:** CS is diverging into execution-layer work (web dev, CRUD apps — actively commoditized) and systems-layer work (AI infrastructure, distributed systems, security — gaining value). This Tier 1 rating applies only to the latter. Students must consciously choose the systems/AI track. The risk: if career stays purely digital without domain depth, long-term positioning weakens as AI eats more of the digital stack.

**Tier 2 — Strong with tailwinds:**

- **MechE, ChemE** — Physical reality + serious math. Underrated. ChemE maps to energy/materials; MechE to robotics/manufacturing/infrastructure. Both provide the "scarce complement" profile naturally. ChemE has the edge for the energy transition; MechE for robotics and defense.
- **Computational biology / bioengineering** — Largest physical-world value sector (biotech) needs this combination. Narrower bet but strong 20+ year tailwind. Graduate degree likely needed for judgment-level work.
- **Applied Math + domain specialization** — Physics-tier formal reasoning, but a tool without a workbench until paired with a domain. Career outcomes are bimodal: excellent with applied domain depth (quant finance, computational science, ML research), mediocre without.

**Tier 3 — Viable, needs supplementation:**

- **Biology, CS (general/web), CogSci (ML track)** — Each has value but gaps: biology lacks formal/computational depth, general CS faces execution-layer commodity risk, CogSci ML has a narrow market.

**Tier 4:** CogSci general BA — soft science with computational garnish.

## 7. Game Theory: What Everyone Else Is Doing

If the crowd zigs, the contrarian position has independent market value — but only if the underlying fundamentals are strong. Current enrollment data makes the dynamics concrete:

**The data (fall 2025, National Student Clearinghouse):**
- CS undergrad enrollment **down 8.1%** at four-year institutions (~606,000 enrolled). CS graduate **down 14.0%** (~190,000). Fresh CS grads face 6.1–7.5% unemployment (Forbes, Apr 2026) — higher than biology or art history.
- Engineering enrollment **up 7.3%** — nearly mirroring the CS decline. Students are rotating from CS to engineering.
- Biological/biomedical sciences **up 3.7%** to ~642,500 enrolled.
- Health professions (nursing) at record enrollment (~268,000 BSN).
- Tech internship competition: **273 applications per posting** on Handshake, nearly 2× prior year.

**Decomposition:** ~40-50% of the CS decline is international student visa concerns, not domestic sentiment. Domestic CS flight is ~4-5%, not 8.1%. But the directional signal is real and entry-level competition is brutal.

**Game-theoretic implications:**

1. **CS panic is likely overdone.** Students enrolling now graduate ~2029-2030, when the current oversupply partially clears and the bootcamp pipeline has collapsed. CS + domain depth remains powerful. The contrarian CS bet has positive expected value — *if* you choose the systems/AI track, not generic web dev.
2. **Engineering is getting crowded fast.** The 7.3% surge means engineering entry-level competition intensifies 2-3 years out. The contrarian value of "zag toward engineering" is already eroding.
3. **Biology/biotech is quietly strong.** Growing enrollment but into a sector that's also expanding. Less zero-sum than CS or engineering.
4. **The real edge isn't picking the unpopular major** — it's assembling the complete profile (Section 5) in *any* rigorous field, which most peers won't. A MechE with serious CS depth and research experience stands out more than another CS grad in 2029.

**Counter-argument to pure contrarianism:** CS still has the highest median starting salaries and the strongest industry network. Being contrarian for its own sake is a cognitive bias, not a strategy. The contrarian edge only works if the underlying fundamentals are strong — which they are for all Tier 1-2 options. Don't pick EE over CS because CS is popular. Pick the field where you'll go deepest and supplement most aggressively.

## 8. What Could Break This Analysis

1. **Fast AGI (5–10 yr)** commoditizes all human cognitive work → no training provides durable advantage. *Low probability but non-zero. Hedge: physical-world and accountability advantages fall last.*

2. **Compressed transition (<5 yr)** → everyone must adapt fast regardless of training; formal foundations reassert because deeper reasoning enables faster adaptation. *Strengthens the math/foundations ingredients.*

3. **New work categories emerge** that don't map to current disciplines → optimizing for any existing domain is wrong; learning velocity is the right target. *High probability. The profile with formal depth + breadth is the best hedge.*

4. **Credentials become irrelevant** → major choice is purely signaling/network, not training. *Strengthens "profile not pedigree" and elevates network effects (Section 6).*

5. **Physical-world automation advances fast** (humanoid robotics, autonomous manufacturing) → the atoms-are-harder moat narrows. *Moderate probability for routine physical work within 10-15 years. Low for novel unstructured situations. Even optimistic humanoid robotics projections put widespread deployment at 2035+. This narrows the moat; it doesn't close it. And it creates new demand for people who design, deploy, and maintain the physical automation.*

6. **AI tutoring radically improves self-teaching** → the "physics/math is hard to self-teach" argument weakens. Major choice shifts from "what's hardest to learn alone" to "what provides the best network, signaling, and lab access." *Already happening incrementally. Could substantially change the self-teach asymmetry within 10 years.*

7. **Credential inflation** → master's/PhD becomes the new bachelor's for judgment-level work. Undergraduate major choice becomes less decisive; the grad-school pipeline matters more. *Already true in biotech and energy. Spreading to semiconductors and AI research. If this accelerates, it strengthens the physics path (best grad school preparation) and weakens terminal-BS engineering paths.*

8. **Industrial policy distorts sector demand** → massive government subsidies (CHIPS Act, IRA, defense spending) create sector-specific booms that override generic frameworks. *Already happening. CHIPS Act alone is driving semiconductor hiring surges. IRA is driving clean energy investment. These are large-scale demand signals that favor specific majors (EE, ChemE, materials science) over generic "best preparation." Could shift the optimal bet toward whichever sector gets the most capital — but political winds shift, so don't overfit to current policy.*

## The Answer

**The post-AI era rewards a profile, not a major.** The profile:

- Formal reasoning depth that compounds over decades
- Physical-world fluency where AI is weakest and capital is flowing
- Serious computational fluency — AI as amplifier, not competitor
- One domain deep enough for real judgment (weighted toward structural moats: accountability and taste over capability moats that erode)
- Learning velocity and breadth for the categories that don't exist yet
- Early enough career entry to start compounding judgment through real stakes

**Two paths assemble it:** Major in formal + physical foundations (physics, EE, MechE, ChemE) and invest seriously in computational depth. Or major in deep CS and invest seriously in physical-world domain exposure. Both work. Neither works halfway.

**Sub-field choice within a major often matters more than the major itself.** EE (embedded ML + robotics) ≠ EE (power systems). CS (distributed systems + AI infrastructure) ≠ CS (web development). Physics (experimental condensed matter) ≠ physics (string theory). Choose your courses, labs, and research experiences deliberately. A deliberate MechE beats an aimless EE.

**The worst move is single-axis optimization** — pure theory without computation, pure coding without physical intuition, pure breadth without depth.

**If forced to name one major:** EE (modern focus). It sits on the Pareto frontier of formal depth, physical-world fluency, computational skill, and career entry — the only Tier 1 major that doesn't require significant supplementation to cover its gaps. But this is a narrow margin over Physics+CS (for grad-school-bound students) and CS-systems/AI (for transition-period leverage). The honest answer is that these three are close calls with different risk profiles:

| Path | Best for | Primary risk |
|---|---|---|
| **EE (modern)** | Broadest resilience, physical-digital interface | "Modern" sub-field required; median EE may be less distinctive |
| **Physics + CS** | Deepest foundations, grad school pipeline, 40-year view | Career-entry friction without grad school or active supplementation |
| **CS (systems/AI)** | Transition period leverage, tech network, highest short-term earnings | Long-term commodity risk if purely digital; requires conscious sub-field choice |

**Finally: your objective function matters.** This analysis optimizes for *career resilience across AI-disruption scenarios*. If you're optimizing for:
- **Maximum impact on climate** → ChemE or EE (energy focus)
- **Maximum impact on health** → CompBio or bioengineering
- **Maximum short-term earnings** → CS at an elite program
- **Maximum intellectual depth** → physics → PhD
- **Maximum optionality with minimum planning** → EE

The profile framework holds regardless. The weights on each ingredient shift with your goals.
