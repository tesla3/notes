## HN Thread Distillation: "Amazon accused of widespread scheme to inflate prices across the economy"

**Source:** [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47145907) (219 pts, 66 comments) · [Article](https://www.thebignewsletter.com/p/amazon-busted-for-widespread-price) (Matt Stoller, BIG newsletter) · [CA AG press release](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-bonta-exposes-amazon-price-fixing-scheme-driving-costs)

**Article summary:** California AG Rob Bonta filed for a preliminary injunction against Amazon, alleging a hub-and-spoke price-fixing scheme: Amazon pressures vendors to raise prices at competitors (or pull products entirely), using its 200M-member Prime monopoly as leverage. Bonta is asking for emergency relief *now*, a year before the January 2027 trial — a move that signals he believes his evidence is overwhelming. Stoller connects this to the Pepsi-Walmart price-fixing case and the FTC's "Project Nessie" revelations, arguing consolidation-driven price-fixing is a structural cause of inflation.

### Dominant Sentiment: Cynical resignation, not outrage

The thread is notable for how little surprise anyone expresses. The dominant mood is "we've known this for years, nothing will happen." This tells you something about the normalization of monopoly abuse — when the response to a major antitrust injunction is a collective shrug, the enforcement system has already lost credibility regardless of the legal merits.

### Key Insights

**1. The insider reframe is the most revealing comment — and the most dangerous**

`binarysolo`, an Amazon seller/distributor for over a decade, offers the most detailed comment in the thread and essentially argues the price inflation is an *emergent property* of two reasonable systems colliding: Amazon Search wants lowest prices on Amazon, Amazon Vendor Central wants margins. "Designing a system to incentivize sellers to have their lowest prices on Amazon... I'm not sure if calling it a 'widespread scheme to inflate prices' is the fairest thing."

This is the most useful comment because it's also exactly how Amazon would frame its defense — and multiple commenters catch this immediately. `dataflow` delivers the sharpest rebuttal: "You might as well hand someone a toy and say 'Think of this as a toy gun. But this is where it gets a bit more complicated: 40% of these have a trigger that shoots bullets.' Whom are you kidding?" The framing of "two separate well-intentioned systems that happen to produce price inflation" is precisely the legal and rhetorical strategy a hub-and-spoke defendant uses. The AG's complaint specifically alleges *explicit agreements to increase retail prices* — emails, documented communications — not just emergent algorithmic effects. binarysolo's "emergent behavior" frame and the AG's evidence of deliberate vendor arm-twisting describe fundamentally different things.

**2. The platform bootstrapping paradox undermines the simple villain narrative**

`jadenPete` raises a genuinely underexplored point: any two-sided marketplace faces the problem of users treating it as a free index and then transacting off-platform. "If left unchecked, this could make running the platform financially unviable." This is real — it was a fatal problem for many startups. `BrenBarn`'s dismissive response ("Sounds great to me!") gets upvotes but dodges the actual economics. The uncomfortable truth is that Amazon's price-parity rules exist on a *continuum* with legitimate platform economics. The line between "protecting your marketplace investment" and "using monopoly power to inflate economy-wide prices" is drawn by market share, not by the mechanism itself. Amazon crossed that line years ago, but the thread doesn't wrestle with where the line actually is.

**3. Manufacturers already route around price controls — the coupon arbitrage**

`crazygringo` points out that manufacturers circumvent Amazon's price-matching rules by listing at parity but offering perpetual "sitewide" 20-25% coupons on their own sites. "So I don't know how much this is really raising actual prices in the end." This is an important empirical question the thread doesn't resolve. If the workaround is widespread, the harm to *sophisticated* consumers may be modest — but it creates a regressive information tax where unsophisticated buyers (who don't know to check manufacturer sites) pay inflated Amazon prices while savvy shoppers find the coupon. The scheme's real damage may be less about aggregate price levels and more about who bears the cost.

**4. The enforcement credibility crisis is the real story**

`paxys`: "Walmart and Pepsi engaged in a blatant decade-long price fixing scheme... the case was thrown out the minute Trump took office." `jimbokun`: "the length of time required to get a decision in court is the single biggest impediment to justice being served." `chuckadams`: "Amazon better watch their step or they might get fined a single-digit percentage of the profits they made off this scheme." The thread is far more animated about whether enforcement is even *possible* than about what Amazon actually did. This is the meta-story Stoller's article gestures at but the thread articulates more bluntly: the legal system's multi-year timeline is itself a form of regulatory capture. A preliminary injunction is significant precisely because it short-circuits this — but the thread is too cynical to notice that Bonta is trying the exact remedy they're lamenting the absence of.

**5. Amazon's quality collapse is doing more enforcement work than the AG**

`aschla`'s cancellation story is a catalog of enshittification: returned items resold as new, closed return centers, 3-4 day shipping delays (on Prime), half-sponsored listings, XIJGNU-brand garbage, LLM-generated fake reviews. `SoftTalker`: "I saw through the Amazon Prime scam about four years ago." `bitmasher9`: "A product being on a shelf at a Walmart is a better indicator of quality than anything you could post on an Amazon listing." The irony is that Amazon's quality degradation may be a more powerful check on its market power than any legal action. When the platform that justified monopoly rents through convenience stops being convenient, the lock-in weakens. The thread's anecdotal evidence suggests Amazon is eroding its own moat faster than regulators can breach it.

**6. The $3000/household figure is more noise than signal**

An extended subthread between `jimbokun`, `twoodfin`, `raw_anon_1111`, and others debates whether $426B in North American revenue ÷ US households = $3000 is a meaningful statistic. `twoodfin` correctly notes this includes Whole Foods and isn't shocking at 2% of average household income. `raw_anon_1111` objects that mean vs. median matters. This is the thread's most tedious tangent — a classic HN pattern where statistical pedantry displaces substantive discussion. The actual number doesn't matter for the antitrust case; what matters is Amazon's *market share* in product categories where it can exercise pricing power, which nobody discusses.

### What the Thread Misses

- **The preliminary injunction is the news, not the allegations.** An AG asking a court to enjoin a company *before trial* is extraordinarily aggressive. It signals the evidence is strong enough that Bonta expects to win the "likelihood of success on the merits" standard. The thread treats this as another in a series of complaints rather than a qualitatively different legal move.
- **The Pepsi-Walmart parallel.** Stoller's most important claim is that hub-and-spoke price-fixing through dominant platforms is a *structural pattern* — not an Amazon-specific story. If Amazon, Walmart, and their respective vendor networks are all engaged in similar schemes, the aggregate inflation impact is far larger than any single case suggests. Nobody in the thread connects these dots.
- **AWS cross-subsidy dynamics.** Amazon can afford to play pricing games in retail because AWS prints money. The thread doesn't discuss whether AWS profits subsidize the predatory pricing/margin-squeezing cycle that created the monopoly position now being used to *raise* prices. This is the classic two-stage monopoly play: subsidize below-cost to win, then extract once dominant.
- **Evidence destruction is the buried lede.** The article mentions that the FTC alleges Bezos and senior executives used Signal with 10-second auto-delete while under legal hold — potential spoliation that could be more consequential than the pricing claims themselves (consciousness of guilt, adverse inference instructions at trial). The thread completely ignores this.
- **International implications.** Amazon operates globally. If the CA injunction succeeds, does it create precedent or pressure for EU/UK regulators who are already investigating Amazon? Zero discussion.

### Verdict

The thread correctly identifies that Amazon's price inflation is real and enforcement is broken, but misses that this particular legal action — a preliminary injunction — is designed to fix exactly the timeline problem everyone is complaining about. The deeper dynamic nobody names: Amazon has entered the **extraction phase** of the classic platform lifecycle (subsidize → dominate → extract), and the quality collapse reported by multiple commenters is the visible symptom. The legal case is about pricing, but the real story is that Amazon's entire value proposition to consumers is degrading simultaneously across price, quality, speed, and trust — and the company is betting that Prime's 200M-member inertia will hold long enough for the legal system to run out the clock. Bonta's injunction is an attempt to call that bluff.
