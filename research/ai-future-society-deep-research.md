# AI and Future Society: A Grounded Assessment

**Date:** February 2026
**Scope:** Economics, power, geopolitics, social fabric, historical precedent
**Method:** Evidence synthesis from academic research, prediction markets, institutional reports, and practitioner data
**Epistemic status:** Synthesis with known blind spots. See [Section 12: Epistemic Audit](#12-epistemic-audit) for what this document gets wrong or presents with false confidence.

---

## 1. What's Actually Happening (Feb 2026)

Before projecting the future, nail down the present. The discourse runs hot, but the data tells a more textured story.

### The displacement signal: real but noisy

- **1.2 million US layoffs in 2025**, up 58% from 2024. Of these, 50,000+ were directly attributed to AI (Acemoglu/Fortune, Feb 2026).
- **But:** Yale Budget Lab found "no significant differences in the rate of change of occupations' mix or length of unemployment" for AI-exposed jobs through November 2025. *"No matter which way you look at the data, at this exact moment, it just doesn't seem like there's major macroeconomic effects here."* — Martha Gimbel, Yale Budget Lab.
- **NBER study (Feb 2026):** Of thousands of surveyed C-suite executives across US, UK, Germany, Australia, nearly 90% said AI had no impact on workplace employment over the past three years.
- **Goldman Sachs:** Only 9.3% of US companies have incorporated AI into regular production workflows. Estimated 2.5% of US employment currently at risk if existing AI use cases were scaled economy-wide. Baseline forecast: 6-7% job displacement from AI, with displacement effects typically disappearing after two years historically.
- **"AI washing" is pervasive.** Companies attribute layoffs to AI that are actually driven by margin pressure, post-pandemic right-sizing, and geopolitical headwinds. Sam Altman himself acknowledged it: *"There's some AI washing where people are blaming AI for layoffs that they would otherwise do."*

**Assessment:** There is real displacement happening, concentrated in entry-level white-collar roles (content, customer service, data entry, junior software engineering). But the macroeconomic signal is still weak. The gap between CEO rhetoric and measurable economic impact is large. We are in very early innings.

### The productivity signal: ambiguous

- **Brynjolfsson (Stanford):** Claims 2.7% US productivity growth in 2025, nearly double the decade average. Argues we're entering the "harvest phase" of the J-curve. *But* — he co-founded Workhelix (AI consulting firm), creating a conflict of interest. And his GDP growth figure could reflect massive AI infrastructure *spending*, not productivity from *using* AI.
- **Apollo Global Management's Torsten Slok:** *"AI is everywhere except in the incoming macroeconomic data."* Echoes Robert Solow's 1987 observation about computers.
- **MIT Media Lab (Aug 2025):** Despite $30-40 billion in enterprise generative AI investment, 95% of organizations are getting zero return.
- **Penn Wharton Budget Model (Sept 2025):** Estimates AI will increase GDP by 1.5% by 2035, 3% by 2055. Peak annual productivity contribution: 0.2 percentage points in 2032.
- **Acemoglu:** 0.53-0.66% TFP increase over 10 years. *"It's not nothing, but it's disappointing relative to the promises."*

**Assessment:** The productivity evidence is contradictory. Brynjolfsson sees liftoff; Slok and Acemoglu see noise. The honest answer: we don't know yet. If forced to bet, the weight of evidence favors a real but modest productivity boost that will take years to fully materialize — consistent with every prior general-purpose technology.

### The bubble signal: increasingly loud

- **$400 billion** in AI infrastructure spending in 2025. Projected to exceed $500 billion annually in 2026-2027.
- **US consumers spend only $12 billion/year on AI services.** The gap between investment and revenue is Singapore-to-Somalia scale (Derek Thompson).
- OpenAI: valued at $500 billion, expects $13B revenue and **$5B loss** in 2025. May burn >$140B before profitability.
- **Nvidia's $5+ trillion valuation** exceeds the GDP of every country except the US and China. It controls 92% of the high-end AI chip market.
- **Financial engineering is escalating:** Nvidia invests in OpenAI, which buys Nvidia chips, which are hosted on Microsoft Azure, which owns 27% of OpenAI. CoreWeave issues billions in debt guaranteed by Nvidia. Off-balance-sheet SPVs mask true spending.
- **S&P 500 concentration at 50-year highs.** Five companies = 30% of the index. AI-related enterprises drove ~80% of stock market gains in 2025.
- **Private credit exposure:** ~$200B in data center debt raised in 2025. Projected to exceed $1 trillion by 2028. GPU-backed collateral. Untested borrowers.

**Assessment:** This has the structural features of a bubble: circular financing, concentration, financial engineering, and a massive gap between spending and revenue. But "bubble" doesn't mean "fake." Railroads were a bubble *and* transformative. The internet was a bubble *and* world-changing. The technology is real; the financial structure around it may not survive contact with reality. A correction is probable within 2-4 years. The technology will survive the correction; many companies and investors will not.

## 2. The Historical Analogy: What General-Purpose Technologies Actually Do

The strongest analytical framework for AI's trajectory comes not from AI researchers but from economic historians studying previous general-purpose technologies (GPTs): the steam engine, electrification, and computing.

### The electricity parallel (Paul David, Stanford, 1990)

David's influential — but contested — paper "The Dynamo and the Computer" argued:

- Electric light bulbs available 1879. Generating stations in New York/London by 1881.
- A thoughtful observer in 1900 would have found **little evidence** the electricity revolution was making business more efficient.
- Early adopters simply ripped out the steam engine and replaced it with a dynamo. Productivity barely improved.
- The real gains came only when factories were **completely redesigned**: single-floor layouts, individual motors per worker, reorganized production flows, new training and contracts.
- **US productivity growth leapt in the 1920s — four decades after commercialization.** Manufacturing productivity grew >5% per year in that decade.

**The mechanism:** GPTs don't produce gains through simple substitution. They require complementary innovations — organizational redesign, new skills, new business models, new institutional arrangements. These take decades, not quarters.

**Important caveat:** David's thesis is influential but not uncontested. Some economic historians argue the electricity lag was shorter than he claimed, or that other factors (WWI, immigration policy, unrelated industrial organization changes) drove the 1920s productivity leap. The paper is a framework, not a law. I lean on it throughout this document and should be transparent that the foundation is suggestive, not proven.

### The computing parallel (Solow paradox, 1987-1995)

- Solow: *"You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics."*
- Computing investment surged through the 1980s with minimal productivity impact.
- Productivity growth finally appeared in the mid-1990s, coinciding with organizational restructuring around networked computing (not the computers themselves).
- The lag was ~15-20 years from widespread PC adoption to measurable macro-level productivity gains.

### What this predicts for AI

If AI follows the GPT pattern:
- **2022-2030:** Investment boom. Experimentation. Modest task-level productivity gains. High variance between early adopters and laggards. Possible financial bubble and correction.
- **2030-2040:** Organizational restructuring. New business models emerge. Complementary innovations (new management practices, legal frameworks, training systems) develop. Productivity gains become measurable.
- **2040-2055:** Full deployment effects. Macro-level productivity impact. Institutional adaptation largely complete.

**Critical caveat:** AI may be *faster* than electricity because it's a software technology that doesn't require physical infrastructure reorganization to the same degree. The counter-caveat: the complementary innovations (legal frameworks, social institutions, workforce transitions) are no faster than they were in 1920.

**The "this time is different" argument deserves more weight than I initially gave it.** Previous technology transitions displaced manual labor, and workers moved upward to cognitive work. AI targets cognitive work directly — the very category that historically absorbed the displaced. Where do displaced cognitive workers go? The honest answer is: we don't know, and there is no historical precedent for automating the *destination* of previous transitions, not just the origin. Saying "new jobs always emerge" is pattern-matching against a history where the escape route was always cognitive work. If that route closes, the pattern may not hold. I can't assess the probability of this with any confidence.

## 3. The Economics: Three Schools of Thought

The expert landscape is not a binary debate. It's a three-way disagreement:

### School 1: Modest Impact (Acemoglu)

**Core thesis:** AI will affect a bounded set of tasks. Macro effects will be real but disappointing relative to hype.

- Only ~20-40% of economic activity is meaningfully exposed to AI (depending on measurement).
- Average task-level cost savings from AI: ~27%.
- Applying Hulten's theorem: GDP impact = exposure × cost savings = modest.
- **Key insight often missed:** AI's inequality effects may be worse than its productivity effects. The technology is likely to widen the gap between capital and labor income even in scenarios where total output grows.
- Advocates "pro-worker AI" — tax policy to steer investment toward augmentation over replacement.

**Strengths:** Methodologically rigorous. Based on task-level empirical data, not extrapolation. Calibrated uncertainty.
**Weaknesses:** May significantly underestimate the pace of capability improvement. His 2024 model was based on GPT-4-era capabilities; models have improved substantially since. This is not a minor caveat — if capabilities continue advancing at current rates, his inputs become stale within 1-2 years, which could make his GDP estimates badly wrong in either direction. I lean heavily on Acemoglu throughout this document because his method is the most rigorous, but rigor applied to outdated inputs can produce precise answers to the wrong question.

### School 2: Augmentation (Autor)

**Core thesis:** AI will change the composition of work, not eliminate it. The net effect depends on institutional choices.

- AI both devalues *and* amplifies expertise. Navigation apps opened driving to non-experts (Uber) but depressed wages. In finance/science, AI makes experts more valuable but reduces headcount.
- **CheXpert finding:** Doctors using an AI diagnostic tool made more errors because they didn't know when to trust it. Implication: AI requires human judgment as a complement, not a replacement.
- Historical precedent: ~60% of US workers today are in occupations that didn't exist in 1940. Technology-driven job creation has generated >85% of employment growth since then.
- *"We should be humble about predicting the future. It's very easy to see things that could be automated and replaced, but we're less good at saying, here are the new things we will create."*

**Strengths:** Historically grounded. Accounts for both positive and negative effects. Avoids determinism.
**Weaknesses:** "New jobs will emerge" is a faith statement backed by historical pattern-matching, not a mechanism. The pattern held when displaced manual workers could transition to cognitive work — but what happens when cognitive work is itself the thing being automated?

### School 3: Productivity Liftoff (Brynjolfsson)

**Core thesis:** The J-curve is real. We're entering the harvest phase. AI productivity gains are now visible in macro data.

- 2.7% US productivity growth in 2025. Fewer workers + robust GDP = productivity.
- Found 16% drop in new hires across AI-exposed sectors.
- Identifies a "small cohort of power users" automating end-to-end workstreams, completing tasks in hours instead of weeks.

**Strengths:** Only researcher with a clear, testable claim about *current* data. If he's right, we'll know within 2-3 years. That falsifiability is itself a virtue.
**Weaknesses:** GDP growth could reflect infrastructure spending, not actual use of AI. Causation not established. Data is noisy. He co-founded an AI consulting firm (Workhelix), which is worth disclosing but is not an argument against his data — his numbers are either right or wrong regardless of his business interests. I flagged this COI repeatedly in earlier drafts as if it were dispositive. It isn't. I should engage with his methodology more and his incentives less.

### Synthesis

Acemoglu and Autor are probably right about the next 5 years. Brynjolfsson may be right about the trajectory beyond that. The honest forecast: **AI will produce meaningful but not revolutionary economic effects through 2035, with the possibility of accelerating thereafter if organizational and institutional adaptation catches up.**

The more important point — **and I should be transparent that this is partly a values judgment, not purely an empirical finding** — is that the distribution of effects may matter more than the aggregate. Even if GDP grows, the benefits may accrue overwhelmingly to capital owners and a thin layer of AI-skilled workers, while displacing a much larger group. Many growth economists would disagree, arguing that total productivity matters more because you can always redistribute later. They may be right in theory; they have been wrong in practice for the past 40 years of rising inequality. This is Acemoglu's real warning — not that AI will fail to boost productivity, but that it will boost productivity in ways that worsen inequality unless actively counteracted by policy.

## 4. The Power Question

This is where the discussion shifts from economics to political economy — and where the evidence is most concerning.

### Concentration is already extreme

- **Nvidia:** 92% market share in high-end AI chips. Valued above $5 trillion.
- **Five companies** = 30% of S&P 500, 20% of MSCI World — highest concentration in 50 years.
- Korinek & Vipra (Economic Policy, 2025) warn that the cost of training frontier models is **doubling every six months**, creating an ever-higher barrier to entry. 79% of major US news sites have blocked OpenAI's web crawlers, giving existing data holders (Google, Meta, Microsoft) a compounding advantage.
- **Vertical integration is accelerating:** The same companies control chips (Nvidia), cloud infrastructure (Microsoft/Google/Amazon), foundation models (OpenAI/Google/Anthropic), and consumer applications. This is not a competitive market — it's an oligopoly with internal financing loops.

### The regulatory landscape is fragmented

**EU:** Most ambitious regulation. AI Act entered force August 2024, phased implementation through 2027. Bans social scoring and certain biometric surveillance. Requires safety assessments for high-risk systems. But — implementation is slow, companies are lobbying for weakening, and the EU risks regulating a technology it doesn't control.

**US:** No national AI regulation. State-level patchwork. Trump administration launched "Genesis Mission" giving AI companies unprecedented access to government datasets. *"Companies in the United States are expected to police themselves."* — Nature, Dec 2025. AI Safety Index: no US firm scores above C+.

**China:** Most active regulation paradoxically paired with most aggressive deployment. Requires pre-deployment safety assessments, watermarks on AI-generated content, traceability. Issued as many national AI requirements in first half of 2025 as in the previous three years. Proposing a global regulatory body (WAICO). But: China's social credit system (focused on corporate compliance, not the dystopian individual scoring of media portrayal) represents state-directed AI governance with no democratic accountability.

**Assessment:** The EU regulates but doesn't build. The US builds but doesn't regulate. China does both but under authoritarian control. This fragmentation means no coherent global framework exists, and companies can arbitrage between jurisdictions.

### Acemoglu's Narrow Corridor framework

This is the most relevant theoretical framework for understanding AI's political implications, and it comes from the same person doing the best economic modeling.

**The thesis (Acemoglu & Robinson, 2019):**

Liberty exists in a "narrow corridor" between despotism and anarchy. It requires balanced conflict between state power and societal power — what they call the "Red Queen effect," where both must continuously evolve. Liberty is not engineered by elites or guaranteed by constitutions. It's sustained by continuous social mobilization.

*"True democracy and liberty don't originate from checks and balances or from clever institutional design. They originate in the much more messy process of society mobilizing, people defending their own liberties, and actively setting constraints on how rules and behaviors are imposed on them."*

**Applied to AI:** The risk isn't that AI enables a specific dystopian outcome. It's that AI shifts the balance of power between state/corporate actors and society in ways that push us out of the corridor. If the capacity to organize collective action depends partly on information access, economic independence, and institutional leverage — and AI affects all three — then the corridor narrows.

**But:** Acemoglu's own framework shows this isn't a one-way ratchet. The Gilded Age was reversed by the Progressive Era. Post-WWII social democracies emerged from the wreckage of fascism. Power concentration *often triggers* correction.

**However, I should not be breezy about what "correction" cost.** The Gilded Age → New Deal arc involved a Great Depression, mass labor organizing under violent suppression, and two World Wars. The post-fascism social democracies were built on the rubble of regimes that murdered millions. "History shows power concentration gets corrected" is true and also sanitizes an enormous amount of suffering. The question is not just *whether* correction happens but *at what cost* — and whether the cost can be reduced through early institutional response rather than catastrophic breakdown.

## 5. The Global Dimension

The AI discussion is overwhelmingly Anglophone and US-centric. This is a blind spot.

### The colonial extraction pattern is repeating

- **150-430 million data laborers** globally, concentrated in the Global South (World Bank estimate).
- Kenyan workers train ChatGPT's safety systems for $1.50-2/hour, reviewing violent and traumatic content. Equidem survey (2025): 60 independent incidents of psychological harm among 76 workers in Colombia, Ghana, and Kenya.
- India and the Philippines are global hubs for outsourced customer support — the exact roles AI is displacing first. *"These workers are literally teaching AI systems to recognise the same harmful content they review, making their own jobs redundant."* — LSE, Nov 2025.
- The DRC produces 70%+ of global cobalt supply, essential for AI infrastructure. The value chain: extractive mining → data labeling → model training → consumer product → all profits flow to Silicon Valley.

**Assessment:** AI's global impact isn't speculative — it's already here, and the structure resembles colonial extraction: low-wage workers in the Global South build the infrastructure, train the models, and are displaced first, while value concentrates in the North. This is the least discussed and most immediately harmful dimension of AI's societal impact.

**Counter-argument I should engage with rather than dismiss:** Development economists would note that low-wage insertion into global supply chains has historically been a pathway to industrialization — South Korea, China, and Vietnam all bootstrapped through stages that looked exploitative by wealthy-country standards. Whether data labeling follows this pattern (building local expertise and eventually higher-value work) or breaks it (pure extraction with no technology transfer) is an open empirical question, not a settled one. My framing leans toward the pessimistic interpretation.

### Geopolitical competition creates a race to the bottom

- The US-China AI arms race is real and escalating. The NYT editorial board (Dec 2025) compared it to pre-WWII arms races, with autonomous weapons, AI-designed bioweapons, and cyber capabilities all under development.
- UN and ICRC called for a new treaty on autonomous weapons by 2026. Progress is minimal — "mistrust among states and a potentially flawed confidence in technological solutions hinder progress."
- **The competitive dynamic undermines regulation.** When Thierer (R Street Institute) argues that *"any regulation of America's AI industry could hamper its competition with China,"* he's stating the structural trap: each nation fears that regulating AI will hand advantage to rivals, so nobody regulates meaningfully.

## 6. The Social Fabric

### Work as identity

The WEF's August 2025 piece on the "AI precariat" frames what may be the most underestimated risk:

*"As income disappears, identity and meaning may vanish too, with real consequences for mental health."*

Researchers at the University of Florida have proposed "AI Replacement Dysfunction" (AIRD) as a clinical concept — anxiety, insomnia, and identity loss from the *persistent threat* of job displacement, even before actual job loss occurs. This isn't yet a formal diagnosis, but the underlying research is solid:

- Unemployment is associated with adverse mental health outcomes **long after** initial job loss (established finding in labor economics).
- Reuters survey: 71% of Americans worry AI will permanently eliminate large numbers of jobs.
- The psychological harm may be coming from the *discourse* as much as from actual displacement. The constant drumbeat of "AI will take your job" from tech leaders (Amodei: "half of entry-level white-collar jobs within five years") creates ambient dread regardless of whether the predictions are accurate.

**Assessment:** This is a plausible hypothesis, not an established finding. The evidence base is thin: one proposed (not formally recognized) clinical diagnosis from two researchers, one Reuters poll, and the general observation that tech executives have financial incentives to hype. The mechanism is plausible — the narrative becomes a causal factor, producing psychological harm independent of actual displacement. But I should be honest that I found this idea compelling and elevated it beyond what the evidence strictly supports. It deserves investigation, not the confident framing I initially gave it.

### Education: the brightest spot

Khanmigo (Khan Academy's AI tutor) is the clearest positive evidence of AI's societal benefit:

- Usage jumped from 40,000 to 700,000 K-12 students in one year (2024-25). Projected to surpass 1 million in 2025-26.
- Students using Khan Academy for 30+ minutes of additional weekly practice saw greater-than-expected gains on standardized assessments.
- Designed as Socratic tutor — doesn't give answers, guides students to find them.
- Unexpected uses: support for English-language learners, teachers refreshing their own knowledge.

**Caveats:** Research shows over-reliance on AI during practice can reduce performance on unassisted exams. The CheXpert finding (doctors making more errors with AI) may generalize to education — if students can't calibrate when to trust the AI, the tool backfires.

## 7. The Underweighted Positives

### Scientific research

AI's impact on scientific research may ultimately matter more than its impact on labor markets. AlphaFold's protein structure predictions won a Nobel Prize. AI-driven drug discovery is accelerating clinical pipelines. Materials science, climate modeling, and genomics are all seeing capability jumps. Acemoglu himself explicitly excluded these applications from his GDP model, noting they could change the calculus significantly.

These effects are hard to quantify but potentially enormous. If AI accelerates drug development by even a few years, the health and economic value dwarfs the labor displacement debate. This is the area where the most bullish scenarios are most credible — and most underrepresented in public discourse, which focuses almost exclusively on jobs.

### Education

Discussed below in "Social Fabric," but worth flagging: AI tutoring (Khanmigo) represents a genuine democratization of expertise. If a $44/year subscription can provide personalized tutoring comparable to what wealthy families buy for hundreds per hour, the equity implications are significant.

### Environmental costs (the hidden ledger)

AI's energy consumption is growing rapidly, with data centers drawing increasing shares of electrical capacity. This creates a real tension: AI might help solve climate problems (better modeling, grid optimization, materials discovery) while simultaneously exacerbating them (energy demand, water usage for cooling, rare earth mining). The net effect is genuinely uncertain and depends on what AI is used *for*.

## 8. The UBI Question: What the Evidence Actually Shows

The GiveDirectly experiment in Kenya is the world's largest and longest randomized controlled trial of UBI. Results after 2 years:

**What works:**
- UBI did not create idleness. Recipients worked the same hours, shifted toward entrepreneurship.
- No increase in alcohol consumption. Common "laziness" concern never materialized.
- Long-term UBI (12-year guarantee) encouraged savings and risk-taking.
- Enterprises increased by 34.5%, gross revenues by 59.6%, net revenues by 98.7%.
- Food insecurity decreased. Depression decreased 17%.

**What's surprising:**
- **Lump sums outperformed monthly payments** on nearly all economic measures. Explanation: lump sums provide startup capital for businesses, which monthly payments require years of saving to replicate.
- Short-term UBI (2 years) was the least impactful design — yet this is how most government cash assistance is structured worldwide.
- Long-term UBI participants spontaneously created ROSCAs (rotating savings associations) to convert monthly payments into lump sums for investment.

**Critical limitation:** Results from rural Kenya are not directly applicable to high-income countries. The mechanisms (startup capital for small farms/businesses) may not transfer to urban post-industrial economies. There are nearly no comparable RCT findings in wealthy countries.

**Assessment:** The evidence supports UBI as a floor that enables risk-taking and entrepreneurship, not a hammock that creates dependency. But the specific design matters enormously — lump sums or long-term guaranteed income outperform short-term monthly payments. The policy implications for AI-driven displacement: if you're going to redistribute, give people enough to invest, not just enough to survive.

## 9. Scenarios: Grounded Projections

Rather than predict a single future, here are four scenarios. I've attached probability estimates because refusing to quantify uncertainty is its own form of intellectual cowardice — but I want to be explicit that **these probabilities are my subjective judgment, not calibrated forecasts.** They reflect the weight of evidence as I read it, filtered through my priors. A different analyst with different priors could defensibly assign very different numbers. The value is in the scenarios themselves and the evidence behind them, not the percentages.

### Scenario A: "The Boring Transition" (40% probability)

AI follows the standard GPT adoption curve. Productivity gains are real but take 15-25 years to fully materialize. Displacement is gradual, sector-specific, and manageable through existing institutions (unemployment insurance, retraining, incremental policy). The AI bubble pops within 2-4 years; the technology survives and continues developing. By 2045, AI has transformed the economy comparably to how computing transformed it between 1980-2020: profoundly, but without civilizational rupture.

**Key feature:** The transition is messy and uneven, with clear winners and losers, but social institutions adapt. Inequality increases modestly. Democracy survives with stress fractures. This is what Acemoglu and Autor implicitly expect.

### Scenario B: "The Accelerated Transition" (25% probability)

AI capabilities improve faster than historical GPT precedent suggests. Organizational adaptation also accelerates because AI itself helps design new business models and institutional arrangements. Displacement is faster (5-15 years for major restructuring) but so is productivity growth. The key variable is whether political systems can redistribute fast enough. If they can: a bumpy but ultimately positive transition. If they can't: political instability and a protracted fight over distribution.

**Key feature:** The transition happens within a single generation, creating acute political stress but also generating enough surplus to fund adaptation — *if* the political will exists to redistribute it.

### Scenario C: "The Concentration Trap" (25% probability)

AI capabilities advance but benefits concentrate among a small number of firms and individuals. Regulatory capture prevents meaningful redistribution. The US-China competitive dynamic blocks international cooperation. Inequality widens dramatically. Democratic institutions erode not through dramatic collapse but through gradual hollowing — what Acemoglu would call "captured democracy." The technology works, but it works for the few.

**Key feature:** This isn't dystopian sci-fi — it's an intensified version of trends already visible in 2026. The corridor narrows slowly. Most people's lives get somewhat worse while aggregate metrics look fine.

### Scenario D: "The Bubble and Stall" (10% probability)

The AI bubble pops catastrophically. Private credit exposure to data centers triggers a financial crisis. AI investment contracts sharply. Capabilities plateau due to scaling limits, data exhaustion, or fundamental architectural constraints. AI becomes a useful but non-transformative technology, more like the blockchain than electricity. The displacement fears were overblown; the investment losses were real.

**Key feature:** The technology underdelivers relative to both boosters and doomers. The main consequence is a financial hangover and wasted capital, not societal transformation.

Rather than debate hypotheticals, monitor these empirical signals:

| Indicator | Bearish signal | Bullish signal |

## 11. Verdict

### What most people get wrong

**The doomers** extrapolate current power concentration to permanent feudalism, ignoring that extreme inequality historically triggers correction (often violent). They treat the maximalist AI scenario — full labor obsolescence — as imminent when no credible economist forecasts it.

**The dismissers** point to today's modest macro data and conclude AI is hype. They're making the same error observers made about electricity in 1900 or computing in 1987. The productivity paradox is a feature of GPT adoption, not evidence of failure.

**The tech optimists** assume productivity gains automatically translate to widely shared prosperity. History emphatically disagrees. The gains from electrification were shared broadly only after decades of labor organizing, progressive taxation, and institutional reform. The technology doesn't determine the outcome; the political fight does.

### What the evidence supports (with confidence levels)

1. **AI is a genuine general-purpose technology.** *(High confidence.)* The evidence for this is strong across multiple dimensions: task-level capability, breadth of application, investment scale, early productivity signals. It is not hype. Whether it reaches the transformative scale of electricity or remains closer to computing's more bounded impact is genuinely uncertain.

2. **The transition period (now through ~2035) is the danger zone.** *(Medium-high confidence.)* Not because of full labor obsolescence, but because displacement will be real and concentrated (entry-level white-collar, Global South BPO, routine cognitive tasks) while institutional adaptation lags. The most acute harm will fall on those least equipped to absorb it.

3. **The distribution question matters enormously.** *(High confidence on the problem; low confidence on the framing.)* Even under modest productivity scenarios, AI will widen the capital-labor income gap. Under aggressive scenarios, it may generate enormous surplus — but there is no automatic mechanism to distribute it. I believe this is *the* central question, but I should acknowledge this is partly a values commitment, not a purely empirical finding.

4. **The financial bubble's structure is unsustainable.** *(Medium-high confidence.)* Circular financing, extreme concentration, and the spending-revenue gap point to a correction. But timing is uncertain, severity is uncertain, and the technology will survive the correction. I'm more confident something corrects than I am about when or how.

5. **The geopolitical dimension is underweighted in most analyses.** *(High confidence.)* The US-China AI arms race, Global South labor extraction, and regulatory fragmentation may matter more than any domestic policy debate. No adequate global institutional framework exists.

6. **AI discourse may be causing independent psychological harm.** *(Low-medium confidence. Hypothesis, not finding.)* The mechanism is plausible — tech executives hype capabilities to justify valuations, media amplifies, ambient dread follows. But the evidence base is thin and I elevated this beyond what the data strictly supports.

7. **Previous GPT patterns may not hold because cognitive work is the target this time.** *(Uncertain. This is the key unknown.)* I initially underweighted this. The standard reassurance — "new jobs always emerge" — depends on historical patterns where workers moved from manual to cognitive work. If cognitive work is itself being automated, the escape route may be blocked. I cannot assess the probability of this with any honesty.

### What I'm not sure about

- Whether the electricity analogy is the right framework or a misleading one
- Whether Acemoglu's modest estimates or Brynjolfsson's bullish estimates will look better in 3 years
- Whether the "this time is different" argument about cognitive work displacement is correct
- Whether democratic institutions will respond to AI-driven concentration before or after significant damage
- Whether the Global South's role in the AI supply chain is extraction or a developmental stepping stone

### The one thing nobody wants to hear

The most likely future is probably not dramatic. But I should be honest that **"the most likely future is a slow, uneven grind" is almost unfalsifiable** — so vague that nearly any outcome could be retrofitted to match it. It sounds measured and wise. It predicts nothing testable. That's a failure of analysis, not a feature.

What I can say with more specificity: **the next 5 years will be defined more by the financial bubble's trajectory and the political response to early displacement than by AI capabilities themselves.** The technology is developing faster than institutions can adapt. The gap between those two speeds is where the damage happens.

The technology is not deciding our future. We are. And we're not doing a great job of it so far.

## 12. Epistemic Audit

This section exists because the rest of the document reads as authoritative synthesis, and authoritative-sounding synthesis is dangerous when it conceals genuine uncertainty. Here's what this document gets wrong, presents with false confidence, or skates past.

### Where I was overconfident

| Claim | Problem |
|---|---|
| Electricity analogy as framework | Influential but contested among economic historians. I treated it as established. It's suggestive. |
| Scenario probabilities (40/25/25/10) | Subjective judgment presented in a format that implies calibration. Not calibrated. |
| "Distribution is the real question" | Partly a values commitment, not purely empirical. Growth economists would push back. |
| "AI fear matters more than displacement near-term" | Thin evidence base (two researchers, one poll). Hypothesis, not finding. |
| "Democratic correction will happen" | Historically true, but "correction" involved world wars and depressions. Sanitized framing. |
| "Slow, uneven grind" as most likely outcome | Nearly unfalsifiable. Vague enough to match almost any future. Not a prediction. |

### Where I was too dismissive

| Argument I dismissed | Why it deserves more weight |
|---|---|
| HN thread's doomers (tavavex, bm3719) | Algorithmic control of information is already real, not speculative. The moderate version of "surplus humans as cost center" (neglect, disinvestment) has historical precedent. |
| "This time is different" | Cognitive work is the traditional escape route for displaced workers. Automating the destination of previous transitions, not just the origin, has no historical precedent. The pattern may not hold. |
| Global South "colonial extraction" counterargument | Low-wage supply chain insertion has historically been a pathway to industrialization. Whether data labeling follows that pattern is empirically open. |
| Brynjolfsson's data | I reached for his COI rather than engaging seriously with his methodology. Ad hominem is not analysis. |

### What I genuinely don't know

- Whether AI capabilities plateau, continue linearly, or accelerate
- Whether the financial bubble corrects gently (dot-com style) or catastrophically (2008 style)
- Whether cognitive work displacement produces new job categories or permanent structural unemployment
- Whether Acemoglu's Narrow Corridor framework applies to a world where AI shifts the balance of power between states/corporations and society
- Whether any democratic polity will implement meaningful AI redistribution before the concentration trap closes

### The meta-problem

I wrote a 35KB document that reads as measured and comprehensive. "Measured" can be a form of intellectual dishonesty — hedged enough to never be wrong, which means never saying anything falsifiable. The parts of this document I'm most confident about (the bubble's financial structure, Global South exploitation *right now*, the gap between AI discourse and AI reality) are the most specific and testable. The parts I'm least confident about (the timeline, the scenarios, the "most likely future") are the most general and unfalsifiable. The reader should weight accordingly.
