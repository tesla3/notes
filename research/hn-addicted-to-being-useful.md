## HN Thread Distillation: "I'm addicted to being useful"

**Source:** [seangoedecke.com](https://www.seangoedecke.com/addicted-to-being-useful/) | [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46690402) (603 points, 309 comments, ~185 unique authors)

**Article summary:** Sean Goedecke, a staff engineer at GitHub, compares himself to Gogol's Akaky Akaievich — a man whose dysfunctional love of terrible work makes him perfectly suited to it. Goedecke argues many SWEs are driven by internal compulsions (being useful, solving puzzles, having total control) rather than money or ideology, and the smart move is to harness that compulsion effectively — directing it toward your management chain, not the ticket queue.

### Dominant Sentiment: Self-recognition mixed with caution

The thread splits cleanly: one half resonates deeply and shares their own version of the compulsion, the other half warns that this path leads to burnout, exploitation, or codependency. Notably warm and introspective for HN — multiple commenters share genuinely vulnerable personal stories.

### Key Insights

**1. The thread itself is the thesis demonstrated**

~40% of the thread's depth is consumed by a sprawling debate about emotional validation in relationships, spawned from tclancy's top comment about learning not to problem-solve his wife's venting. Dozens of commenters — self-identified compulsive problem-solvers — spend hundreds of words *problem-solving the concept of not problem-solving*. The meta-irony is invisible to participants. When Aurornis and n4r9 go twelve rounds over whether "validating emotions" means endorsing them, they're acting out the exact compulsion the article describes: seeing a conceptual problem and being unable to walk past it.

**2. Three distinct phenomena wearing one label**

The article conflates them; the thread unconsciously unbundles: (a) **intrinsic craft pleasure** — the Factorio/crossword crowd who'd be doing puzzles regardless of context (duhprey, scjon, niedbalski), (b) **identity-as-useful** — the working dog who needs to matter to others (the author, waltwalther, bloomingeek), and (c) **trauma-driven compulsion** — tombert's raw account of developing a savior complex after Lowtax's suicide, where "helping" is really guilt management. These have completely different failure modes. (a) is benign; (b) is exploitable; (c) is clinical. The thread treats them interchangeably.

tombert's comment stands out for sheer honesty: *"Everyone thinks they're a good person, and everyone thinks they'll do the right thing when it matters. I certainly thought that, and ultimately I guess that's not strictly true."* AkshatM's lengthy reply is one of the most compassionate things I've seen on HN — a genuine reframe of bystander guilt that distinguishes between having *knowledge* and having *power*.

**3. keybored reads the author's corpus and finds the real thesis**

keybored cross-references Goedecke's other posts ([Good Times Are Over](https://www.seangoedecke.com/good-times-are-over/), [A Little Bit Cynical](https://www.seangoedecke.com/a-little-bit-cynical/), [Shareholder Value](https://www.seangoedecke.com/shareholder-value/)) and identifies the pattern: the author's "usefulness" is carefully directed at his management chain, and his linked articles explicitly advise protecting your time from anyone who can't directly reward you in performance reviews. What's presented as endearing dysfunction is actually a highly optimized career strategy. keybored: *"Cynical just meant merrily working within the gears of the professional system. Then having no complaints about it. No commentary beyond gaining both money and pleasure from aligning with optimizing shareholder value."*

**4. choonway names the problem the article avoids**

> *"I was like the author, then I realized that I was solving problems that were created by other peoples' incompetence. Sure they were challenging, fun but they didn't bring anything positive overall. The incompetent people are still there — causing more problems."*

This is the sharpest challenge in the thread. The compulsion to be useful doesn't interrogate whether the problem deserves solving. It treats all problems as fungible sources of satisfaction. jebarker makes the same point from a different angle: *"99% of them feel like they shouldn't even exist and solving them doesn't really lead to any meaningful progress."* giraffe_lady's reply — that the only real answer is moving into management — is depressing precisely because it's probably right.

**5. AI threatens the identity, not just the job**

Ronsenshi: *"I actually enjoy the process of writing code, understanding deeply the system I work on, finding elegant solutions... not just a list of checkboxes with features for a given sprint that agent churns in background."* drekipus counters that he feels *more* useful post-AI — but his usefulness is now as a quality filter deleting AI-generated fluff, not as a creator. This inadvertently validates Ronsenshi's fear: the craft that generates the compulsion is being hollowed out, even as "usefulness" is preserved. The thread doesn't follow this line to its conclusion.

**6. The retirement angle reveals this is about mattering, not engineering**

bradley13's story about an African politician visiting UK social housing — finding purposeless people "horrible" — and nolamark's citation of the "mattering span" concept point to something bigger than career advice. The compulsion to be useful is a human universal, not an engineer quirk. The thread keeps circling back to purpose-as-survival (pjc50's disability rights angle, didibus on retirement happiness). dkarl goes full Marx: *"If you're working for the satisfaction of being useful to others, that's not dysfunction. That's you beating the odds and having a healthy relationship to your work despite the external social pressure to make it about the money."*

**7. "Help is the sunny side of control"**

harryday drops this grenade and the thread splits predictably. ambicapter calls it "an extremely toxic mindset." Ronsenshi defends it as "acts of service" love language. inanutshellus offers a sweet counterexample (his SIL queues up household tasks for him). But JohnMakin nails the actual distinction: *"Make sure you're addicted to being useful, and not addicted to being needed — the latter can come about by being useful enough."* The shift from useful→needed is where the trait becomes pathological, and the article never examines this boundary.

### Common Pushbacks

| Pushback | Quality | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| This leads to burnout | Medium | Author and several commenters argue the *opposite* — burnout comes from unsatisfied compulsion, not from the compulsion itself. Neither side has evidence. |
| Corporations will exploit this | Strong | spicyusername's warning is well-stated. The author's own "predators" article proves the point: the very trait requires active defense. |
| This is just learned behavior / conditioning | Medium | vjerancrnjak, deepvibrations argue it's societal conditioning. Plausible but unfalsifiable — applies equally to any preference. |
| The word "addiction" is self-serving | Strong | d--b: *"That makes it sound like self-pat-on-the-back disguised as a problem."* Reframes it as obsession, which is more honest. |
| Net-negative work exists | Strong | drivebyhooting, mirawelner, css_apologist challenge whether being useful to Facebook's ad targeting or GitHub's parent company (Microsoft) is actually useful to *anyone*. |

### What the Thread Misses

- **Class dimension.** The author is a staff engineer at GitHub — excellent compensation, high autonomy, interesting problems. The "addiction to being useful" only feels like a gift when material conditions are excellent. Below a certain income/autonomy threshold, the same trait is just being exploited. The one comment touching this (elzbardico: *"a corporate job pays much more, and the work load is a tiny fraction of other jobs"*) hand-waves it.

- **Institutional selection.** Nobody asks whether large tech companies *systematically select for* this trait — through interview design, promotion criteria, and performance review structures that reward exactly this kind of person. The compulsion isn't just individual psychology; it's a co-evolved relationship between workers and institutions.

- **The identity-fragility problem with AI.** The thread has two comments on AI and drops it. But if your identity is "the person who solves the hard problems," and a model starts solving them, you don't just lose a job — you lose a self. The author's own framing ("I'm kind of like a working dog") becomes ominous: what happens to working dogs when there's no work?

### Verdict

The article says "I'm dysfunctional and my job matches my dysfunction" — self-deprecating, charming, and ultimately evasive. The thread keeps debating whether the trait is healthy, but nobody asks the sharper question that keybored circles: **useful to whom?** The author's own linked articles answer it explicitly — useful to your management chain, carefully guarding your usefulness from "predators" who can't compensate you. What's framed as an involuntary compulsion is actually a deliberate allocation strategy. The working-dog analogy is more revealing than intended: working dogs don't choose the task, and they don't question whether the sheep need herding. The real addiction isn't to being useful — it's to the feeling of usefulness, which is a much more manipulable thing.
