## Key Insights

### 1. The Timeline Conflict Is More Nuanced Than It Appears
If the team gets Western Edge (Long Beach, May 28–31), the Premier Event itself is **12+ days after the last AP exam**. There is no direct conflict between the event and exams. The real pressure is the **8-week prep period (mid-March through mid-May)** when FTC robot improvement work competes with AP study and coursework.

### 2. She's at the Bottom of the Advancing Group
Bengal Bots ranked 16th with Think Award 2nd Place. Among the 11 advancing teams, they are the weakest by the user's own assessment. There are 15+ teams with awards or playoff finishes competing for 11 slots — Bengal Bots may be on the bubble. This matters because: if they're a waitlist alternate, investing emotional energy into planning is premature.

### 3. Think Award = Her Superpower and Her Flexibility
Think Award evaluates engineering notebook quality, software strategy, and iterative design documentation. This is likely her core contribution. Critically, **this work can be done asynchronously in small blocks** — it doesn't require daily shop time. This is the basis for any "structured hybrid" approach.

### 4. De Facto Lead Without Title = Maximum Burden, Minimum Authority
She carries the work of captain without the standing to delegate. She can't easily say "someone else own this" because the team structure doesn't support it. This is an energy drain that's invisible on any schedule but real in practice.

### 5. She Has Two More Seasons
As a sophomore in her first FTC year, she has junior and senior year ahead. Think Award 2nd Place + Premier Event advancement in year 1 is already exceptional. The Premier Event is a bonus, not the capstone. A stronger story: return next year as captain with the experience and authority to lead properly.

### 6. AP Scores vs. AP Grades — Different Stakes
AP exam scores matter for college credit/placement but are mostly invisible during admissions (self-reported, arrive post-decision). What matters for admissions is the **GPA in those AP classes** and the transcript showing she took 4 APs as a sophomore. Risk: if FTC prep erodes her daily performance in classes, the grade damage is more consequential than a lower AP exam score.

### 7. The $2,000+ Cost Is Real
Registration alone is $2,000 for Western Edge. Plus travel, food, lodging for Long Beach weekend. For a family/community-sponsored team with no corporate backers, this is a significant financial commitment that should factor into the decision.

## Possible Scenarios

### A: Go to Western Edge, Structured Two-Phase Approach
- March–May 16: FTC at 3–5 hrs/week (documentation, code review, strategy). Full AP focus.
- May 17–28: Full FTC sprint after exams end.
- **Requires:** team can function at reduced capacity from her, other members step up, boundaries hold.
- **Risk:** she over-commits out of felt obligation, boundaries don't hold, grades slip.

### B: Go, But Reframe as Learning Experience
- Same timeline as A, but drop competitive expectations.
- "We're a 2nd-year team, we're here to learn."
- Reduces robot-improvement pressure, which reduces prep hours.
- **Probably the healthiest framing if they go.**

### C: Decline, Come Back Stronger Next Year
- Season ends with Think Award 2nd Place at regionals as a sophomore. Strong line.
- Junior year: return as experienced veteran, earn captain title, better-balanced AP load.
- **When this is right:** grades already strained, team can't self-sustain, financial burden is real, or she's already running on fumes.

### D: Accept If Confirmed, Don't Chase If Waitlisted
- If "nominated to advance" means waitlist: don't agonize. Submit preferences as a low-cost option, focus on APs, and treat a slot materializing as a pleasant surprise rather than a commitment to plan around.
- **This might be the most pragmatic path given the ambiguity.**

## Parent's Decision Framework

The core question is not "FTC or APs." It's: **what does she need to learn right now about managing competing commitments?**

- If she tends to overcommit and burn out → Scenario C teaches strategic withdrawal as strength.
- If she tends to play it safe → Scenario B teaches showing up imperfectly.
- If she's genuinely energized → Scenario A with real structure and parental scaffolding on boundaries.

**Diagnostic question to ask her:** "If the team didn't need you, would you still want to go?" The answer reveals whether this is intrinsic motivation or felt responsibility.
