## What ISEF Is

Regeneron ISEF — world's largest pre-college STEM competition. ~1,700–1,800 finalists from ~70 countries. **22 categories.** Up to $9M in annual prizes. 0.03% acceptance rate from the ~7 million students in affiliated fairs globally.

**Pipeline:** School Fair → Regional/County → State/National Affiliated Fair → ISEF

### Grand Awards (Per Category, 22 Categories)

Dozens of professional organizations (NOAA, AAAI, IEEE, ACS, ASA, DCAT, etc.) give independent awards ($500–$50K+). A project can win **multiple** Special Awards on top of Grand Awards. Special Award judges visit booths independently with their own criteria.

### Dominant Pattern

1. **Massive cost/accessibility improvement** — make expensive/inaccessible → cheap/accessible
2. **Elegant simplification** — fewer steps, not more complexity
3. **One-sentence quantified impact** — "$75/g → $12.50/g" is unforgettable

5. **Deep ownership** — can explain every decision under pressure

> "Judges look for students who have become **budding scientists**, not the one with the fanciest methodologies or most robust results."

## Judging Criteria (100 Points)

**Science and Engineering have separate rubrics** — both with 5 sections:

| Section | Points | Key |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Research Question / Engineering Goals | 10 | Clear, focused, novel, addresses a gap |
| Design & Methodology | 15 | Rigorous, controls defined, reproducible |
| Execution & Data | 20 | Thoroughness, skill, statistical rigor |
| Creativity & Innovation | 20 | Evaluated through **outcomes and analysis**, not idea alone |
| Analysis & Conclusions | 15 | Sound interpretation, limitations acknowledged, null results included |
| **Presentation/Interview** | **20** | Understanding, independence, ability to discuss failures |

### What Judges Probe in Interview

- Degree of **independence** ("What did YOU do?")
- **Why** each methodological choice was made
- Honest discussion of **failures** — proves ownership
- Future vision and real-world impact
- Understanding of **limitations**
- If lab-based (Form 1C): can they separate their work from the lab's?

## Critical Rules & Compliance (2026)

### 🚨 AI Usage Policy — Disqualification Risk

> "The inappropriate use of AI [is] prohibited and grounds for the project to fail to qualify."

- ✅ AI as brainstorming/literature tool — **if cited**
- ✅ AI as research *subject* (building/testing ML models)
- ❌ AI writing abstract, paper, or presentation
- ❌ Using AI content without disclosure
- ⚠️ AI-generated code you can't explain in interview = risky
- **Failure to disclose = disqualification**

In 2025–2026, students were disqualified for AI-hallucinated citations (wrong authors, nonexistent journals).

### 12-Month Research Window

Max 12 months continuous research; cannot include work more than 18 months before competition. For ISEF 2027: only research from ~Nov 2025–May 2027 qualifies. Background reading/planning before this is fine.

### Form 1C — Independence Paradox

Lab-based work triggers scrutiny. **Optimal structure:** mentor provides intellectual direction (advisory, remote); student executes independently (home/school lab). Home-based with remote mentor = procedurally safest.

### Form 7 — Continuation Projects

Must demonstrate genuinely **new and different** research each year. Cannot re-present Year 1 data.

### Virtual Booth (Required)

All finalists must complete a virtual booth in addition to physical display. Viewed by Special Award judges who may not visit physically.

### Forms Before Experiments

SRC approval, IRB (human subjects), IACUC (animals), Form 1C (lab work). Missing forms = disqualification. Register SRC by September.

## The Affiliated Fair Bottleneck

Each fair has a designated ISEF allocation. Variance is **enormous**: a large state fair might advance 5-15 from 500+ entries; a smaller regional might advance 2-3 from 50. **Choosing which fair = strategic decision.** Check: findafair.societyforscience.org

## ISEF as an Admissions Signal

### Signal Tiers (How Admissions Officers Read It)

| Tier | What | Signal Strength |
|------|------|-----------------|
| 1 | Grand Award Winners (top ~20) | Near-guaranteed admit anywhere |
| 2 | Category Awards (~600 students) | Very strong — but 1 in 3 finalists win something |
| 3 | ISEF Finalist (no award) | Strong extracurricular, not a "spike" |
| 4 | Regional/State winner (no ISEF) | Modest signal — thousands reach this level |

> "For college admissions, an ISEF Grand Prize carries weight comparable to international olympiad medals or published research in peer-reviewed journals."

**Caveat:** No credible source backs "85% of finalists admitted to top-30" claims. That conflates finalists (1,800) with Grand Prize winners (3-5/year).

### vs. Regeneron STS

STS: 2,600 applicants → 300 scholars → 40 finalists → 10 winners. Paper-based (harder to fake). 85-year track record (Nobel laureates). Arguably a purer signal than ISEF, but much smaller (40 finalists vs. 1,800).

## Integrity Threats

### Fraud

- **Krish Pai (2024):** Won $55K first prize with fabricated data/photoshopped gel images. Caught by anonymous whistleblower, NOT by ISEF's review process. Withdrew after confronted.
- **AI-hallucinated citations (2025–2026):** Multiple disqualifications for non-existent papers/journals generated by ChatGPT.

### The Commercial Coaching Industry

A sub-vertical of the ~$3.4B college consulting industry:

**Tier 1 — ISEF-Specific Coaching ($5K–$15K+):**
- ScienceFair.io: 10 coaching sessions + 45 hours research mentorship + publication-ready paper + mock judging
- YRI Science: $2,997 (PhD mentors, comprehensive coaching)
- CollegeNinja.ai: Targeted ISEF mentorship

**Tier 2 — Research Mentorship Platforms ($2,500–$13,000):**
- Polygence (starts $2,895), Lumiere Education, Algoverse (AI/ML specialist), Veritas AI

**Tier 3 — College Consultants Packaging "Research Spike":**
- Cosmic College Consulting, others orchestrating the full admissions narrative

### The Assembly Line

```
Grade 8-9: College consultant identifies "STEM spike" strategy
Grade 9-10: Paid PhD mentor shapes topic + methodology ($3K-$13K)
Grade 10-11: ISEF coach optimizes presentation + mock judging ($3K-$10K)
Grade 11-12: Qualify → ISEF → admissions packaging
Total investment: $10K-$30K+ over 2-3 years
```

### The Equity Reality

A winning project typically requires: university lab access, faculty mentor (via connections), $1K-$10K+ in materials, months of dedicated time, travel costs. Disproportionately measures access to resources alongside talent.

### Assessment

> ISEF is not broken, but it's no longer a "clean" signal like a Math Olympiad gold (timed exam, you solve it or you don't). It's closer to a research publication — impressive but verifiable only with effort, increasingly gamed by those with resources.

## ML/AI at ISEF — Specific Intelligence

- **11% of 2025 ISEF winners used AI/ML** — ~2× higher than overall finalist pool
- BUT: **judges have ML fatigue.** A longtime judge (2023): "ML was EVERYWHERE. 75% of TMED projects were ML. Judges were annoyed. Most aren't ML experts. They feel students replace foundational science with ML flash."
- **Recommendation:** Don't use ML unless you have strong underlying science AND can explain backpropagation/training mechanics. ML as black-box tool = liability in interview.

## Optimal Strategy (Ranked by Impact)

### Non-Negotiable Foundations

1. **Find a PhD-level mentor, structured correctly** — advisory role + independent execution. Satisfies quality bar AND Form 1C.
2. **Solve a real problem with quantified impact** — take something expensive/inaccessible → cheap/accessible. One number.
3. **Comply flawlessly from Day 1** — SRC before experiments, all forms, AI disclosure, lab notebook, 12-month window.

### Competitive Differentiation

4. **Choose qualifying path strategically** — research local affiliated fairs and their ISEF allocation. Less competitive fair with 2 slots from 40 entries > hyper-competitive state fair with 10 from 600.
5. **Select less saturated category** — cross-disciplinary projects create legitimate optionality.
6. **Design for the scoresheet** — structure everything around the 100-point rubric explicitly.
7. **Target Special Awards** — align project with relevant SAOs' missions. High-EV side strategy.

### Execution Excellence

8. **Interview is where it's won or lost** — practice with mock judges (scientists). Be enthusiastically honest about failures. Explain every decision without notes.
9. **Abstract: 250 words, precision** — question → approach (one sentence) → key results (quantified) → significance.
10. **Virtual booth + physical display** — both are evaluated. Virtual reaches judges who won't visit physically.

## Key Insight: The Coaching Paradox

Almost every top ISEF project has PhD-level mentorship. The commercial ecosystem makes this available for $3K-$13K. The ethical spectrum:

| Level | Description | Assessment |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Student cold-emails professor for lab access | Initiative + luck | Clean |
| School teacher mentors student | System working | Clean |
| Free merit-selected program (RSI, PRIMES) | Merit-selected | Clean |
| Paid Polygence-style mentor ($3K) | Paid mentorship | Gray |
| $10K+ ISEF coach + research mentor + mock judging | Professional optimization | Deeply gray |
| Mentor designs project, student executes | Ghost research | Fraud-adjacent |

The line between "extensive help" and "ghost research" is nearly impossible to police. The Society for Science rules require student's own work — but when a PhD spends 45 hours shaping methodology, topic, and analysis, what's "the student's own work"?

**Bottom line:** Get mentorship (everyone does). But the student must genuinely own the science — because the interview will expose who doesn't.
