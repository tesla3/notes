# Pacific Ridge College Guidance — Critical Evaluation

**Sources:**
- Google Sites CG page: https://sites.google.com/pacificridge.org/collegeguidance (all subpages)
- Main school website CG section: https://www.pacificridge.org/academics/college-guidance (including grade tabs, matriculations)
- College Profile 2025-26 (PDF), Curriculum Guide 2026-27 (PDF), Curriculum Progressions 2025-26 (PDF), 9th Grade Launch Slides 12/4/25 (PDF)
**Date reviewed:** 2026-03-19
**Self-audit:** 2026-03-19 — Corrected errors in testing policy attribution (MIT/Georgetown), AP exam statistics interpretation, filmmaking level count, NACAC→ASCA attribution, Google Sites mobile claim, essay/interview/ED-EA coverage (found on main site), Wellesley/Wesleyan discrepancy framing, course rotation example, engineering AP count. ## Summary Verdict

A program with genuine structural strengths — excellent counselor ratio, cohort model, robust financial aid education, impressive programming (Coastline Case Studies, Summer Kickoff, Camp College), and a curriculum with real depth. But its public-facing communication is fractured across two disconnected websites, neither of which tells the full story. The Google Sites CG page has detailed programming content but no outcomes data, no grade-by-grade services, and significant maintenance issues. The main school website has structured grade-by-grade content and matriculations but buries it in collapsible tabs. Internal documents (college profile, curriculum guide) reveal a school significantly more serious than either website suggests. The central problem is not what the school does — it's that families have to assemble the picture from scattered, inconsistent, and sometimes contradictory sources.

## What's Strong

### 1. Counselor Ratio & Cohort Model
4 full-time counselors for 108 seniors (Class of 2026) — roughly **27 students per counselor** (the 9th grade launch slides say ~30). Each counselor joins a grade-level team and follows their class from 10th grade through graduation. This is genuinely excellent — it builds relationship depth that matters for writing school reports and giving individualized advice. Weekly senior meetings are high-touch and rare even among well-resourced independent schools. For context, ASCA recommends a 250:1 ratio for school counselors (most public schools far exceed this); top independent schools aim for 20-35:1 for college counseling specifically. PRS is solidly within that range.

### 2. Financial Aid Programming
This is the standout. The multi-part Financial Aid Forms & Formulas series, partnership with College Money Method, and deliberately sequenced Zoom sessions across the year (targeted by grade level) is more sophisticated than what most independent schools offer. The framing — "it is never too early to begin understanding" — is correct, and the specific session topics (how colleges assess ability to pay, evaluating loans, comparing awards, appeal strategies) cover ground that many schools leave to families to figure out on their own. Genuinely impressive and clearly designed by someone who understands that financial literacy is a prerequisite for good college decisions.

### 3. Anti-Resume-Padding Philosophy
The messaging consistently pushes authenticity over credential-stuffing: "Think of it less as currency and more as discovery" (summer opportunities), "Be true to yourself. Authenticity matters most, so look to the substance of the experience, beyond the name brand college sponsoring it." This is the right message, and the school appears to mean it. The recommended reading list (Schwartz's *Paradox of Choice*, Kahneman's *Thinking Fast and Slow*, Pope's *Looking Beyond the Ivy League*) signals intellectual seriousness about the meta-problem of college admissions anxiety.

### 4. Arts/Portfolio Guidance
The Artists & Designers page is the most practically detailed page on the entire site. SlideRoom instructions, fee structures, file size limits, timeline advice, the YouTube `_popup` trick, and the connection to MIT's Maker Portfolio — this reads like someone who has walked students through this process many times and compiled the actual questions they get asked. The acknowledgment that engineering programs have optional Maker Portfolios broadens this beyond fine arts.

### 5. Camp College & Summer Kickoff
Seniors return to campus before classes begin for **Camp College Days** — refining Common App, preparing UC application, reviewing interview policies, priority deadlines, and application options with admission officers on site. This creates momentum before the academic year competes for attention.

Separately, **Summer Kickoff Days** in May brings a dozen admission officers to campus to help *juniors* begin application work: Common App account creation, page-by-page review, short-answer essay drafts, a research exercise, mock interviews, and essay review with college reps. This is a strong bridge between junior and senior year.

### 5a. Coastline Case Studies — A Hidden Gem
The main website reveals a program not mentioned on the Google Sites CG page: **Coastline Case Studies** brings 100+ college admission officers to San Diego annually to meet in small groups with juniors and parents. They run **mock admissions committees** — reading 3-4 applications, deciding who's admitted, waitlisted, or denied. PRS dedicates grade-level time in April to preparing students for this event. This is genuinely impressive programming that teaches students *how admissions actually works* from the inside. The fact that it's buried in the main website's grade tabs and not featured prominently on the CG site is a missed marketing opportunity.

## What's Weak

### 6. Outcomes Data Exists — But Split Across Two Disconnected Platforms
Matriculation data IS published on the **main school website** (pacificridge.org/academics/college-guidance/collegematriculations) — a "Where Our Firebirds Fly Next" page listing 446 graduates across Classes 2022-2025. However, it does **not** appear on the **Google Sites college guidance page** (sites.google.com/pacificridge.org/collegeguidance) — which is the CG office's primary working site with all the detailed programming, testing, and timeline content. The two platforms are disconnected with no cross-links.

This split web presence is itself a problem. A parent lands on the Google Sites CG page and finds no outcomes data. They'd have to independently navigate to the main PRS website's college guidance section — which has different content, different navigation, and a different design. The CG office effectively maintains two sites that don't talk to each other.

Additionally, the main website's matriculation list **differs from the college profile PDF** sent to colleges, despite both claiming to cover the same 4-year period. The website adds ~15 schools not in the profile (including Duke, Wesleyan, Vassar, Notre Dame, Wake Forest, Washington & Lee) and drops ~9 (Cal Poly Pomona, Grossmont College, Wellesley, Michigan State, etc.). Several schools gain asterisks (Harvard, Pomona, Fordham). These discrepancies are unexplained — either the website was updated post-printing, the profile was an incomplete snapshot, or there are data quality issues. See §19 for detailed analysis.

**What best-practice looks like:** One authoritative source for matriculation data, consistent across platforms, plus scattergrams in SCOIR showing GPA-vs-outcome by school.

### 7. Heavy on Philosophy, Light on Actionable Tools
The 9-12 Grade Timelines page is a narrative essay, not a usable timeline. It's ~1,000 words of flowing prose. There are no:
- Month-by-month checklists
- Specific deadlines (beyond the PSAT date)
- Action items students can track
- Visual timelines or calendar views

Compare to a school like Lakeside (Seattle) or Harvard-Westlake — their timelines are structured, scannable, and designed as working documents families reference repeatedly. PRS's version is something you read once and then forget what it said.

### 8. Essay Support Exists In-Person — But No Public-Facing Resources
**Correction:** The main website reveals essay support is more substantial than the Google Sites page suggests. **Essay Workshops** are presented by CG in junior English classes (in collaboration with faculty), counselors provide **input on subsequent essay drafts**, and Summer Kickoff Days include short-answer essay drafting and essay review with college reps.

However, there remains:
- No dedicated essay resources page on either site
- No guidance on what makes a strong personal statement
- No links to quality external resources
- No explanation of supplemental essay types (Why Us, Community, Activity, etc.)
- No information about how many essays a typical applicant should expect to write

The service exists; the public-facing documentation doesn't. A family reviewing either website before enrolling would have no idea this support is available. For current families, a resources page would extend the in-person workshop value with tools students can reference independently.

### 9. No College List Building Framework
How should a student construct a balanced college list? How many schools? What's the reach/match/likely framework? How do you research schools beyond rankings? What questions should you ask on a campus visit? What factors actually predict student satisfaction?

None of this appears anywhere. The philosophy says students are "guided to create [college options] for themselves" but provides no framework for doing so. This is table-stakes content for a college guidance site.

### 10. ED/EA/RD Strategy: Mentioned in Passing, Never Explained
The main website's 12th-grade tab references "priority deadlines" and "application options" — confirming these topics are covered at Camp College. But the terms Early Decision, Early Action, Early Decision II, Restrictive Early Action, and Rolling Admission never appear on either website by name. There is no explainer content for families encountering these concepts for the first time.

These are among the most consequential tactical decisions in the application process. ED acceptance rates at selective schools run 2-3x regular decision rates. The strategic implications are enormous, particularly when intersected with financial aid (ED is binding; you can't compare packages). For a school with strong financial aid programming, the absence of ED/financial aid tension discussion is a notable gap.

### 11. Testing Advice Has an Anti-Testing Bias
The testing pages are heavily editorialized toward discouraging testing. Key claims:

- *"If ever there was a time not to rush to testing, this is it!"*
- *"Contrary to the current hype, it is a small number of colleges/universities that have gone back to requiring test scores"*
- *"it had been more than 5 years in the making, so their test-blind policy in admission is unlikely to change in the foreseeable future"* (about UC)

The problem: the landscape has shifted meaningfully since these pages were written. Georgetown has always required scores (never went test-optional). MIT reinstated its requirement in 2022. And in the 2024-26 admissions cycles, Dartmouth, Brown, Yale, Caltech, Harvard, Cornell, Princeton, Purdue, and others returned to requiring or strongly recommending scores. The "small number" framing is increasingly inaccurate for the tier of schools PRS families are likely targeting.

The blanket advice — *"If testing seems like a strength for you, press on it. If not, let's talk about more interesting and rewarding ways to spend your time!"* — is too casual about the competitive advantage scores provide at institutions that remain test-optional (where submitting strong scores materially helps, per admissions data). For a school serving affluent families with access to test prep resources, this advice may be leaving advantage on the table.

The charitable read: they're protecting students from the anxiety industrial complex of test prep companies. The critical read: they're telling families what they want to hear rather than what they need to hear.

### 12. No Mental Health / Wellness Resources
The philosophy acknowledges the process is "emotionally charged." The site provides zero resources for:
- Managing application-season anxiety
- Handling rejection or waitlists
- Maintaining perspective and well-being
- Knowing when to seek help

Given the documented mental health impact of college admissions pressure on high school students, this is a notable gap for a program that claims to be student-centered.

### 13. No Gap Year or Alternative Pathways
For a school that champions "authentic self-discovery," the complete absence of gap year information, community college transfer paths, vocational/trade options, or anything beyond the 4-year-college conveyor belt is revealing. It's "College Guidance," not "Post-Secondary Guidance" — and the framing tells you where the institutional assumptions live. Not every student's authentic path leads to a 4-year university in the fall after graduation.

### 14. No Recommendation Letter Guidance
Which teachers should you ask? When should you ask? How many? What information should you provide to recommenders? How do counselor recommendations differ from teacher recommendations? None of this is covered.

### 15. Interview Preparation: Exists, Not Documented
**Correction:** The main website reveals **mock interviews** at Summer Kickoff Days and Camp College covers **interview policies**. So interview prep does happen in practice. But no resources or guidance appear on either website for students preparing independently — what to expect, how to prepare, common formats (alumni vs. admissions, evaluative vs. informational).

## Maintenance & Presentation Issues

### 16. Stale Content
- NCAA Night PowerPoint is from **January 2022** — over 4 years old. NCAA rules have changed significantly (NIL, transfer portal).
- NYT articles linked on the athletics page are from **2005**. Twenty-one years old.
- The ACT digital transition phrasing ("The ACT is last to launch their own, shorter online ACT") reads as temporally ambiguous — the transition completed in 2024 for US students, but the present-tense "is last to launch" could describe either an ongoing rollout or a completed event. The Science section is described as "optional" without noting that this change took effect in spring 2025. The page would benefit from a clear "as of [date]" framing.
- Summer opportunities links (collegexpress, etc.) are of unknown viability.

### 17. Spelling and Grammar Errors
On official school pages:
- **"Programing"** (nav) vs. "Programming" (page title) — inconsistent, former is misspelled
- **"most relevent"** → "most relevant"
- **"Grave Level Project"** → "Grade Level Project"
- **"famlies"** → "families"
- **"autonomy and and encourage"** → doubled "and"
- **"the college search and application experience is"** — dangling clause after "We treat"

For a school selling a premium educational experience, copy errors on the college guidance site are a bad signal. They suggest the site isn't reviewed or maintained with care.

### 18. Platform Limitations
Google Sites is a free, basic platform. The navigation buries pages behind a "More" dropdown. There are no embedded calendars, checklists, or interactive tools. Meanwhile, the main school website runs on **Finalsite** — a professional school CMS — and the CG office's content could be integrated there instead. The choice to maintain a separate Google Sites presence alongside the main site's CG section creates the fragmentation problem described in §6, and makes the CG office look less polished than the institution it represents.

## Curriculum & College Profile: What the Internal Documents Reveal

The college profile and curriculum guide paint a significantly richer picture than the guidance website. They also expose tensions the website ignores entirely.

### 19. Matriculation Data: What It Reveals (and What Doesn't Add Up)

PRS publishes matriculation data in two places that don't agree with each other: the main website (pacificridge.org) and the college profile PDF. Both claim Classes 2022-2025 / 446 graduates, but the lists diverge.

**Schools on the website but NOT in the profile PDF:** Duke, Wesleyan, Vassar, Wake Forest, Notre Dame, Washington & Lee, Sciences Po/Columbia, University of Toronto — all selective additions. Also: Emerson, Southern Connecticut State, BYU Idaho, Regis, Santa Monica College, University of Redlands.

**Schools in the profile PDF but NOT on the website:** Cal Poly Pomona, CSU Fullerton, Grossmont College, Marist, Michigan State, Seton Hall, Wellesley (!), Boise State, University of Alabama.

**Asterisk upgrades on website vs. profile:** Harvard (single → multiple), Pomona (single → multiple), Fordham, Ohio State, St. Olaf, University of San Francisco all gain asterisks on the website.

Some discrepancies may be benign — the website was likely updated after the profile PDF was printed, incorporating late enrollment data and corrections. The Wellesley (profile) → Wesleyan (website) swap could be a name correction (these are different schools with similar names, and a data entry error in either direction is plausible). Harvard University → Harvard College\* is a name correction plus updated count. But the net pattern — selective schools added, less-selective schools removed — could also reflect editorial choices. Without explanation, the inconsistencies create unnecessary doubt. **A simple versioning note ("Updated March 2026") or a single authoritative source would resolve this.**

**The top tier, read generously:** Harvard\*, Princeton, Yale, Stanford\*, Columbia\*, Brown, Duke, Georgetown\*, Cornell\*, Johns Hopkins\*, Rice, UPenn, Vanderbilt\*, WashU\*, Wesleyan, Pomona\*. Over 4 years / 446 graduates, placing ~20-25 students at highly selective schools (rough estimate given asterisks). That's roughly 5% into Ivy-plus tier — respectable for a school this size but not elite-feeder territory.

**The center of gravity:** UC Berkeley\*, UCLA\*, UCSD\*, USC\*, Northeastern\*, BU\*, NYU\*, Chapman\*, LMU\*, Pepperdine\*, SCU\*, SDSU\*. Strong UCs and well-regarded private universities. This is where the bulk of PRS graduates land, and it's a solid outcome.

**Community colleges with asterisks:** MiraCosta\*, Palomar\*, College of the Redwoods\*, Santa Monica College\*, DeAnza, Santa Barbara City College. Multiple students from a ~$45K/year school enrolling at local community colleges over 4 years. This could reflect smart financial decisions, gap year pivots, learning differences, or students who weren't well-served. The college guidance site's complete silence on alternative pathways and community college as a deliberate choice is more conspicuous when you see this data. If community college is a valid outcome — and it can be — say so and support it. If it's not the intended outcome, that's worth understanding too.

**Regional distribution** (2025 only): California 20%, Northeast 35%, Southeast 15%, West/Mountain 17%, Midwest 8%, Pacific NW 5%. The Northeast pull is strong for a SoCal school — credit to the guidance office for broadening horizons beyond the UC system.

### 20. The AP Cap Is a Consequential Policy That Guidance Never Contextualizes

PRS caps APs at **3 for juniors, 4 for seniors**, recommended by faculty. This is stated in the college profile sent to colleges and in the curriculum guide, but the college guidance website never mentions it.

This cap is philosophically defensible — it prevents burnout, protects the school's distinctive non-AP courses, and keeps workload manageable. Many progressive independent schools do it. But it's also a **competitive constraint** that families need to understand:

- At highly selective schools, applicants from peer institutions often present 8-10 APs. PRS students max out at 7 (3+4).
- The cap means **course selection is zero-sum.** Taking AP Bio means *not* taking AP Chem junior year. This makes sequencing advice critical.
- Combined with the school's anti-testing messaging, students have **two of the three major academic signals** (APs, test scores, GPA) constrained. The school is betting heavily that the profile, the Harkness method, and the counselor relationship compensate. That's a bet some families may not want to make unknowingly.

The college guidance site should explain: here's our cap, here's why, here's how colleges view it, here's what the school profile says to provide context. Instead: silence.

### 21. The Integrated Science Sequence Creates a Navigational Challenge

PRS uses an integrated model: **Science 9** and **Science 10** (interdisciplinary, not traditional biology/chemistry/physics). Students don't take a named, standalone lab science until 11th grade electives. This is progressive pedagogy — and it creates a specific problem for college applications that the guidance site ignores.

**The issue:** Selective colleges, especially STEM programs, expect to see Biology, Chemistry, and Physics on the transcript. PRS students show "Science 9" and "Science 10" — which could mean anything to an admissions reader unfamiliar with the school. The college profile explains the curriculum, but:

- There is no guidance for students on which 11th/12th science electives to choose based on intended major
- A student aiming for engineering likely needs Honors Physics + AP Physics C + AP Chemistry. While Honors Physics isn't an AP, taking AP Physics C (which requires concurrent calculus) + AP Chemistry + AP Calculus consumes all 3 junior AP slots for STEM alone — zero room for humanities APs until senior year
- A pre-med student needs AP Biology + AP Chemistry minimum, leaving only 1 AP junior year for non-science
- The guidance website never addresses how to navigate these tradeoffs

The curriculum guide shows Science 9/10 covers interdisciplinary content (physics, chemistry, engineering, biology, ecology), but **the college guidance office should help families understand how this is perceived and how to compensate.**

### 22. SAT/ACT Score Distribution Contextualizes the Testing Bias

The college profile reports the Class of 2026's **middle 50% SAT: 1150-1450, ACT: 29-34.**

This is revealing. A 1150 SAT (25th percentile) is below the median admitted student at most Top 50 universities. A 1450 (75th percentile) is competitive at most schools outside the Ivy-plus tier. The range of 300 points is wide — this is not a uniformly high-testing population.

**This contextualizes the guidance office's anti-testing stance.** They are likely calibrating advice to the median student (for whom testing may genuinely not help) rather than the top quartile (for whom a 1450+ would be a significant asset at test-optional schools). The problem is that the website presents this as universal philosophy rather than individualized advice — the very thing they claim to offer.

**212 AP exams taken by 90 seniors, 86 scored 3+, 75 scored 4-5.** The profile's formatting is ambiguous: do 86 and 75 refer to exams or students? If exam-level (86/212 = 40.6% scoring 3+), the results are modest. If student-level (86/90 = 95.6% of test-takers scored 3+ on at least one exam, 75/90 = 83.3% scored 4-5 on at least one), the results are strong. The latter interpretation is more likely — school profiles typically report student-level success metrics. Either way, 90 of 108 seniors (83%) chose to take AP exams despite the cap, suggesting strong student engagement with rigorous coursework.

### 23. The Curriculum Is Stronger Than the Guidance Site Suggests

The curriculum guide reveals a school with genuine academic depth:

- **Math goes to Multivariable Calculus and Differential Equations** — this is rare for a school of 650 students. Students who move through the honors track can reach post-AP math by senior year.
- **Three world languages** (Chinese, French, Spanish) each taken through AP and Level 5 Honors — strong depth.
- **English electives are remarkably diverse** for the school's size: African American Literature, Women in Literature, World Literature, Genre Studies, Rhetoric & Composition alongside AP Lang and AP Lit.
- **History offerings are exceptional:** Ethnic Studies, Middle East History and Diplomacy, Theory of Knowledge, Asian American & Pacific Islander Studies, Latin American History, Theories of Social Change, Contemporary Human Geography — this is a curriculum that would be impressive at a school twice PRS's size.
- **SHTEAM trimester electives** (AI, Nanotechnology, Forensics, Industrial Design, Product Design, Entrepreneurship) are creative and distinctive.
- **Arts pathways** go deep: 3 levels of filmmaking, photography through AP 2D Design, graphic design through AP, theater through Honors ensemble, dance through Honors ensemble.

**The gap:** The college guidance site never helps students think strategically about how to present this rich curriculum in applications. Which courses are most distinctive? How do you explain Harkness pedagogy in a "Why Us" essay? How does the interdisciplinary approach translate for admissions readers? The curriculum is a selling point — and the guidance office doesn't sell it.

### 24. Course Rotation Creates Planning Complexity the Guidance Site Ignores

Many history and science electives rotate on a **2-year cycle.** For example, Middle East History and Theories of Social Change are available 2026-27 but not 2027-28, while AAPIS and Contemporary Human Geography are the reverse.

A student can take courses from both pools across junior and senior year — Ethnic Studies one year, AAPIS the next. But with a 3 AP junior cap and required US History, they can only fit 1-2 elective history courses per year, and they can't choose *which year* offers which courses. A student graduating in an odd year never gets access to AAPIS or Theory of Knowledge during their junior/senior years (when these courses are most valuable for applications). Planning requires understanding the rotation schedule 2+ years ahead.

**Neither website provides guidance on strategic course planning.** No "if you're interested in X major, consider this sequence." No "here's how the rotation affects your options." The main site's 11th-grade tab mentions counselors "look ahead to senior course selection" during the junior family meeting — but there's no public-facing framework. For a school with an AP cap and rotating courses, long-range planning is essential.

### 25. No AP Computer Science — A Notable Omission

The curriculum offers Programming 1 & 2 (Java-based) but **no AP Computer Science A or AP Computer Science Principles.** For a school with SHTEAM electives in AI and a Product Design pathway, this is a gap. Students interested in CS apply to college without an AP signal in the discipline. Combined with the AP cap, a student passionate about CS must choose between Programming (no AP weight) and other APs.

The SHTEAM trimester on AI is creative but it's a trimester elective, not a year-long AP course. While strong CS programs value demonstrated interest through any means (personal projects, hackathons, self-study), AP CS A is a standard signal at competitive programs. Students passionate about CS should understand this gap exists and discuss with counselors how to compensate — through Programming 1 & 2, the AI elective, and outside work. The guidance site doesn't address this.

### 26. The 9th Grade Launch Reveals Aspirational Messaging, Not Practical Guidance

The 9th Grade Launch presentation (12/4/25, 51 slides) introduces the college guidance office to incoming families. The CG-related content amounts to:
- Slide showing 4 counselors with ~30 students each
- Slide titled "Junior & Senior Year Programming" (no detail)
- Slide of NACE/FYE competencies (skills list)
- "Parent Portal, collegeguidance@pacificridge.org"

The competency slide lists: *"Be present & prepared, motivated and motivating / Capitalize on strengths, push into areas for growth / Active listening, inquiry, critical thinking / Advocate for yourself & for others"* — these are **career readiness competencies**, not college application guidance. It signals the CG office is thinking about life beyond college, which is philosophically appealing. But for 8th-grade parents in the room trying to understand what their $45K/year buys in college support, it's vapor.

### 27. Grade-Level Projects Are Distinctive — and Underexplained for College Applications

The college profile describes a remarkable 4-year arc:
- **9th:** Orations (persuasive speech to the school community)
- **10th:** Memorial Project (research + creative gallery walk)
- **11th:** Expertise Interview (interview someone in a field of interest, present to school)
- **12th:** Senior Seminar (capstone project reflecting emerging identity)

These are genuinely distinctive co-curriculars that no other school in PRS's competitive set likely replicates. They're built into the curriculum, so every student has them. They develop real skills (public speaking, research, initiative, reflection).

**But the college guidance site never explains how to leverage these in applications.** Where does the Oration go in the activities list? How do you reference Senior Seminar in a personal statement? Does the Expertise Interview connect to a potential major? These projects are ammunition for college applications — and the guidance office doesn't teach students how to use them.

### 28. 39% Students of Color, Rich Identity Curriculum — No Targeted College Guidance

The profile reports 39% students of color, 36 multilingual households, 13 born outside the US, 8 first-generation college students. The curriculum leans into identity and diversity: African American Literature, Ethnic Studies, AAPIS, Women in Literature, Theories of Social Change, Latin American History.

But the college guidance site says nothing about:
- How students of color are specifically supported in the college process
- Fly-in programs and diversity recruitment opportunities at selective colleges
- Scholarship programs targeted at underrepresented students (QuestBridge, Posse, Gates)
- How first-gen students (8 in the class) are supported differently
- How multilingual students can leverage their background

The philosophy statement says the office is "committed to valuing and supporting all identities and experiences." The website provides no evidence of how this commitment manifests in practice.

### 29. Financial Literacy Course Exists But Isn't Connected to College Financing

The curriculum includes a **Financial Literacy** elective covering bank accounts, credit, budgeting, investing, starting a business. The college guidance office runs excellent financial aid programming. These two things exist in parallel with zero visible integration.

A smart institutional move: connect Financial Literacy students to the college financing content. Have them analyze real financial aid packages as a class exercise. Make "understanding your college investment" part of the curriculum, not just a parent night topic. The school has the pieces — they just haven't assembled them.

### 30. The "Final Mastery" Grading System Needs Explanation for Families

PRS uses a **final mastery grade system** — only the year-end grade appears on the transcript and in the GPA. Trimester grades are "grades-in-progress." The midyear report to colleges provides "a snapshot of grades through interim 2."

This is unusual and has implications:
- Students who start poorly but finish strong are rewarded — good pedagogically
- But the midyear report (sent in late January) shows in-progress grades, not final ones — meaning colleges see a less favorable snapshot for students who improve over the year
- How does this interact with EA/ED decisions, which rely on first-trimester grades?
- The college guidance site explains none of this

## Bottom Line

### The Core Tension

PRS has built a genuinely distinctive school — integrated science, Harkness pedagogy, deep humanities, grade-level projects, global travel, SHTEAM innovation, a 27:1 counselor ratio. The in-person programming is stronger than it first appeared: Coastline Case Studies (mock admissions committees with 100+ real officers), Summer Kickoff Days, Camp College, essay workshops in junior English, mock interviews, and robust financial aid education. The curriculum guide reads like a school that knows what it's doing academically. The college profile shows respectable outcomes across a wide range.

**The problem is communication and coherence, not capability.** The school has created a curriculum full of distinctive features — and then doesn't teach families (on either website) how to explain those features to colleges. It has imposed constraints (AP cap, integrated science, rotating courses) that require careful strategic planning — and provides no visible planning tools on either platform. It serves a wide ability range (SAT 1150-1450) — and gives uniform, one-size-fits-all advice on testing. It enrolls first-gen students and students of color — and offers no visible targeted support. Its two websites tell different parts of the story without ever linking to each other.

### The Paradox

The school's philosophy — student-centered, authentic, anti-anxiety — is the right philosophy. The in-person execution appears solid based on the programming described on the main website. But the **public communication** of that execution has tipped into fragmented, inconsistent, and incomplete. Two websites with different content and no cross-links. Matriculation data that differs between the website and the profile PDF. Testing guidance that hasn't kept pace with the landscape. Distinctive programs (Coastline, grade-level projects) that aren't positioned as the selling points they are.

Real student-centeredness means giving families the tools to make informed decisions — and making those tools *findable*. The AP cap, the grading system, the integrated science sequence, the testing landscape — these all have tactical implications that families need to understand. The guidance office may well be providing this in their 27:1 relationship. But the public face doesn't reflect it, and that's a disservice both to prospective families evaluating the program and to current families trying to navigate it.

### What a Parent Should Be Asking

1. **"Can I see where graduates from the last 5 years were accepted and enrolled?"** The data exists on the main website and in the college profile — but they don't match. Ask which is authoritative. Ask for *acceptance* data, not just matriculation — where students got in but chose not to attend tells you about the school's range. Ask for SCOIR scattergrams for your target schools.

2. **"How do you help my student explain Science 9/10 to colleges that expect Biology/Chemistry/Physics?"** The school profile explains it — but does the student know how to narrate it?

3. **"With a 3 AP cap junior year, how should my student prioritize?"** This depends on intended major, and the answer requires guidance the website doesn't provide.

4. **"What's the testing strategy for students who want to apply to schools that have reinstated score requirements?"** The guidance office's public messaging doesn't reflect the 2024-26 shift. Press for individualized, current advice.

5. **"How do you help students who might benefit from a gap year or community college start?"** Multiple PRS graduates enroll at community colleges. Is this planned or unplanned? Supported or stigmatized?

**Quick wins (this week):**
1. Fix the typos and stale links — 2 hours, signals care
2. Reconcile the two matriculation lists (website vs. profile PDF) — they contradict each other, which erodes trust
3. Cross-link the Google Sites CG page and the main website CG section — families shouldn't have to discover they exist separately
