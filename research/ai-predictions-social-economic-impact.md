# Who Best Predicts AI's Social and Economic Impact?

*Research date: 2026-02-24*

## Executive Summary

**Nobody has a verified track record on AI's social/economic impact because it hasn't played out yet.** But three economists have the best frameworks *and* verified track records on previous technology transitions: Acemoglu (automation/labor), Autor (which jobs, how), and Perez (financial/economic cycle). The meta-lesson: the best predictors of AI's societal impact are not AI researchers or tech leaders — they're economists and economic historians who study how *previous* general-purpose technologies transformed society. The mechanism is always the same: technology is the easy part, organizational/institutional adaptation is the bottleneck, and the distribution of gains depends on power dynamics, not technical capability.

---

## Tier 1: Verified on Previous Tech Waves, Most Rigorous on AI

### Daron Acemoglu (MIT, Nobel 2024) — The Single Best Bet

His automation/new-task framework (with Pascual Restrepo) is the most rigorous economic model for how AI affects labor. Core insight: automation displaces workers, but historically also creates *new tasks* that require humans. The ratio determines whether workers win or lose.

His "so-so automation" concept nails *why* some tech creates inequality — automations that replace workers without dramatically improving output (self-checkout, automated phone trees) are the worst case. They cut labor costs without creating enough new value to generate new jobs.

**Verified track record:**
- Correctly predicted that routine-task automation would hollow out middle-skill jobs (confirmed by 2000-2020 US labor data)
- His Narrow Corridor framework (with Robinson) correctly describes how power concentrations trigger institutional responses — tested against centuries of political development data
- His "Power and Progress" (2023) argument — that technology's benefits depend on institutional choices, not technology itself — is the most historically grounded version of this claim

**On AI specifically:** 0.53-0.66% TFP increase over 10 years. The most conservative estimate among serious economists. His methodology is the most transparent — explicit about inputs, assumptions, and what's excluded (scientific discovery applications, which he admits could change the calculus significantly).

**Weakness:** His model inputs go stale fast if capabilities keep leaping. Rigor applied to outdated inputs can produce precise answers to the wrong question. See the [full assessment](ai-future-society-deep-research.md) for details.

**How to use:** Best for understanding the *structure* of AI's economic impact (who wins, who loses, why) rather than the *magnitude* (which depends on capability inputs he can't predict).

### David Autor (MIT) — Best on Labor Markets Specifically

His task framework is *the* standard for analyzing automation's job impact. Rather than asking "will AI replace jobs?" he asks "which tasks within jobs will AI affect, and what happens to the remaining tasks?"

**Verified track record:**
- Predicted labor market polarization (hollowing of middle-skill jobs while high-skill and low-skill grow) before it was visible in the data. Confirmed across multiple countries over two decades.
- His framework correctly identified that automation affects *routine* tasks (both cognitive and manual), not *all* tasks — which is why bookkeepers disappeared but plumbers didn't.

**Novel AI prediction:** AI might actually *reverse* polarization by giving mid-skill workers expert-level capabilities. A nurse with AI diagnostic support becomes more like a doctor; a paralegal with AI research tools becomes more like a lawyer. This is the "AI as great equalizer" thesis — genuinely contrarian among economists and untested.

**Weakness:** The reversal thesis requires that organizations actually restructure to let mid-skill workers take on expanded roles. His own framework shows this kind of organizational adaptation takes decades. The optimistic prediction requires the same slow process he documents elsewhere to happen fast.

**How to use:** Best for predicting *which* jobs AI affects and *how*. Nobody else has his granularity on labor market dynamics.

### Carlota Perez (LSE) — Best on the Financial/Economic Cycle

Her "Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital" (2002) is the single best framework for how technology waves play out economically. She identifies a repeating five-phase pattern across 250 years of tech revolutions:

1. **Irruption** — new technology emerges, early adopters experiment
2. **Frenzy** — financial capital floods in, speculation, bubbles
3. **Turning point** — crash, recession, political/regulatory response
4. **Synergy** — deployment phase, real productivity gains, institutional adaptation
5. **Maturity** — technology fully absorbed, diminishing returns, next revolution gestates

**Verified track record:**
- Her framework *perfectly* described the dot-com pattern: frenzy (1995-2000) → crash (2000-2002) → deployment (2003-2015, when the internet actually transformed commerce, media, social life)
- Also fits railroads (1840s mania → 1847 crash → actual railroad network deployment), electrification (1920s speculation → 1929 crash → postwar deployment), automobiles
- Predicted that each revolution requires not just technology but a new "techno-economic paradigm" — new business models, regulatory frameworks, social norms. This is consistently confirmed.

**Applied to AI:** We're in the frenzy phase. The $400B+ annual AI infrastructure spending vs. $12B consumer revenue gap is textbook installation-phase dynamics. A financial correction is probable within 2-4 years. The technology survives the correction; many companies and investors don't. Real productivity gains come *after* the crash, during the synergy phase, when organizations actually restructure (not just bolt AI onto existing workflows).

**Weakness:** Her framework describes *patterns* but doesn't predict *timing* precisely. The turning point could come in 2027 or 2032. She also doesn't account for the possibility that AI is qualitatively different from previous GPTs because it can recursively improve itself.

**How to use:** Best for understanding *where we are in the cycle* and what comes next structurally. Essential for investment timing and career planning. If Perez is right, the worst time to make irreversible bets on AI is *now* (peak frenzy); the best time is after the correction.

## Tier 2: Important Frameworks, Less Verified

### Erik Brynjolfsson (Stanford) — Right Framework, Conflicted Messenger

His "J-curve" concept (productivity initially *drops* during GPT adoption as organizations invest in restructuring, then rises sharply) is the standard model for tech adoption lags. "The Second Machine Age" (2014, with McAfee) correctly predicted AI would transform cognitive work when that wasn't consensus.

**On AI specifically:** Claims 2.7% US productivity growth in 2025, nearly double the decade average. Argues we're entering the "harvest phase" of the J-curve.

**Problem:** Co-founded Workhelix (AI consulting firm), creating a direct financial interest in bullish AI-productivity narratives. His GDP growth figure may reflect massive AI infrastructure *spending*, not productivity from *using* AI. The NBER survey (Feb 2026) finding 80% of firms report zero AI productivity impact contradicts his harvest-phase claim.

**How to use:** His J-curve framework is sound — adopt it while discounting his specific timing claims. If the J-curve is real, current flat productivity data is *expected* and doesn't falsify eventual gains.

### Carl Benedikt Frey & Michael Osborne — Influential but Flawed

Their 2013 "The Future of Employment" paper (47% of US jobs at risk of automation) shaped the entire public debate about AI and jobs.

**What was right:** The directional insight — AI would affect white-collar cognitive work, not just blue-collar manual work — was genuinely novel and correct. They shifted the discourse from "robots on factory floors" to "algorithms in offices."

**What was wrong:** The 47% number was misleading. They analyzed tasks in isolation, ignoring that (a) most jobs combine automatable and non-automatable tasks, (b) organizational and regulatory barriers prevent automating whole jobs even when tasks are automatable, and (c) automation of some tasks can increase demand for the remaining human tasks. OECD replication using their method but at the task-within-job level got 9%, not 47%.

**How to use:** Treat as a corrective to naive "robots only replace factory workers" thinking. Ignore the specific numbers.

### Rodney Brooks — Best on Deployment Reality

Already covered in [the main forecasters file](ai-predictions-best-forecasters.md). His core value for social/economic prediction: he has been more right about *deployment timelines* than anyone else. When will AI actually change operations on the ground, not just in demos? Brooks' answer: much slower than you think.

His 2018 predictions (no general-purpose home robots by 2030, no AI with common sense by 2030) are looking correct. His core insight — "people overestimate technology in the short run and underestimate it in the long run" — is banal but empirically robust across every tech wave.

**How to use:** Apply his deployment discount to any capability-based economic projection. If a model says "AI can do X," Brooks' framework asks "how long until organizations actually *deploy* AI doing X at scale, reliably, with liability coverage?"

## Tier 3: Social and Political Impact (Speculative, Less Rigorous)

### Shoshana Zuboff — Surveillance Capitalism

"The Age of Surveillance Capitalism" (2019) correctly identified the dynamics of data extraction and behavioral modification before it was mainstream discourse. Her framework (tech companies as extractors of "behavioral surplus") applies directly to AI-powered surveillance, ad-targeting, and manipulation.

**Verified:** Google and Meta's business models evolved exactly as she described. The "behavioral futures market" is now the foundation of the attention economy. Her prediction that this would erode democracy through asymmetric information and manipulation has been partially confirmed (Cambridge Analytica, algorithmic radicalization).

**Limitation:** Her framework was built for pre-generative-AI Big Tech. It captures AI-as-surveillance well but doesn't address AI-as-productivity-tool or AI-as-scientific-instrument.

### Kate Crawford — AI's Material Supply Chain

"Atlas of AI" (2021) correctly predicted that AI's environmental and labor costs (data labeling, content moderation, rare earth mining, energy consumption) would become major issues. The "AI has a supply chain" insight is now mainstream but she was early.

**Verified:** AI energy consumption is now a front-page issue. Data center water usage is controversial. The exploitation of content moderators (especially in Kenya and the Philippines) has been documented extensively. All of these were in Crawford's book before they were in the news.

**Limitation:** More descriptive than predictive. Identifies costs and externalities but doesn't model trajectories or quantify effects.

### Paul David (Stanford) — The Historical Foundation

His "The Dynamo and the Computer" (1990) paper established the intellectual framework that everyone else builds on. His key finding: electrification took 40+ years to produce measurable productivity gains because it required complete factory redesign, not just swapping steam engines for dynamos.

**Verified:** The computing productivity paradox (Solow, 1987-1995) repeated the same pattern — investment surged, productivity didn't move, then eventually leapt when organizations restructured around networked computing.

**Caveat:** Some economic historians contest David's specific timing claims about electrification. The framework is influential but not uncontested. See the [full discussion](ai-future-society-deep-research.md).

## Synthesis: What the Best Predictors Agree On

Despite disagreeing on magnitude and timing, the Tier 1 predictors converge on several structural points:

1. **Organizational restructuring is the bottleneck, not capability.** Acemoglu, Autor, Perez, and David all agree: bolting AI onto existing workflows produces minimal gains. The real productivity comes when organizations redesign around AI — which takes years to decades.

2. **Distribution matters more than aggregate GDP.** Acemoglu's central warning: AI can boost productivity while worsening inequality. Whether workers benefit depends on institutional choices (regulation, labor policy, education), not technology.

3. **The financial cycle will produce a correction.** Perez's framework, reinforced by the $400B spending / $12B revenue gap, the circular financing structures (Nvidia → OpenAI → Microsoft → Nvidia), and S&P 500 concentration at 50-year highs.

4. **AI affects tasks, not whole jobs (initially).** Autor's framework. Most jobs are bundles of tasks; AI automates some while leaving others. This means gradual transformation, not mass overnight unemployment.

5. **Deployment lags capability by years.** Brooks and David's consistent finding. The gap between "AI can do X" and "organizations reliably deploy AI doing X" is measured in years, not months. Regulation, liability, trust, integration, and training all throttle adoption.

**Where they disagree:** Magnitude (Acemoglu: modest; Brynjolfsson: transformative) and whether this time is qualitatively different (all historical frameworks assume AI follows prior GPT patterns; if recursive self-improvement breaks the pattern, all bets are off).
