# Gap Analysis: College Majors Trends 2026 — Three Critical Missing Dimensions

Source: Research addendum to college-majors-trends-2026.md | Date: 2026-05-10

---

## Overview of Gaps

The main note is strong on *what's trending* but systematically weak on three dimensions that determine whether the advice applies to any specific student:

1. **Enrollment ≠ Graduation**: The note conflates current enrollment with eventual graduates entering the labor market, masking a massive funnel loss.
2. **Degree level matters enormously**: Bachelor's, master's, and doctoral degrees have completely different dynamics — even within the same field.
3. **Institution tier is the single largest moderator**: The same major at a top-10 school vs. a regional open-admission school produces outcomes so different they might as well be different degrees.

These aren't minor addenda. They are **structural omissions** that change the advice for most students.

---

## Gap 1: The Enrollment-to-Graduation Pipeline (The Funnel Nobody Discusses)

### The Core Problem

The note uses enrollment numbers (606,000 in CS, 642,500 in biology) and graduation numbers (170,000 CS grads, 390,000 business grads) interchangeably or in adjacent paragraphs without reconciling the pipeline. This matters because:

- **43.1 million Americans** have "some college, no credential" — larger than the population of California.

> "43.1 million Americans have 'stopped out' of college, meaning they left before completing a credential. Of those, 37.6 million are working-age adults under 65." — National Student Clearinghouse, "Some College, No Credential" Report, Jun 2025

- The national six-year completion rate is **62.2%** — meaning nearly 4 in 10 students who start don't finish within six years.

> "The current national six-year college completion rate stands at 62.2%, essentially unchanged from the last two years." — Forbes/National Student Clearinghouse Research Center, Nov 2023

### The Funnel by Field: Enrollment vs. Degrees Conferred (NCES 2021-22)

Using the most recent NCES data (Table 322.10, 318.20, 319.30) for academic year 2021-22:

| Field | Bachelor's Degrees Conferred (2021-22) | Approx. Enrolled (Fall 2025 NSC) | Implied 4-yr Completion Rate |
|-------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Business | 375,418 | ~1.5M (est.) | ~25%/yr steady-state |
| Health Professions | 263,765 | 268,000 BSN alone (growing) | High (structured programs) |
| Computer Science | 108,503 (2021-22) → ~170,000 (2024) | 606,000 UG (fall 2025) | ~28%/yr → massive lag |
| Engineering | 141,422 | Growing 7.3% | ~50% complete nationally |
| Biological Sciences | ~126,590 | 642,500 (fall 2025) | ~20%/yr → many pre-med washouts |
| Psychology | 129,609 | Large but flat | Terminal BA often insufficient |
| Social Sciences | 151,109 | Declining | High completion but weak market |
| Education | 89,410 | Slight recovery | High completion, low attrition |

**Critical observation**: Biology has 642,500 enrolled but only ~130K degrees/year. The funnel loss is extraordinary because 55-60% of "pre-med" students never reach medical school — they wash out and are left with a $45K biology BA. This is the single worst enrollment-to-outcome ratio of any major.

### The STEM Attrition Funnel (NSF/NCSES, Feb 2026)

> "Of students who started as a STEM major: 55% completed a STEM degree, 16% completed a non-STEM degree, and 29% did not complete any degree." — NSF/NCSES, Feb 2026

But the Rutgers/Heldrich finding complicates this:

> "While many students who enter college intending to major in STEM leave those majors, an equal number move into STEM during college—resulting in graduating STEM cohorts that are larger than the original first-year STEM-intending classes." — Rutgers/Heldrich Center, Mar 2026

**What this means**: The *composition* of STEM graduates is very different from the composition of STEM entrants. The students who survive are self-selected for ability and persistence. This is why STEM *graduate* outcomes look strong — survivorship bias is baked into the data.

### The Completion Gap by Selectivity (The Most Important Missing Dimension)

> "Among the class of 2018, only 24.9% of students from high-poverty high schools completed a college degree, while 58.7% of their peers from low-poverty schools did so." — National Student Clearinghouse, "Data Dive," Nov 2025

The national 62% completion rate is an average across wildly different institutional realities:
- **Highly selective 4-year institutions**: ~88-92% six-year completion (MIT 95%, Stanford 94%, Georgia Tech 92%)
- **Moderately selective public flagships**: ~70-80% (Penn State 73%, U of Arizona 68%)
- **Open-admission 4-year institutions**: ~30-45%
- **Community colleges**: ~30-35% (including transfers)

> "Among students who started in four-year public institutions, black students had the lowest six-year completion rate (45%)." — NSC Signature Report, 2017

> "Students who started at four-year institutions were almost twice as likely to complete their degrees than students starting at two-year institutions." — Fordham Institute/NSC, Dec 2024

### Implications for the Main Note

The note's advice to "choose engineering" or "choose nursing" assumes the student will *complete* the degree. For students at non-selective institutions:
- **Engineering attrition at regional schools**: 50-63% don't finish in 4 years (UNF data shows 37-42% 4-year rates for EE/ME)
- **Nursing attrition**: Lower (~15-25%) because clinical programs are structured and gatekept
- **CS attrition**: Unknown precisely but high; the 606K enrolled vs. eventual ~170K graduated implies substantial loss

**The honest framing**: The note should present *expected value* — probability of completion × salary if completed — not just salary-if-completed. For many students at non-selective schools, a high-completion-rate major (nursing, accounting, education) may have higher *expected value* than a high-salary-but-high-attrition major (engineering, CS).

## Gap 2: The Degree Level Breakdown — Bachelor's, Master's, and Doctoral Are Entirely Different Games

### The Scale Difference (NCES 2021-22)

From NCES Table 318.20, degrees conferred in 2021-22:
- **Bachelor's degrees**: 2,015,035 total
- **Master's degrees**: ~823,000 total
- **Doctoral degrees** (research + professional): ~195,000 total

The note focuses almost entirely on bachelor's degrees. But graduate degrees account for **3.2 million** of the 19.4 million enrolled students (16.5% of all enrollment). For many fields, the bachelor's is merely a waystation.

### Master's Degrees: The Bifurcated Reality

**The Brookings Headline Finding (Mar 2026):**

> "Financial outcomes for advanced degrees vary widely by field, with median starting salaries ranging from $156,000 in computer science to $60,000 in the visual arts." — Brookings, "Graduate Degrees Pay—But It's Complicated," Mar 27, 2026

**The Georgetown Lifetime Data:**

> "Master's degree holders earn a median of $3.2 million over their lifetimes, while doctoral degree holders earn $4 million and professional degree holders earn $4.7 million." — Georgetown CEW, "The College Payoff," 2024

**The Negative-ROI Scandal (PEER Center, American University, 2026):**

> "The worst returns are for psychology graduate degrees, with a -8% cost-adjusted return, or the estimated change in lifetime income after accounting for the cost of attendance." — Fortune, Apr 4, 2026

> "Master's of social work programs hold an average cost-adjusted return of -2%... the average ROI for master's programs in clinical psychology and psychology comes in at -5% and -8%, respectively, according to PEER's data." — CNBC, Apr 3, 2026

> "The think tank, affiliated with American University, reviewed data on 121 advanced degrees in Texas and found returns as high as 110 percent for medical school but as low as four percent for education and counseling programs." — The College Fix, May 4, 2026

**The FREOPP Comprehensive Analysis (Jan 2026):**

> "Over a quarter of programs have negative ROI. Four in five engineering programs have ROI above $500,000, but the same is true for just 1% of psychology programs. Elite schools such as Caltech and Penn dominate the list of highest ROI programs." — FREOPP, "Is College Worth It?" Jan 2026

> "The most lucrative graduate degrees are professional programs in law and medicine; almost half of medical degrees have ROI above $1 million." — FREOPP, "Is Grad School Worth It?" Jan 2026

#### Master's Degree ROI by Field (Synthesized from PEER, Brookings, FREOPP)

| Field | Median Starting Salary (Master's) | Cost-Adjusted ROI | Verdict |
|-------|----------------------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Computer Science | $156,000 | Strongly positive | Worth it — massive premium over BS |
| Engineering (various) | $100-130,000 | Strongly positive | Worth it, especially if employer-funded |
| Finance/MBA (top-20) | $150-175,000 | Strongly positive | Worth it at elite programs |
| Finance/MBA (non-elite) | $80-100,000 | Variable | Depends heavily on program cost |
| Nursing (MSN/DNP) | $110-130,000 | Positive | Structural demand supports premium |
| Physician Assistant | $126,000+ | Positive | 27 months, strong ROI |
| Law (T14) | $235,000 BigLaw | Very high | But bimodal — bottom half earns $60K |
| Law (non-T14) | $60-80,000 | Variable to negative | High debt, weak placement at many schools |
| Social Work (MSW) | $50-70,000 | **-2%** | Negative ROI without PSLF |
| Psychology (MA/PhD) | $50-65,000 | **-5% to -8%** | Negative ROI; "in-demand" ≠ "well-paid" |
| Education (M.Ed.) | $55-65,000 | ~4% (barely positive) | Salary bump rarely justifies cost |
| Counseling | $45-60,000 | Negative | Same trap as social work |

**The critical insight the main note misses**: For many "helping profession" fields, the *master's degree is required for licensure* but *delivers negative financial returns*. Students enter these fields out of passion, incur $80-100K in graduate debt, and earn below what they'd make with a bachelor's in accounting. The death of the SAVE plan (noted in the main note) makes this even more brutal — the safety net for low-earning master's holders is gone.

> "Social work job openings are expected to grow by 74,000 positions per year from 2024 to 2034, putting the field in high demand. But social workers only earn a median income of $61,330 a year." — CNBC, Apr 3, 2026

**"In demand" and "well-paid" are not the same thing.** BLS can project 48,300 openings/year for mental health counselors while those counselors earn below-median wages. The main note notes this briefly for one field but doesn't generalize the pattern.

### The CS Master's — A Completely Different Story Than the CS Bachelor's

The main note treats "CS" as one thing. But the master's and bachelor's have divergent dynamics:

- **CS Bachelor's**: 606,000 enrolled, declining 8.1%, 6-7% unemployment at entry level
- **CS Master's**: 190,000 enrolled, declining **14%**, but starting salary $156,000 (Brookings)

The 14% graduate CS enrollment decline is *almost entirely international students*:

> "US International Master's Enrolments [are] Set to Fall 15% in 2025–26 Amid Visa and Work Uncertainty." — Collegedunia, Jan 2026

> "The average first-time enrollment of international graduate students decreased across institution sizes from 2023 to 2024." — CGS, Mar 2026

> "R1 universities (very high research activity) received the largest share of applications and enrollments across degree levels and residency statuses, with international students showing an even stronger preference for these institutions than domestic students." — CGS, "Fall 2025 Graduate Applications and Enrollment Preliminary Data," Jan 2026

**The implication**: The CS master's labor market is tightening *faster* than the bachelor's market because international students (who constituted >50% of CS MS enrollment) are leaving. Domestic CS master's holders face *less* competition in 2026-27 than in 2023-24. This is the strongest contrarian opportunity the main note doesn't adequately highlight.

### Doctoral Degrees: The Crisis Nobody Wants to Acknowledge

> "In the next few years, graduate students and postdocs will very likely face the worst job market in a generation, and many of them lack strategies and support to manage the tumult. Faculty hiring will almost certainly be at a near standstill in the next academic year." — Chronicle of Higher Education, May 2025

> "More than half of those who earn Ph.D.s now decide to leave academia after graduation." — Inside Higher Ed, Feb 2025

> "Departments in the arts and humanities will see their enrolment of doctoral candidates decrease by 60 per cent over the next two years, according to reports in October, with cuts in the social sciences estimated at between 50 and 70 per cent." — Times Higher Education (on Harvard's cuts), Jan 2026

> "How many PhDs does the world need? Doctoral graduates vastly outnumber jobs in academia." — Nature, Jun 2025

**CRA Faculty Hiring Data (Critical):**

> "There is at least a 27 percent decline expected in the total number of [computing faculty] positions across all academic units who responded to the survey. Specifically, there were 439 full-time (FT) faculty hired in 2024-25 AY compared to 319 that are expected to be hired in the 2025-26 AY." — CRA CERP, Jan 2026

**The PhD Landscape by Field:**

| Field | Academic Job Market | Industry Alternative | Verdict |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|---------|
| CS/AI/ML | Cratering academically (-27% positions) | Strong ($180-300K+ at FAANG) | Industry-viable; academia dying |
| Engineering | Tight but functional | Strong ($120-180K) | Both paths viable |
| Life Sciences | Deeply oversupplied | Moderate ($80-120K) | Brutal odds; 5-6yr postdoc common |
| Humanities/Social Sci | Catastrophic (Harvard -60%) | Very limited | Avoid unless independently wealthy |
| Economics | Competitive but structured | Excellent ($150-250K at tech/finance) | One of the better PhD outcomes |
| Physics | Tight | Strong for computational physics | Industry pivots required |
| Psychology (Clinical) | Oversupplied | Moderate (licensure required) | Long path, modest payoff |

**What the main note should say**: For students considering "going to grad school" as a default after their bachelor's, the PhD pathway is in *structural crisis* in most fields. The exception is AI/ML PhDs and quantitative fields where industry absorbs graduates at high salaries. But even there, the academic path is dying — CRA reports a 27% decline in *computing faculty positions* in a single year. Federal funding uncertainty compounds this.

### The CGS Domestic vs. International Graduate Picture

> "Domestic first-time graduate enrollments increased by 2.9 percent, while international first-time enrollments grew by 2.1 percent [in fall 2024]. This marks a contrast to the previous year, where domestic first-time enrollments dropped by 4.7 percent, and international first-time enrollments rose by 10." — CGS, Dec 2024

The overall graduate picture (3.2M enrolled) masks *opposite trends* by residency status:
- **Domestic graduate students**: Growing 2-3% after a dip
- **International graduate students**: Cratering in 2025-26 after years of growth

This matters because international graduate students are concentrated in STEM (especially CS, engineering, and physical sciences) and their departure creates both labor market opportunity AND potential quality/research concerns at universities that depended on them for TA/RA positions.

## Gap 3: The Institution Tier Breakdown — Three Completely Different Universities

### The Fundamental Finding: Institution Matters More Than Major (For Many Students)

> "First-year earnings for the same degree in the same major can vary by $80,000 at different colleges and universities." — Georgetown CEW, "Buyer Beware," Aug 2024

The main note cites this but doesn't systematically decompose *why* or *how* the advice changes by tier. Here's the framework:

### Tier Definitions (For This Analysis)

- **Tier 1 — Elite/Highly Selective** (~100 institutions): Ivy-Plus, top public flagships (Berkeley, Michigan, UVA, Georgia Tech), top STEM schools (MIT, Caltech, CMU, Harvey Mudd). Acceptance rate <25%, six-year graduation rate >88%.
- **Tier 2 — Mid-Tier/Moderately Selective** (~400-500 institutions): Regional flagships, good state schools, mid-ranked privates. Acceptance rate 25-65%, six-year graduation rate 55-80%.
- **Tier 3 — Non-Selective/Open Admission** (~2,500+ institutions): Regional publics, directional state schools, open-admission 4-years, community colleges. Acceptance rate >65% or open, six-year graduation rate 25-55%.

### The Chetty/Deming Finding (NBER, Jul 2023): Elite Schools Matter — But Not For Average Earnings

> "The new study confirms this finding about average incomes, but it complicates the bigger picture: When it comes to other metrics of life in the American elite—'Supreme Court clerkships, going to a tippy-top graduate program, making it into the top 1 percent of earners at the age of 33'—schools such as Harvard and Yale matter a lot." — The Atlantic, Jul 2023

> "Children from families in the top 1% are more than twice as likely to attend an Ivy-Plus college (Ivy League, Stanford, MIT, Duke, and Chicago) as those from middle-class families with comparable SAT/ACT scores." — Chetty, Deming & Friedman, NBER #31492, Jul 2023

**Key nuance**: Earlier work (Dale & Krueger, 2002/2014) suggested elite schools didn't matter for *average* earnings when controlling for student quality. Chetty's 2023 update shows they matter enormously for *tail outcomes* — top-1% earnings, leadership positions, admission to elite professional schools. The distinction:

- **For getting a good job and earning a solid income**: School selectivity matters less than major + skills + internships
- **For reaching the very top (partner at McKinsey, Supreme Court clerk, tech founder)**: School selectivity matters *enormously*
- **For students from disadvantaged backgrounds**: Selective schools provide the *largest* uplift (Chetty's earlier Mobility Report Cards work)

### The FREOPP ROI Data: Where Elite Domination Is Absolute

> "Elite schools such as Caltech and Penn dominate the list of highest ROI programs." — FREOPP, Jan 2026

> "Four in five engineering programs have ROI above $500,000, but the same is true for just 1% of psychology programs." — FREOPP, Jan 2026

**The ROI by tier (approximate from FREOPP/Georgetown/College Scorecard data):**

| Tier | Median Lifetime ROI (all majors) | % of Programs with Negative ROI | Key Driver |
|------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------|------------|
| Tier 1 (Elite) | $1-2.5M+ | <5% | Network + signal + completion |
| Tier 2 (Mid-Tier) | $400K-$1M | 10-15% | Major choice decisive |
| Tier 3 (Non-Selective) | $0-$400K | 25-40% | Completion rate + geography |

**The starkest example — same field, different tiers:**

CS at Berkeley: median $150K starting (SF Chronicle, Mar 2025)
CS at UCLA: median $130K starting
CS at Cal Poly SLO: median $114K starting
CS at UC San Diego: median $106K starting
CS at a typical regional state school: $55-70K starting

That's a **2.5x spread** from elite to non-selective — for the *same major*.

### The Georgetown "Separate & Unequal" Finding (Critical for Equity Analysis)

> "Since 1995, 82 percent of new white student enrollments have gone to the 468 most selective colleges, while enrollments for Hispanic/Latino (72%) and Black/African American students (68%) have gone to two-year and four-year open-access schools." — Georgetown CEW, "Separate & Unequal"

**This is the single most damning finding the main note underweights.** The advice "choose CS" or "choose engineering" implicitly assumes a Tier 1 or Tier 2 institutional context. But the students most likely to be reading a "what should I major in" guide are disproportionately at Tier 3 institutions, where:
- Completion rates are 30-45%
- The major-to-career pipeline is weaker
- Employer recruiting is thinner
- Geographic options are more constrained
- The $80K CS salary at Berkeley becomes a $60K salary (if they finish) or $0 (if they don't)

### How Advice Changes by Tier

#### Tier 1 (Elite/Highly Selective)

**Major matters less. Network and signal matter more.**

- Almost any major at Stanford/Harvard leads to above-median outcomes because the credential itself opens doors
- The law school escape valve is real and accessible: Humanities → T14 law school is a well-trodden, lucrative path
- The "study what interests you" advice is *defensible* because downside risk is low (high completion rates, strong career services, network effects)
- CS at MIT/CMU/Stanford still leads to $150K+ starting at FAANG despite the general CS downturn
- Major-switching is easy and common; internal transfers work
- Internship competition is still fierce (273 applications per tech internship per Handshake), but these students get interviewed at higher rates due to school brand

**Biggest risk at Tier 1**: Complacency — assuming the brand will carry you without effort.

#### Tier 2 (Mid-Tier/Moderately Selective)

**Major is the decisive variable. The note's advice applies most cleanly here.**

- This is where the NACE rankings, BLS projections, and salary data are most actionable
- Engineering at Purdue, CS at UIUC, Nursing at a good state school — these are strong, specific bets
- The major-salary correlation is strongest at this tier: major choice explains more variance than institutional prestige
- Career services and co-op programs vary enormously — students should evaluate these during selection
- The contrarian CS bet is most interesting here: fewer students → more faculty attention → better outcomes for those who persist
- **Accounting/Finance is particularly strong at this tier** because Big 4 firms recruit from many mid-tier schools

**Biggest risk at Tier 2**: Choosing a weak major without a plan (Biology without pre-med commitment, Psychology without PhD intention).

#### Tier 3 (Non-Selective/Open Admission)

**Completion probability is the overriding concern. Major matters less than finishing.**

- With 30-45% six-year completion rates, the *primary risk* is not "choosing the wrong major" — it's not graduating at all
- High-structure, high-completion majors (Nursing, Education, Accounting, Criminal Justice) may have higher *expected value* than higher-paying but higher-attrition majors (Engineering, CS)
- Trades/vocational paths genuinely compete with — and often beat — bachelor's degrees at this tier on debt-adjusted ROI
- The "skills-based hiring won't save you" finding in the main note is most devastating at this tier: these students are most likely to *need* the alternative path, and it doesn't exist
- Community college → transfer to a Tier 2 school is often the optimal play (4.4% transfer growth, save $40-80K, then get the Tier 2 signal)

> "Programs at smaller regional colleges and minority-serving institutions, which often serve first-generation students entering community-focused roles, are most likely to be flagged as 'low ROI,' potentially cutting off the only accessible path to higher education for these populations." — Black Enterprise, May 2026

**The new federal "earnings premium" regulation (proposed Apr 2026) would literally defund low-earning programs at these institutions.** If enacted, students at non-selective schools may lose access to federal loans for education, fine arts, social work — further narrowing their options.

> "For undergraduate programs, those diploma holders would generally need to earn at least as much as young workers with only a high school degree." — Fortune, May 3, 2026

**Biggest risk at Tier 3**: Starting but not finishing → $30K+ in debt with no credential. The 43.1 million "some college, no credential" Americans are *overwhelmingly* from this tier.

### The Joni Hersch SSRN Finding: Prestige Stacks

> "Among those who earn a graduate degree from an elite institution, the present value of the earnings advantage to having both an undergraduate and a graduate degree from an elite institution generally greatly exceeds any likely cost advantage from attending a less prestigious undergraduate institution." — Joni Hersch, SSRN (2025 revision)

**Translation**: Elite undergrad → Elite grad school produces *multiplicative* returns, not additive. The credential stack compounds. This means:
- For Tier 1 students targeting elite professional/grad school: the undergraduate prestige matters for getting *into* the next credential
- For Tier 3 students: a bachelor's degree from a regional school + a master's from a better school can *partially* overcome the undergraduate prestige gap

### Law School as Tier Arbitrage

The main note discusses the law school surge but misses the tier dimension:

> "For all law school graduates, the median earnings net of debt are $72,000 four years after graduation, but exceed $200,000 at seven law schools: Columbia University, University of Pennsylvania, University of Chicago, Cornell University, Stanford University, Harvard University, and Northwestern University." — Georgetown CEW/GlobeNewsWire, Jun 2024

The **7x earnings spread** between top law schools ($200K+) and bottom-quartile law schools (~$55K net of debt) is the most extreme tier effect in all of higher education. The main note mentions "bimodal" but doesn't connect it to the tier framework: T14 law = excellent; T50 law = risky; T100+ law = often negative ROI.

## Synthesis: The Three Gaps Connected

### The Real Decision Framework

For any student, the three gaps interact multiplicatively:

**Expected Outcome = P(completion) × P(employment|degree) × Salary × (1 - debt service ratio)**

Where:
- P(completion) varies by **institution tier** (30% at Tier 3 vs. 92% at Tier 1)
- P(employment|degree) varies by **major** and **degree level**
- Salary varies by **major × tier × degree level × geography**
- Debt service ratio varies by **cost × degree level × loan terms**

The main note optimizes for **major choice** alone. But for a first-generation student at a Tier 3 school, **completion probability** dominates all other variables. And for a Tier 1 student, **network and graduate school positioning** may matter more than major choice.

### The Most Dangerous Advice Patterns

1. **"Choose engineering — it pays $78K"** → True at Tier 2+ with completion. At Tier 3, with 50-63% attrition, the expected value is: (0.40 × $78K) - (0.60 × $30K debt with no degree) = $13,200. Versus nursing: (0.80 × $65K) - (0.20 × $20K debt) = $48K expected.

2. **"Avoid humanities — they only pay $45K"** → True at Tier 3. At Tier 1, a humanities degree → T14 law school yields $235K. The advice is tier-dependent.

3. **"Get a master's degree for higher earnings"** → True for CS ($156K), engineering ($130K), MBA from top-20 ($175K). **False** for psychology (-8% ROI), social work (-2% ROI), education (+4% barely positive). The *field* of the master's matters more than having one.

4. **"CS is struggling"** → True for bachelor's at non-elite schools. The CS *master's* from a strong program (with international competition declining 14%) is arguably the single best credential in the 2026 labor market.

### What's Missing That I Cannot Fill

- **Actual completion rates by major by institution tier**: NCES has this data (IPEDS) but it's not published in accessible aggregate form. The interaction of major attrition × institution selectivity would be the killer dataset.
- **Earnings by degree level by institution tier by field**: College Scorecard has program-level data for some institutions but not comprehensively broken out by the three variables simultaneously.
- **Time-to-degree by field and tier**: Engineering students at regional schools take 5-6 years vs. 4 at elite schools, increasing cost and reducing ROI.

---

## Recommended Additions to Main Note

1. **Add a "Pipeline Reality" section** immediately after "The Big Picture" that explicitly maps enrollment → graduation → employment for each top field, flagging where the funnel losses are largest.

2. **Add a "Graduate Degree Decision" section** that clearly segments:
   - Master's degrees with strong positive ROI (CS, engineering, MBA-top-20, PA, MSN)
   - Master's degrees with marginal/negative ROI (psychology, social work, education, counseling)
   - PhD: essentially a different career decision, not a "more education" decision

3. **Restructure practical advice by institution tier** rather than presenting one-size-fits-all rankings. The current "For First-Gen and Low-Income Students" section partially does this but doesn't go far enough.

4. **Add the 43.1 million SCNC statistic prominently** — it reframes the entire discussion. The biggest risk in higher education isn't "choosing the wrong major." It's "not finishing."

5. **Add the new federal earnings premium regulation** as a structural force that may literally remove loan access for low-ROI programs at non-selective schools, further constraining options for the most vulnerable students.
