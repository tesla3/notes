## Executive Summary

The three-document research set is **exceptionally strong** — well-sourced (45+ primary sources), properly skeptical, and avoids listicle traps. After independent verification against primary sources and additional research, I find:

- **Core claims verified**: All major factual claims check out against primary sources

## I. Verified Core Claims (With Source Confirmation)

### CS Enrollment Decline: 8.1% — CONFIRMED

> "Undergraduate enrollment at four-year institutions fell by 8.1% to about 606,000 students. Graduate enrollment dropped sharply by 14.0%, with about 190,000 students."
> — National Student Clearinghouse, "The Changing Landscape of Postsecondary Education," Feb 10, 2026
> https://www.studentclearinghouse.org/nscblog/changing-landscape-of-postsecondary-education/

The "6% system-wide" (TechCrunch) vs "8.1%" (Clearinghouse) discrepancy is scope difference: 6% includes community colleges (smaller decline); 8.1% is four-year institutions only. The note correctly leads with the primary-source 8.1% figure.

### AI Employment Effect: 16% Decline — CONFIRMED (with nuance)

> "We find that since the widespread adoption of generative AI, early-career workers (ages 22-25) in the most AI-exposed occupations have experienced a 16 percent relative decline in employment even after controlling for firm-level shocks."
> — Stanford Digital Economy Lab, "Canaries in the Coal Mine?" Apr 28, 2026
> https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/publication/canaries-in-the-coal-mine-six-facts-about-the-recent-employment-effects-of-artificial-intelligence/

**Important nuance**: The Aug 2025 working paper reported 13%; the Apr 2026 published version reports 16% (updated with more recent payroll data). Some outlets (Fortune Apr 30) still cite 13%. The correct current figure is **16%**. Additionally:

> "Declines are strongest where AI automates tasks; where AI augments workers, entry-level employment holds up or grows. Adjustments are happening through headcount, not pay—wages are stickier."
> — datasciencebyexample.com (summarizing Stanford findings), Aug 29, 2025

## II. Critical Gaps Not Addressed in the Research Set

### GAP 1: The 2026 Iran War — A Massive Unaddressed Macro Shock

The notes were written through May 10, 2026 but **almost completely ignore** the US-Iran war that started in early March 2026. This is the single biggest macroeconomic event affecting the Class of 2026's immediate job prospects.

**What happened**:
- US military conflict with Iran began ~early March 2026
- Strait of Hormuz closure disrupted global oil supply
- Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, UAE oil infrastructure attacked

> "In the eight weeks since the Iran war started, the conflict has driven gas prices above $4 a gallon, strained homebuyers and pushed inflation to its highest level in nearly two years. Even if the war ends soon, Americans are likely to feel the financial sting for months."
> — CBS News, Apr 27, 2026
> https://www.cbsnews.com/news/iran-war-economic-impact-gas-prices-inflation-2026/

> "The World Trade Organization (WTO) said yesterday that if oil and gas prices remain high for the rest of the year, it could reduce the forecasted 2026 growth in global GDP by 0.3 percent."
> — Council on Foreign Relations, Mar 20, 2026
> https://www.cfr.org/articles/the-iran-wars-global-economic-impact

> "A conflict that was supposed to be brief has sent oil prices soaring and raised the risk of a worldwide recession."
> — The New Yorker, Mar 23, 2026

**Why this matters for major selection**:
1. Rising inflation + gas costs disproportionately hurt commuter students and those at non-residential schools
2. Oil price shock strengthens the case for energy/engineering but risks recession
3. Defense sector hiring likely accelerates (benefiting EE, ME, aerospace, cybersecurity)
4. The "tentative ceasefire" makes near-term uncertainty extreme
5. **The job market was *starting* to thaw** (JOLTS Mar 2026 shows hires jumping to 3.5%) — but the war may stall that:

> "Is the hiring recession finally over? There are encouraging signs. The big concern is the war in Iran could halt that much needed progress in the labor market."
> — Heather Long, chief economist, Navy Federal Credit Union (via CNBC, May 5, 2026)

### GAP 2: The Full Severity of the Underemployment Crisis

The notes mention underemployment in passing but **dramatically understate** the headline numbers:

> "42.5% of recent college graduates (aged 22 to 27 with a bachelor's degree or higher) are underemployed as of December 2025 — the highest rate since October 2020."
> — CNBC (citing NY Fed), Mar 11, 2026
> https://www.cnbc.com/select/underemployed-college-grads-finance-tips/

> "In 2025, the share of unemployed Americans who are new workforce entrants hit a 37-year high, peaking at 13.3% in July before settling at 10.6% this February. That is still higher than at any point during the Great Recession."
> — Fortune, Mar 21, 2026
> https://fortune.com/2026/03/21/entry-level-jobs-gen-z-not-their-fault/

> "Only 19% of graduates now say it's a good time to find a quality job — down from over 70% in 2022."
> — Kelly Services, Apr 2026

> "College students expect to make $80,000 on average one year after graduation. The average salary for new grads is about 30% lower."
> — CNBC, May 4, 2026

The **5.7% recent grad unemployment rate** (Q4 2025, NY Fed) is 36% higher than the overall unemployment rate (4.2%) and the **42.5% underemployment** means nearly half of degree holders work jobs that don't require their degree. This reframes the entire note's premise: major choice matters, but 42.5% of graduates end up underemployed *regardless* of major. The *institution + internship + timing* variables explain more of the variance than the note acknowledges.

### GAP 3: The White-Collar vs. Blue-Collar Divergence in the "Recovery"

The May 2026 jobs data reveals a critical structural split that the notes miss:

> "The job market is healing for everyone—except in the office."
> — Fortune, May 8, 2026
> https://fortune.com/2026/05/08/jobs-report-april-2026-ai-white-collar-layoffs-finance-wages/

> "After a 2025 where monthly job growth averaged an anemic 10,000, the 2026 average is now 76,000 — enough of an improvement that economists are starting to ask whether the 'hiring recession' of the past two years is finally ending."
> — Fortune, May 8, 2026

BUT:

> "Finance and information services — industries that once provided an on-ramp for the lion's share of recent college graduates — are hemorrhaging jobs, shedding an average of 9,000 jobs per month since 2023. Before the pandemic, those same industries were adding 44,000 jobs per month."
> — Fortune, Mar 21, 2026

**Translation**: The job market IS recovering — but selectively. Healthcare + construction + services are hiring. Finance + tech + information services are still contracting. This directly contradicts the NACE finding that "Finance" is the #1 most in-demand major (61.3% of firms hiring). Resolution: NACE surveys *large* firms that survived and are growing; the -9K/month figure includes all firm sizes including ones that shut down or shrank. Both are true simultaneously but describe different labor markets.

**Implication for students**: The "safe" white-collar path (business → finance → office job) that worked for decades is structurally impaired. The recovery is in healthcare, trades, and services — fields requiring physical presence and human interaction.

## III. The NACE vs. Handshake Contradiction — Now Partially Resolved

The notes correctly identify this as the biggest unresolved tension. Here's the updated picture as of May 2026:

| Signal | Source | Finding |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Optimistic | NACE (Apr 2026) | +5.6% hiring intent |
| Pessimistic | Handshake (Jan 2026) | -15% to -16% postings YoY |

| Pessimistic | Fortune (May 8) | White-collar still shedding; recovery is blue-collar/healthcare |

**Resolution**: The "hiring recession" appears to be *ending in aggregate* but the recovery is **sector-specific**. Large firms (NACE's sample) are growing. Small/mid firms and government (Handshake's universe) are still contracting. The net for college graduates specifically:

- Healthcare majors: strong and improving
- Engineering (physical): improving with defense spending
- Finance/Accounting: large firms hiring; sector overall still negative
- CS/Tech: still compressing at entry level despite overall software market growth
- Humanities/Social Sciences: continued pain

## IV. The Stanford Study: Precision on the 13% vs 16% Figure

The note cites "16%" which is correct for the *current published version* (Apr 28, 2026). However, some citing outlets use 13% (the Aug 2025 working paper figure). For maximum accuracy:

- **Aug 2025 working paper** (initial release): 13% relative decline
- **Apr 28, 2026 published version** (same authors, updated data): 16% relative decline

> "Young workers in 'AI-exposed' roles have seen a 16% relative decline in employment, with entry-level hiring slowing most severely for roles where AI automates rather than augments."
> — Stanford Accelerator for Learning, Apr 29, 2026
> https://acceleratelearning.stanford.edu/story/catalyzing-early-career-potential-in-the-ai-era/

The increase from 13% to 16% over ~8 months of additional data suggests the effect is **accelerating, not stabilizing**. This strengthens the note's warning about AI exposure.

## V. Revised Assessment: What the Evidence Actually Supports (May 2026)

### Things the Notes Get RIGHT

1. **CS is a contrarian bet, not dead**: 8.1% enrollment decline + 14% grad decline + 317,700 annual BLS openings = tightening supply. Morgan Stanley's Jevons Paradox thesis ($24B→$61B market) provides demand-side support. But the *timing* is uncertain — could be 3 years or 7 years before supply-demand rebalances.

2. **Healthcare is the structural winner**: BLS projects +8.4% sector growth. NP at +40%. 681K home health aide positions. Supply constraints (80K rejected nursing applicants, faculty shortages) keep wages rising.

3. **Accounting is genuinely a hidden gem**: 300K lost since 2020, 150-hour rule dying across states, 26% minority entrant suppression being removed. $72K starting. Low attrition. Geographic flexibility.

4. **Skills-based hiring is vaporware**: 0.14% impact confirmed across multiple sources through May 2026. The degree remains the filter.

5. **The entry-level market is brutal regardless of major**: -15% postings, +26% applications per role, 42.5% underemployment, 37-year-high new-entrant unemployment share. This is the *context* within which any major choice operates.

### Things the Notes Get WRONG or Overstate

1. **"77% of 2025 grads found jobs within 3 months"** (cited from CNBC, Apr 2026) — This conflicts with the NY Fed's 5.7% unemployment + 42.5% underemployment data. Resolution: "found jobs" ≠ "found appropriate jobs." Many of that 77% are underemployed. The 77% figure masks the quality problem.

2. **The NACE optimism is likely overstated for the median graduate**: NACE surveys surviving, growth-oriented large employers. The median new grad's experience is better captured by Handshake (broad market) and the 42.5% underemployment figure. NACE's optimism applies to students from target schools with internship experience — not to the median grad.

3. **The "recovery" framing needs qualification**: The job market IS improving (JOLTS Mar 2026: 3.5% hires rate, BLS Apr 2026: +115K jobs). But the recovery is concentrated in healthcare, construction, services — NOT in finance and tech, where most college grads historically entered. The Iran war adds uncertainty.

4. **Engineering "surge" needs the defense-spending caveat**: Part of the 7.3% engineering enrollment growth maps to defense demand (war spending). This creates jobs but also creates political/moral considerations and potentially unsustainable demand if conflict ends.

### Things Genuinely UNCERTAIN (Honest Unknowns)

1. **Whether AI displacement accelerates or plateaus**: Stanford shows 16% decline (accelerating from 13%). But Morgan Stanley shows 20% CAGR in software spending. Both are real. Net effect on *entry-level hiring specifically* in 2029-2031 is genuinely unknowable.

2. **Iran war duration and economic consequences**: A quick end → inflation fades → hiring continues thawing. Prolonged conflict → recession risk → all bets off for the Class of 2026/2027.

3. **Whether the CPA pathway reform actually *increases* entrants**: States are removing the 150-hour barrier, but accounting's reputation problem (perceived as boring, high burnout) may limit the supply response. Watch 2026-2027 enrollment data.

4. **Whether the CS enrollment decline is a leading indicator or lagging**: If it's sentiment-driven panic (like 2001-2007), it'll reverse within 3-5 years. If it's structural (AI genuinely reduces junior dev need permanently), the trough could last a decade. No one knows.

## VI. The Single Most Important Finding Not in the Notes

> "Finance and information services — industries that once provided an on-ramp for the lion's share of recent college graduates — are hemorrhaging jobs, shedding an average of 9,000 jobs per month since 2023. Before the pandemic, those same industries were adding 44,000 jobs per month."
> — Fortune, Mar 21, 2026

This is a **53,000 jobs/month swing** in the two sectors that historically absorbed the most college graduates. It explains everything:
- Why 42.5% are underemployed despite choosing "good" majors
- Why even finance/accounting students (NACE #1 and #4 in demand) face competition
- Why "the job market is recovering" but not for office workers
- Why the trades narrative has substance (construction/healthcare growing while offices shrink)

The structural question for anyone advising a high schooler: **Will finance and tech hiring return to pre-pandemic levels by 2030?** If yes, the "choose CS/finance" advice holds. If no — if this is a permanent structural shift toward leaner white-collar operations — then the advice needs fundamental revision toward healthcare, physical engineering, and trades.

## VII. Updated Recommendations (Changes to Main Note)

### Must-Add Content

1. **Iran War section** — Cannot publish advice about 2026 without acknowledging the biggest macro shock. Even if temporary, it affects the Class of 2026's immediate outcomes and potentially Class of 2027-2028's through recession risk.

2. **42.5% underemployment headline** — Should appear in the first page. This is the *baseline reality* against which all advice operates. Nearly half of degree holders are underemployed regardless of major.

3. **White-collar structural compression** — The -9K/month in finance and information services, vs. +44K/month pre-pandemic. This is a -53K/month swing. It's the most important single labor market fact for college-bound students.

4. **CPA reform acceleration** — Kansas, Kentucky, Maryland, West Virginia, Nebraska, Rhode Island, Louisiana all reforming in 2026 alone. The 150-hour rule is functionally dead as a national standard. This *lowers barriers* and may attract more entrants — the shortage may *partially* self-correct faster than nursing (which has physical infrastructure constraints).

5. **Salary expectation gap** — Students expect $80K; reality is ~$56K. A 30% expectation gap. This matters for financial planning (loan repayment assumptions).

### Must-Fix Claims

1. **Stanford figure**: Use 16% (Apr 2026 published version), not 13%. Cite the publication date explicitly.

2. **"77% found jobs"**: Qualify immediately with "but 42.5% were underemployed" — the 77% figure alone is misleading without quality context.

3. **NACE optimism**: Frame as "large-employer intent" not "market reality." The median grad's experience is captured by Handshake + NY Fed, not NACE.

### Structural Improvements

1. **Lead with the brutal truth**: The entry-level market is at a 37-year worst for new entrants. Major choice matters at the margins, but the macro environment dominates all individual choices right now.

2. **Explicitly separate "intent to recover" from "actual recovery"**: NACE says +5.6%; JOLTS shows actual hiring improved in March; but white-collar is still compressing. These are different stories.

3. **Add temporal dimension**: The notes advise for freshmen entering 2026 and graduating ~2030. But the *immediate* job market (for the Class of 2026 graduating now) is materially worse than what freshmen will face in 4 years. Separate the advice for "choosing a major now" from "graduating now."

## X. Bottom Line

The research set is **publication-quality** with three material gaps:

1. **Add the Iran War** — it's the elephant in the room for 2026 graduates
2. **Lead with 42.5% underemployment and 37-year-high new-entrant unemployment** — this is the context for all advice
3. **Name the white-collar structural compression explicitly** — finance and tech shedding 9K jobs/month (vs. +44K pre-pandemic) is *the* structural fact that explains why "good majors" still produce bad outcomes

The underlying analytical framework (supply-demand dynamics, institution-major-internship bundle, AI displacement vs. augmentation) is sound. The gaps are contextual/macro rather than analytical.
