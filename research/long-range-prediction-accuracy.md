# Who Are the Most Accurate Long-Range (10+ Year) Predictors?

*Research date: 2026-02-24*

## Executive Summary

**The honest answer is: almost nobody has a rigorously verified track record for 10+ year predictions.** The field is plagued by vague predictions, survivorship bias, and absence of systematic scoring. That said, the evidence points to a clear hierarchy, and the *methods* that work are well-established even if the *individuals* are hard to crown.

**Best-documented accurate long-range predictors:**
1. **IPCC / Climate modelers** — The gold standard for 30+ year verified quantitative predictions
2. **Domain experts with deep mechanistic understanding** — Bill Gates/MS leadership (1990s tech), John Sawyer (1973 climate)
3. **Tetlock's "foxes" / Superforecasters** — Best methodology, but mostly validated at 1–5 year horizons
4. **Samotsvety** — Elite forecasting group, top of every platform they compete on
5. **Isaac Asimov** — ~50% accuracy on 50-year technology predictions (surprisingly good)
6. **Bryan Caplan** — 23/23 on bets, but deliberately picks safe sides

**Worst-documented despite fame:**
- Ray Kurzweil (~7% accuracy per Dan Luu's rigorous scoring)
- Michio Kaku (~3-6%)
- Nearly all professional "futurists" from Wikipedia's list

## The Core Problem: Long-Range Forecasting Is Barely Studied

Open Philanthropy's Luke Muehlhauser (2019) conducted the most serious meta-analysis of long-range forecasting and concluded:

> "My key finding so far is that it is difficult to learn much of value from [past long-range forecasting] exercises."

Seven structural problems plague the field:
1. **Imprecise statements** — Most predictions are too vague to score
2. **Uncheckable** — Even precise predictions often can't be verified
3. **No quantified confidence** — Rarely do forecasters state probabilities
4. **No baseline comparison** — Was the prediction better than naive extrapolation?
5. **Weak incentives** — Nobody pays a cost for being wrong
6. **No causal identification** — Can't tell what made someone accurate
7. **Unclear relevance** — Past exercises may not generalize

This means the question "who is the most accurate long-range predictor?" is fundamentally harder to answer than it appears. Most claims about great predictors are cherry-picked anecdotes.

## Tier 1: Verified Quantitative Track Records (10+ Years)

### IPCC / Climate Scientists

**The single best example of accurate 30+ year predictions in any domain.**

- **John Sawyer (1973):** Predicted 0.6°C warming by 2000. Actual: 0.51–0.56°C. A 27-year prediction within 0.1°C.
- **James Hansen (1988):** His "Scenario B" (moderate emissions) has tracked observed warming closely for 35+ years.
- **IPCC First Assessment Report (1990):** Predicted warming greater on land than ocean, highest at northern high latitudes, increased precipitation at high latitudes in winter, more intense precipitation events. By 2010, all were confirmed with statistical significance.
- **NASA/Hausfather study (2020):** Systematic evaluation of 17 climate models from 1970–2007: **10 of 17 closely matched observations; 14 of 17 after adjusting for actual emissions paths.**

Why climate science works for long-range prediction:
- Physics-based mechanistic models (not trend extrapolation)
- Well-understood forcing functions (CO2, solar, volcanic)
- Quantified predictions with explicit uncertainty ranges
- Continuous verification against observations
- Institutional incentives for accuracy

**Verdict: Best verified long-range track record of any forecasting domain. Period.**

### Bill Gates / Microsoft Leadership (~1993–1997)

Dan Luu's analysis of memos released during the *United States v. Microsoft* case reveals that Gates, Nathan Myhrvold, and other MS executives made remarkably detailed and accurate predictions about:

- HTTP over TCP becoming the dominant internet protocol
- The browser becoming a more important "platform" than any OS
- The exact mechanisms by which this would threaten Microsoft's monopoly
- The nature of future work and how technology would change it

**Key differentiator:** Deep mechanistic understanding of the technology, not buzzword-level trend extrapolation. Gates famously read every spec in detail and asked probing technical questions.

Dan Luu rates their vision comparably to Steve Yegge's predictions (7–8/9) but notes the predictions were "significantly more ambitious, so they seem much more impressive when controlling for the scope."

**Verdict: The best documented case of accurate long-range tech predictions, driven by deep domain knowledge.**

## Tier 2: Systematically Validated (Mostly 1–5 Years, Some Longer)

### Philip Tetlock's Superforecasters / Good Judgment Project

**The most important scientific work on prediction accuracy ever conducted.**

Tetlock's two landmark studies:

**1. *Expert Political Judgment* (2005):** 20-year study tracking 28,000+ predictions from 284 experts.
- Key finding: Average expert barely outperformed random chance ("dart-throwing chimps")
- Longer-range forecasts performed *worse* — accuracy degraded toward chance at 5+ years
- **"Foxes" (eclectic thinkers) consistently outperformed "hedgehogs" (big-idea thinkers)**, especially at longer horizons
- "What experts think matters far less than how they think"

**2. *Superforecasting* / Good Judgment Project (2011–2015):** IARPA-funded tournament, 5,000+ forecasters, 500+ questions.
- Top 2% ("superforecasters") beat all competitors by 35–72%
- Beat intelligence analysts with classified info by 30%+
- Teams of superforecasters outperformed prediction markets
- Superforecasters assigned probability 72–76% to things that happened, 24–28% to things that didn't

**Critical caveat:** The IARPA tournament tested predictions with resolution horizons of **months to ~1 year** (longest ~12 months). Tetlock himself is candid that superforecasting is validated for short-to-medium range. He has *not* claimed it works for 10+ year horizons.

When asked about long-range at a Long Now talk, Tetlock offered: "Here's my long-term prediction for Long Now. When the Long Now audience of 2515 looks back on the audience of 2015, their level of contempt for how we go about judging political debate will be roughly comparable to the level of contempt we have for the 1692 Salem witch trials."

**What makes superforecasters work:**
1. Triage — focus on questions where effort pays off
2. Fermi decomposition — break problems into sub-problems
3. Balance inside/outside views — use base rates
4. Bayesian updating — continuously revise with new evidence
5. Distinguish fine-grained probabilities (55/45 vs 60/40)
6. Balance under/overconfidence
7. Postmortems — learn from errors
8. Bicultural thinking — people with second cultures forecast better

**Verdict: Gold standard methodology for prediction. Validated at 1–5 year horizons. Unknown for 10+ years — likely much less accurate, but still the best approach available.**

### Samotsvety Forecasting

An elite group of ~15 forecasters, many drawn from top performers on Metaculus and other platforms.

- **Top 4 most accurate forecasters in Infer's (RAND) history** are all Samotsvety members
- Gap between them and 5th place is larger than gap between 5th and 10th
- Praised by Jason Matheny (CEO of RAND) for "impressive accuracy" and "commitment to scoring their own accuracy"
- They beat intelligence analysts who had access to classified info

**Method:** Saturday meetings where members discuss questions, share reasoning, present numerical probabilities, and challenge each other. Key traits:
- Deep research into each question
- Quantified probabilities, not vague assessments
- Self-scoring and accountability
- Diverse membership (economists, neuroscientists, MBA students, independent researchers)
- Explicit discussion of where and why they disagree

**Caveat:** Like superforecasters, Samotsvety's *verified* track record is on questions with resolution horizons of months to a few years. Their long-range predictions (e.g., China/Taiwan by 2030) have not yet resolved.

**Verdict: Probably the best living forecasters on Earth for medium-range questions. Unverified for 10+ year horizons.**

## Tier 3: Historically Notable Long-Range Predictors

### Isaac Asimov

Holden Karnofsky (Cold Takes / Open Philanthropy) commissioned Arb Research to evaluate the "Big Three" sci-fi writers. Asimov's 1964 predictions for 2014 (50-year horizon):

- **~50% accuracy** on nonobvious predictions
- Correctly predicted: flat-screen wall TVs, video calling, self-driving cars (in development), single-purpose household robots, close world population estimate (6.5B predicted vs 7.1B actual), nuclear power, processed meat alternatives
- Missed: underground/underwater living, levitating cars, moon colonies, universal ennui from automation
- Key insight: Often nailed the technology but overestimated adoption enthusiasm

Karnofsky rated Asimov's track record as "very impressive" — about half right on 50-year predictions that seemed nonobvious at the time.

**Verdict: Impressively accurate for casual futurism. But predictions were unscored, imprecise, and include easy hits.**

### Arthur C. Clarke

- Predicted communication satellites (1945) — 20 years before they existed
- Predicted internet-like global information networks (1974)
- Hit rate similar to Asimov's but "correct predictions don't seem as impressive" — more obvious in hindsight

### Steve Yegge

Software engineer who made 9 specific tech predictions in 2004:
- **~50% accuracy** scored strictly (4.5/9), **~80-90%** scored directionally (7-8/9)
- Continued being accurate *after* initial predictions noted — ruling out survivorship bias
- Made "high dimension" predictions right for the right reasons, not just lucky coin flips

**Method:** "If you read a lot, you'll start to spot trends and undercurrents." Deep domain knowledge + intuition from extensive reading.

**Verdict: Strong evidence that deep domain expertise enables accurate 5–10 year predictions. Better than any professional futurist.**

### Bryan Caplan

- **23/23 on public bets** (as of last evaluation)
- Strategy: "waits till he has the nuts" — takes the safe side of foolish bets offered by others
- Andrew Gelman: "Caplan's trick here is to show good judgment on what pitches to swing at"
- Methodology cannot generate positive predictions of big changes — only refute overconfident claims

**Verdict: Valuable as a "tax on bullshit." Not a predictor of future developments, but an excellent filter against bad predictions.**

## Tier 4: Famous But Poor Track Records

### Ray Kurzweil

Despite media reputation as "remarkably accurate," rigorous scoring tells a different story:

- **Dan Luu's scoring: ~7% accuracy**
- Methods: exponential trend extrapolation, "rounding up" data to fit optimistic narratives, cocktail-party-level understanding of specific domains
- Predicted by 2010: self-driving cars, cure for paraplegia, widespread AR
- Predicted by 2019: nanotech computers, realistic full-body VR, pervasive self-driving cars
- The "86% accuracy" claim from Kurzweil himself comes from extremely generous self-scoring where vague directional correctness counts

**Why his reputation persists:** Media selects on his few hits and ignores hundreds of misses. His predictions are often vague enough to claim partial credit.

### Michio Kaku

- **Dan Luu's scoring: ~3-6% accuracy** (in *Visions*, 1997)
- Despite explicitly criticizing other futurists and claiming physicist expertise gives better predictions
- Incorrectly assumed UV lithography would fail, requiring switch to fundamentally different computing substrates by 2020
- "Although Kaku pans other futurists for making predictions without having a real understanding of the topics they're discussing, it's not clear that Kaku has a better understanding"

### Other Professional Futurists

From Dan Luu's systematic evaluation of Wikipedia's futurist list:
- **Jacque Fresco:** ~0% (relies on magical thinking about human nature)
- **Buckminster Fuller:** ~0% (small sample; free-associates from word meanings)
- **Patrick Dixon:** ~10% (best of the futurist list, but right for wrong reasons)
- **Gerard K. O'Neill:** ~0% (predicted massive space colonization by 2010)
- **John Naisbitt:** Unscoreable (predictions too vague — "horoscope-like")
- **Alvin Toffler:** Unscoreable (vague; named trends about to *stop*)

**Common failure modes of professional futurists:**
1. "Big idea" hammer applied to every nail
2. Exponential trend extrapolation without understanding mechanisms
3. No systematic evaluation of past errors
4. Cocktail-party-level understanding of predicted domains
5. Cherry-picking past "hits" to sell future predictions

## What Actually Works for Long-Range Prediction

Synthesizing across all sources (Tetlock, Dan Luu, Muehlhauser, Karnofsky):

### Methods that produce accuracy:
1. **Deep mechanistic understanding** of the specific domain (not just trend-watching)
2. **Fox-like thinking** — drawing from many frameworks, embracing complexity
3. **Quantified predictions** with explicit probabilities and time horizons
4. **Systematic self-scoring** and learning from errors
5. **Bayesian updating** — continuously revising with new evidence
6. **Physics-based models** when available (climate, some tech)
7. **Base rates** — "how often do things of this sort happen in situations of this sort?"

### Methods that fail:
1. **Exponential trend extrapolation** — small rate errors compound catastrophically
2. **"Big idea" reasoning** — one theory applied everywhere
3. **Vague predictions** — unfalsifiable by design
4. **Panacea thinking** — "technology X will solve everything"
5. **No error analysis** — never examining why past predictions failed
6. **Buzzword-level domain knowledge** — predicting without understanding mechanisms

### The uncomfortable truth about 10+ year horizons:

**Nobody has a rigorously verified track record for 10+ year predictions.** Even the best forecasters:

- Tetlock's superforecasters are validated at ~1 year horizons
- Samotsvety's verified wins are at 1–3 year horizons
- Climate models are the exception because they predict a *physical system* with well-understood forcing functions
- For complex sociotechnical systems (economy, politics, technology adoption), accuracy degrades rapidly beyond 5 years
- Muehlhauser's review found no forecasting exercise that demonstrated clear skill at 10+ year horizons

**The best you can do for 10+ year predictions:**
1. Use climate-model-style mechanistic reasoning when possible
2. Apply superforecaster methodology (Fermi decomposition, base rates, Bayesian updating)
3. Focus on "megatrend" direction rather than specific timing
4. Maintain deep domain expertise in what you're predicting
5. State predictions precisely with quantified confidence
6. Score yourself ruthlessly and update your methods
