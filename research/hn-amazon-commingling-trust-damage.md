## Amazon Commingling: The Irreversible Trust Damage Pattern

Elaboration on the behavioral damage signal from the [HN thread on Amazon ending commingling](hn-amazon-commingling-end.md).

The thread has a cluster of 15-20 commenters who don't just say "I'm annoyed" but describe a completed behavioral transition. The key word is *completed*. They're not threatening to leave; they already left and rebuilt their purchasing routines around the absence of Amazon.

### The stages they describe

**1. Trigger event** — usually a specific counterfeit that crossed a personal threshold. For **matsemann** it was fake dental products ("stuff I put in my body is no-go"). For **mouth**, counterfeit SD cards burned them multiple times. For **al_borland**, a counterfeit *water bottle* — something so mundane they hadn't imagined it could be faked. The trigger is almost never a high-value item. It's the realization that *nothing* is safe.

**2. Category-by-category withdrawal** — they don't cancel Prime overnight. They start routing specific categories elsewhere. **mark-r**: "Sandisk makes the best Flash cards but never buy them from Amazon." **squeedles** goes to Staples for toner. **tzs** drives to Best Buy for an Apple cable. **logankeenan** won't buy "high priced high quality items" from Amazon anymore. Each person carves out their own no-go zones.

**3. Discovery that alternatives are good enough** — this is the irreversible part. **logankeenan**: "DTC from quality producers now have decent websites, free shipping, and good customer service." **lizknope** started buying flash cards direct from the SanDisk web store. **julianozen** switched to Walmart for consumer packaged goods. They found the alternatives aren't just acceptable — they're *better* on the dimension that triggered the switch (authenticity), and close enough on convenience. Once you've done 3-4 purchases from a brand's own site and gotten what you expected every time, Amazon's value proposition is specifically that it's faster — not that it's trustworthy.

**4. Residual Amazon usage shrinks to disposable commodities** — **yakattak**: "I basically only order things that I don't care about quality with now." **boelboel**: "Only buy disposable garbage on amazon." Amazon becomes the store for things where counterfeits don't matter because the genuine article is also cheap junk. This is the exact inverse of where Amazon wants to be positioned.

### Why this doesn't reverse

The asymmetry is that trust is slow to build and fast to destroy, but *habits* are slow to build in both directions. These people spent months rebuilding purchasing routines — bookmarking brand sites, finding local stores for specific categories, learning which DTC brands ship fast. That's sunk cost that now works *against* Amazon. Ending commingling removes the reason they left, but doesn't remove the alternative infrastructure they built. They'd have to actively choose to reconsolidate on Amazon, and the only incentive to do so is convenience — which is weaker than the authenticity guarantee they now get elsewhere.

### The demographic problem

HN skews toward exactly the people who buy premium electronics, specialty health products, and brand-name components — high-margin categories where counterfeiting is both most common and most damaging. These aren't the bulk-paper-towel customers. They're the customers who used to buy $70 Apple cables, $200 headphones, and name-brand supplements without thinking twice. Losing them doesn't show up as a user count decline. It shows up as average order value compression and a shift toward low-margin commodity purchases — Amazon slowly becoming the Walmart it once disrupted, but with warehouse overhead.

### The quiet signal nobody quantifies

**comrade1234** and **SoftTalker** both say they haven't bought from Amazon in *years*. They're in a thread about Amazon announcing a fix, and their response is essentially "doesn't matter, I'm gone." That's not anger — anger can be resolved. It's indifference. The most dangerous customer state for any platform isn't hostility; it's the customer who moved on and genuinely doesn't care what you do next.
