## HN Thread Distillation: "Child's Play: Tech's new generation and the end of thinking"

**Source:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47088685 · 219 points · 131 comments · Feb 2026
**Appendix:** [Leverage over effort — deep breakdown](#appendix-leverage-over-effort)
**Article:** https://harpers.org/archive/2026/03/childs-play-sam-kriss-ai-startup-roy-lee/ (Harper's Magazine, Sam Kriss)

**Article summary:** A long-form immersive piece profiling three "highly agentic" young men in SF: Roy Lee (Cluely founder, cheating-tool-turned-meeting-notes startup), Eric Zhu (18-year-old serial founder now running Sperm Racing), and Donald Boat (viral troll who extorted a gaming PC from Sam Altman). Through them, Kriss interrogates Silicon Valley's new theology — that "agency" has replaced intelligence, competence, or expertise as the trait that matters, and that AI will bifurcate humanity into a permanent overclass and underclass. The piece is masterfully written: funny, damning, and genuinely unsettling. Scott Alexander appears as a kind of Greek chorus, admitting the rationalist community accidentally created the AI arms race while hoping the eventual superintelligence will be wise enough to "refuse to give us everything we want."

### Dominant Sentiment: Alarm wrapped in literary admiration

The thread is split between people dazzled by the writing and people genuinely rattled by what it describes. Almost nobody defends the subjects. The dominant mood is: "This is the article that finally said the thing we've all been sensing."

### Key Insights

**1. The article is better than the thread's ability to discuss it**

Multiple commenters (jamesjolliffe, lordleft, bogrollben, 141205) simply praise the writing. "Holy shit, this article is well written. I don't even agree with his point or pessimism. I just enjoy reading this." This is notable because HN rarely yields to literary quality — it usually attacks the argument. When the thread's primary response is aesthetic admiration, the piece has done something unusual: it's made the case through portraiture rather than argument, and the portraits are devastating enough that the argument follows without needing to be stated.

**2. "Agency" is just sociopathy rebranded**

munificent crystallizes what Kriss shows but doesn't quite say: "I think the 'agency' the article talks about is really just 'willingness to take risks.' And the reason some people are high outliers on that scale is a combination of: coming from such a level of privilege that they will be completely fine even if they lose over and over again; willingness to push any losses onto other undeserving people without experiencing guilt; a psychological compulsion towards impulsive behavior and inability to think about long-term consequences. In short, rich selfish sociopaths."

This is the thread's sharpest comment. It takes the Valley's self-mythology — "agency" as a heroic trait — and correctly identifies it as a combination of cushioned downside + absent conscience. Roy Lee's biography confirms this: he could drop out of Columbia and fail spectacularly because the worst case scenario for him was still comfortable.

**3. The erosion of mastery is the real civilizational threat**

voxleone's top comment is the thread's anchor: "A complex technological civilization depends on people willing to go deep, to wrestle with fundamentals, to think in decades rather than funding cycles. If the next generation of capable minds concludes that visibility is more rational than depth, we're not just changing startup culture. You can survive a lot of hype. You can't survive a steady erosion of mastery."

MarceliusK adds the critical corollary: "The scary part is that you can't just 'hire mastery' on demand. You have to grow it." This is a supply-side argument that the "agency" crowd completely ignores. Roy Lee says "once they have money, they can hire competent engineers; it's trivially easy." The thread correctly identifies this as delusional — mastery takes decades to develop and the pipeline is being poisoned by exactly the culture the article describes.

**4. The juvenoia counter is weaker than it looks**

piperswe invokes "juvenoia" — the perennial complaint that kids are dumber than their elders. It's a valid pattern, but saulpw's retort lands harder: "And yet, when they worried about what television would do to a generation of brains, they were right. The Boomers, as a generation, never became wise, and their brains are mushier than ever." The point isn't that young people are categorically stupider — it's that specific technologies can genuinely degrade specific cognitive capacities, and the worry about AI dependency has the same structure as the worry about TV, which turned out to be broadly correct.

**5. The meritocracy-to-aristocracy pipeline**

butterbomb: "We are quickly returning to a world of entrenched social hierarchy where there are lords and peasants and little room even for social mobility. With the corpse of meritocracy too rotted to deny at this point the elite simply seem to have run out of lies for placating the people." stego-tech traces this back 50 years to celebrity C-suites and the decoupling of compensation from value creation.

The article makes this concrete through Roy Lee, who explicitly acknowledges his ethos will "result in a world of rapid inequality." He doesn't see this as a problem — just a fact. The thread's response: this isn't a new tech phenomenon, it's the terminal stage of a longer process where visibility replaced competence as the basis for reward.

**6. The "leverage over effort" quote is the thread's lightning rod**

Cluely's manifesto: "The future won't reward effort. It'll reward leverage." FatherOfCurses: "JFC kill me now that is NOT a future I want to live in." This quote crystalizes the article's core tension — it's not just a marketing slogan, it's a sincere description of the world these people are building. The thread treats it as a confession rather than an aspiration.

**7. SF's cultural erasure is real and specific**

keiferski raises the one angle the article takes for granted: "In SF though, it's as if the previous culture of the place has just been overwritten entirely. Hard to believe that it's the same city which Kerouac, the Beats or Hippies ran around in." This isn't just nostalgia — it's an observation about what happens when an entire city's economy collapses into a single industry controlled by people who "do not obtain value from reading books." Roy Lee's dismissal of the Canterbury Tales as "the Decanterbury Tales" is the article's most economical piece of characterization.

**8. The astroturf detection: one commenter is spam**

atomic128 hijacks the thread to promote their "Poison Fountain" anti-AI project, citing r/hacking vs r/programming reception. GaggiX correctly calls this out: "Your opinion on the two subreddit seems to be just influenced by how much they like your project or not. A project that you spam in every of your comments." Pure self-promotion, not engagement.

### What the Thread Misses

- **The Donald Boat portrait is the article's real thesis and the thread barely touches it.** Kriss uses Donald Boat — who got a gaming PC from Sam Altman by being funny on Twitter — to show that the "agency" economy has been reduced to its purest form: attention → capital, with no product or skill required. Roy is the deluded version; Eric is the bored version; Donald is the honest version. The thread focuses almost entirely on Roy.
- **The Scott Alexander section is dynamite and nobody discusses it.** Alexander admitting "everybody who started AI companies between 2009 and 2019 was basically thinking 'I want to do this superintelligence thing' and coming out of our milieu" — i.e., the AI safety movement *created* the AI arms race — is a genuinely important admission. The thread ignores it entirely.
- **Nobody engages with the article's implicit class analysis.** Roy's parents run a college-prep agency. Eric found the right Discord servers. Donald was already culturally literate enough to write Joycean prose. "Agency" isn't evenly distributed — it correlates with existing social capital. The article shows this without editorializing; the thread doesn't pick it up.
- **The Cluely product failure is underweighted.** The article notes Cluely "still doesn't consistently work" and crashed *during the interview*. By December 2025 it was rebranding to "meeting notes." This is the most concrete evidence against the "agency > competence" thesis — even in this market, you eventually need a product — and neither the article nor the thread emphasizes it enough.

### Verdict

The article is the best piece of tech journalism published in 2026 so far — not because of its arguments (which are implicit) but because of its portraits. Roy Lee, who keeps a vibrator in his office for "girls" and dismisses Huckleberry Finn as "bullshit," is not a caricature. He's a real person who has received tens of millions of dollars for a product that doesn't work, from investors who don't care whether it works, in a culture that has formally abandoned the pretense that competence matters. The thread gets the broad takeaway — mastery is being devalued, agency-as-sociopathy is being rewarded — but misses the article's subtlest move: showing that even the "winners" of this system (Roy, alone in a gray bedroom in his own office) are its victims.

## Appendix: Leverage over effort

A deep breakdown of insight #6 — the Cluely manifesto quote as cultural lightning rod.

The quote in full, from Cluely's viral ad:

> We built Cluely so you never have to think alone again. It sees your screen. Hears your audio. Feeds you answers in real time.... Why memorize facts, write code, research anything—when a model can do it in seconds? The future won't reward effort. It'll reward leverage.

### The quote is correct, and always has been

"Leverage over effort" is not a prediction — it's a description of how capital has worked for centuries. The person who owns the factory has always out-earned the person who works in it. The person who owns the land has always out-earned the person who farms it. "Leverage" is the current euphemism for "capital" — the ability to multiply your output by controlling resources, tools, or other people's labor. This is Ricardo, it's Marx, it's Piketty's r > g. Roy Lee didn't discover anything. He restated the oldest observation in political economy using startup vocabulary.

qgin in the thread asks the obvious question: "Has the world ever rewarded effort?" krapp's sarcastic reply — "No. Not once in the entire history of the human race..." — is meant as a rebuttal, but actually proves qgin's point more than it disproves it. Effort has *sometimes* been rewarded, in *specific institutional arrangements* designed to make it so: labor unions, progressive taxation, public education, professional licensing, tenure systems, minimum wages. These are all artificial structures that override the natural tendency of leverage to dominate effort. They're exceptions, not the default. The default is leverage wins.

### Why the quote horrifies people

**First, it's the quiet part said loud.** There's always been a social contract — partially real, partially mythological — that effort is *supposed* to matter. Meritocracy as ideology says: if you work hard and get good at something, you'll be rewarded. This was never fully true, but it was true *enough* that people organized their lives around it. They went to school, learned trades, built expertise, expected that competence would translate into security. The Cluely manifesto doesn't just predict that this contract is ending — it *celebrates* the end. It's not saying "unfortunately, leverage matters more than effort." It's saying "why would you even try to develop skills when you could have leverage instead?" That reframing — from lament to pitch — is what makes people's skin crawl.

FatherOfCurses's "JFC kill me now" response is the visceral version of this. It's not that they disagree. It's that they don't want to live in a world where this truth is stated without shame, because the shame was the only thing maintaining the fiction that effort mattered.

**Second, it collapses the distinction between description and prescription.** "The future won't reward effort" is a factual claim about incentive structures. "It'll reward leverage" is an invitation to act accordingly. By packaging them together in a product manifesto, Cluely turns an observation into a moral imperative: *stop trying to be good at things and start using our tool instead.* This is the structure of every effective piece of propaganda — take something people already half-believe, remove the ambiguity, and present compliance as the only rational response.

littlexsparkee catches this: "you can fudge it for a while...but not forever. i worry about what kind of message this sends young minds." The worry isn't that the quote is wrong. It's that if enough people *act on it* — if a generation of capable people decides leverage is rational and mastery is for suckers — the prophecy becomes self-fulfilling. You don't need AI to make effort worthless; you just need enough people to believe effort is worthless and stop developing expertise. The infrastructure degrades, the institutional knowledge disappears, and then effort really *is* worthless because there's no functioning system left to reward it.

**Third, the quote reveals who "leverage" actually serves.** When Roy Lee says "leverage," he means: use Cluely to perform above your actual competence level. But who *benefits* from a workforce that can't do anything without an AI intermediary? Not the workers. Their leverage is rented, not owned — it depends entirely on continued access to the tool. The real leverage accrues to whoever controls the tool. Cluely's manifesto is addressed to the worker ("you never have to think alone again") but the product serves the owner. It's the same structure as every platform play: empower the user in a way that makes them dependent, then extract rent from the dependency.

This is where butterbomb's "lords and peasants" comment connects. The "leverage" Cluely offers isn't real leverage — it's a new form of dependency marketed as empowerment. Real leverage is owning equity, owning infrastructure, owning the AI model itself. Using someone else's AI to do your job is the *opposite* of leverage; it's renting someone else's leverage at whatever price they set. The manifesto is, at bottom, an invitation to voluntarily deskill yourself while paying for the privilege.

### The article proves this in a single scene

Kriss tries using Cluely during the interview with Roy. It tells him to say things he's already said. It gets stuck in a loop suggesting he ask to open Cluely, which is already open. Roy asks, confused, "What would you have wanted it to say?" Kriss notes the contradiction: Roy, who "reacted very violently to anyone who tried to tell him what to do," built a product whose sole function is telling people what to do. The leverage-over-effort ideology, when instantiated as an actual product, produces a tool that traps its user in a recursive loop of machine-generated banality. That's not leverage. That's surrender with a subscription fee.

### Why the thread can't complete the analysis

The thread reacts to the quote emotionally — disgust, alarm, resignation — but doesn't quite close the loop. The quote works as a lightning rod because it's the exact point where Silicon Valley's self-mythology ("we're building the future") and its actual function ("we're building new forms of dependency") become indistinguishable. People sense this without being able to fully articulate it, which is why the responses are visceral rather than analytical. The article, to its credit, lets the scene with the malfunctioning product do the analytical work that the thread can't.
