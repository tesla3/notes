## HN Thread Distillation: "People Loved the Dot-Com Boom. The A.I. Boom, Not So Much"

**Source:** [NYT](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/21/technology/ai-boom-backlash.html) by David Streitfeld · [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47107819) (54 comments, 52 points) · Feb 21, 2026

**Article summary:** NYT argues the AI boom faces unprecedented public hostility compared to the dot-com era. Cites YouGov data (43% fear AI-driven extinction), NBER survey (80% of firms report zero productivity impact), Altman admitting adoption is "surprisingly slow," and Jensen Huang complaining critics are "scaring people from making investments." Thesis: the vibes are bad and tech leaders are worried.

### Dominant Sentiment: Article is wrong on its own premise

The thread's sharpest energy goes to demolishing the article's central comparison. Multiple well-sourced comments establish that people did NOT love the dot-com boom — and the article's own author was a dot-com skeptic writing for WashPo in the 1990s.

### Key Insights

**1. The article's premise is self-refuting**

`alephnerd` delivers the kill shot: David Streitfeld himself "was reporting negatively about the Dot-Com boom in the 1990s at the WashPo" and "has been very public about his pride of being 'low-tech.'" The premise that techno-optimism was the default in the 1990s which has now curdled into techno-pessimism is "clearly written in bad faith, when a large portion of the intelligentsia was *already* techno-pessimistic in the 1990s." Seven sources cited. `abetusk` reinforces with FuckedCompany.com, Krugman's "no greater than the fax machine" prediction, and Clay Shirky dismissing online databases. The article manufactures a golden age that didn't exist in order to make the present seem uniquely dark.

**2. The ice cream truck at the burning building**

`cosmic_cheese` offers the thread's best metaphor and framework: three forces in confluence. (1) Too much, too fast — "the gas pedal was floored shortly after OpenAI shipped a usable MVP." (2) Unclear value — lazy bolt-on integrations for tasks people don't need help with. (3) Wrong macro environment — "Many of your average people feel like the dog in the 'this is fine' meme and aren't really in the mood to be sold something that could ultimately further concentrate wealth." The third factor is underexplored in most AI discourse. It's not that the technology is bad — it's that it arrived during a crisis of institutional trust, rising inequality, and post-COVID economic anxiety. Timing is doing as much work as capability.

**3. Tech trust is a depleted resource, not a renewable one**

Where insight #2 is about macro conditions (economy, inequality), this is about the tech industry's specific credibility deficit. `apollo_mojave`: "I don't know if it's really tech itself that turns people off. It's the culture, the failed expectations, the lack of trust, everything, all smushed together." `jcgrillo` lists the ledger: "Google got evil, Twitter was eviscerated by a clown who likes to make nazi salutes, Facebook plumbed depths of enshittification previously unknown to science. The metaverse turned out to be hot air. Bitcoins did not in fact change anything." `Animats` adds to the pile: "3D TV. VR. Metaverse. Electric cars in the US. NFTs. They all worked technically, but just didn't catch on." AI isn't just fighting bad macro timing — it's being sold by companies that have systematically destroyed their own credibility over 15 years. Each dud boom depleted the trust reserve further. There's nothing left to draw on.

**4. The distributional question nobody in industry will answer**

`zaptheimpaler` makes the thread's sharpest structural argument: "You can already see LLMs are making programmers much more productive but it's actually causing lower salaries and job losses — so who's capturing the value of that increased productivity? Not workers." `prydt` frames the lose-lose cleanly: either AI works and you lose your job, or it's a bubble and the crash costs you your job. The dot-com boom at least created visible consumer goods people wanted. The AI boom's visible output is "slop content and chatbots that hallucinate" (`umairnadeem123`). The value proposition for ordinary people is negative in every scenario they can see.

**5. Jensen Huang's "negative vibes" complaint is the tell**

`jcgrillo`: "This guy is at the helm of the most valuable company in *world history* and according to him the thing that's threatening their growth is.. negative vibes? Where are the adults?" When the CEO of a trillion-dollar company blames growth shortfalls on skeptics rather than product-market fit, it reveals that the industry's internal model of adoption is "sentiment-driven" rather than "value-driven." This is exactly backwards — and it's the same mistake the NYT article makes from the opposite direction.

**6. "Someone who knows AI will replace you" is already empirically suspect**

`latchkey` deploys the cliché; `jcgrillo` stress-tests it: "AI coding tools are very sensitive to codebase structure. If you work on a monolith with relatively simple, straightforward structure this is the happy path... It's not clear, though, that for the vast majority of developers AI provides *any speedup whatsoever*. If it only works for the top 5% or whatever, that addressable market is very, very small." This is a more honest version of the productivity story than either boosters or doomers offer.

**7. Software improvements make hardware more valuable, not less**

In a side debate about stranded data center assets, `jsnell` makes the counterintuitive but correct point: "As the software improves on any of the principal axes (cheaper cost for same quality, or new capabilities), that same unit of compute will produce *more* value." `hedora` disagrees, arguing "there's not enough money in the most optimistic revenue projections to pay for them." This is the thread's most technically substantive disagreement and remains unresolved — it hinges on whether demand for inference is elastic enough to absorb efficiency gains (Jevons paradox) or whether the market simply isn't there.

### Common Pushbacks

| Pushback | Quality | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| Dot-com had skeptics too, article premise is false | **Strong** | Well-sourced by multiple commenters, including evidence the author himself was a dot-com skeptic |
| AI value is real, people will come around | **Medium** | `latchkey`, `ars` assert this but don't address WHY adoption curves differ from previous tech |
| This is inevitable, resistance is futile | **Weak** | `latchkey`'s horse-and-buggy analogy gets correctly challenged — not all tech improvements lead to better outcomes, and inevitability isn't an argument for acceptance |
| AI only benefits capital, not labor | **Strong** | `zaptheimpaler`'s point about programmer productivity gains flowing to employers is verifiable and unanswered |

### What the Thread Misses

- **Nobody engages the NBER stat** — 80% of firms reporting zero productivity impact is the most damning data point in the article, and the thread ignores it entirely. If true, the "AI is already useful, people just don't appreciate it" camp has a serious evidence problem.
- **Public sentiment may be irrelevant to adoption.** People adopted smartphones while hating tech companies and used social media while decrying it. The thread assumes sentiment drives adoption; historically, economic incentives for deployment override consumer preferences. The question isn't whether people *like* AI — it's whether the deployment incentives are strong enough.
- **The thread is entirely tech workers talking to tech workers.** Nobody represents the "average person" the article is about. The perspectives are either "I use AI for coding and it's great/terrible" or structural economic arguments. Actual consumer sentiment is only present via survey citations.
- **No one distinguishes consumer AI from enterprise AI.** These could diverge dramatically: enterprises adopt for cost-cutting regardless of worker sentiment, while consumer products flounder because nobody wants to pay for them.

### Verdict

The article and the thread are making the same category error from opposite directions: both treat public sentiment as the signal that determines whether AI succeeds or fails. The NYT says bad vibes mean the boom is fragile; the boosters say people will come around. But sentiment is a lagging indicator of tech adoption, not a leading one. What actually determines whether AI reshapes the economy is whether the deployment economics work — whether the cost savings are real enough that enterprises adopt regardless of what polls say, and whether consumer products emerge that are useful enough that people use them while complaining. The thread circles this truth when `ars` notes "everyone said how terrible it is — and yet they all use it" — but never follows the thread to its conclusion: that the real risk to AI isn't that people hate it, but that the 80% of firms seeing no productivity impact might be right, and the product just isn't good enough yet. The vibes are a distraction. The spreadsheets are the story.
