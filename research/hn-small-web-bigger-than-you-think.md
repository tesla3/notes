## HN Thread Distillation: "The 'small web' is bigger than you might think"

**Source:** [kevinboone.me](https://kevinboone.me/small_web_is_big.html) | [HN thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47401879) — 327 pts, 138 comments, 94 unique authors

**Article summary:** Kevin Boone, a Gemini adopter, tried to build a feed aggregator for the "small web" using Kagi's curated list (~32K sites). After filtering for valid feeds with timestamps and at least monthly updates, he found ~9,000 active sites producing ~1,251 new posts per day — too many for a single-page aggregator. His intended point: the small web is more alive than you'd guess. The unintended reveal: the tooling and mindset designed for Gemini's cozy ~6,000 capsules doesn't transfer.

### Dominant Sentiment: Nostalgic energy, fragmented vision

Everyone agrees the commercial web is broken. Nobody agrees on the fix, and the proposed solutions (Gemini, curated lists, alternative search, new protocols) each attract a different constituency that barely overlaps with the others. There's a telling absence of non-technical voices — the thread is tech professionals debating tech solutions for what may be a cultural problem.

### Key Insights

**1. Kagi's 32K-site list is the best small web index and still deeply inadequate**

Multiple commenters with personal sites reported not being on the list. `flir`: "you haven't found me yet and I'm not hiding, I'm just not chasing SEO." `aquova`: "I suspect there's the potential for an order of magnitude more sites than have been found." `lich_king` identified three structural exclusions: (1) must be a blog with an RSS feed, excluding hobbyist reference sites like [fleacircus.co.uk](https://www.fleacircus.co.uk/History.htm); (2) must be updated within the last 2 years; (3) blanket Substack ban while allowing Blogspot, WordPress.com, and YouTube — producing absurdities like noahpinionblog.blogspot.com qualifying but noahpinion.blog not. `freediver` (the sole maintainer) clarified the feed requirement is practical ("we don't want to overcrawl") and the Substack ban is philosophical ("annoying subscribe popup... against the spirit"). `mattlondon` asked the obvious question nobody else did: "Why would you only include blogs in your small web index? That must be a minute fraction of what is out there." The deeper issue: any list-based approach requires an RSS feed, which structurally biases toward blog-format content and misses the idiosyncratic reference sites that are arguably the soul of the small web.

**2. Discovery is the actual unsolved problem, not protocol or hosting**

The article frames the challenge as aggregation, but the thread converges on discovery. `Gunax` captured the decay mechanism: "at some point [Google] stopped directing traffic to small websites. And like a highway closure ripples through small town economies, it was barely noticed by travellers but devastating to recipients." `Gunax` also named a second force the thread otherwise missed: the shift to video. "Travel blogs were replaced with travel vlogs. Tutorials became videos." — and video is expensive to self-host, pushing people to platforms. `marginalia_nu` (Viktor, solo developer of Marginalia Search) explained why his engine feels different: "it simply does not take popularity into account," breaking the feedback loop where search engines show what they've already shown. `upboundspiral` called an independent search engine "a public good as valuable as the Internet Archive." `cosmicgadget` proposed a complementary approach: similarity-based graphs where "good, non-commercial webpages will be similar to other good webpages" — enter the graph in the right place and quality clusters together. The structural insight: Google's click-optimization creates a self-reinforcing loop that buries anything without existing traffic, but this is a *design choice*, not an inevitability. Marginalia demonstrates that alternative discovery mechanisms exist — the bottleneck is resources, not theory.

**3. The encryption debate exposes a false dichotomy**

`GuB-42` argued the small web should reject encryption — removing TLS eliminates financial transactions, simplifies microcontroller hosting, and enables caching proxies. `marginalia_nu` countered with history: "Big thing that made encryption required is arguably that ISPs started injecting crap into webpages." `UqWBcuFx6NV4r` was blunter: "Can all this performative love for unencrypted HTTP just die already. You've all forgotten what it was actually like." `zzo38computer` offered the most nuanced position: allow but don't require encryption — the client and server should use it when they can. Notably, `zzo38computer` also pointed out that Gemini is non-commercial *despite* requiring TLS, which demolishes the premise that removing encryption is necessary to prevent commercialization. The constraint that keeps Gemini non-commercial is cultural and format-based, not cryptographic. GuB-42's proposal conflates two independent properties: "no commerce" and "no encryption."

**4. Gemini's deliberate constraints serve implementors, not readers**

`mattlondon` delivered the sharpest critique: Gemini forbids inline images, tables, MathML, SVG — "very limited for even dry scientific research papers, which I think would otherwise be an excellent use-case." The defense from `wibbily` reveals the real priority: "one of the tenets of Gemini is that a lone programmer can write their own client in a spirited afternoon/weekend. Markdown is just a little too hard to clear the bar." `krapp` noted the protocol's creator reportedly ranted when someone added a *favicon*. `wibbily` added nuance: "the Gemini protocol is just a way of moving bytes around; nothing stops you from sending Markdown if you want (and at least some clients will render it)." `mattlondon` and `krapp` discussed a hypothetical "markdown-web" borrowing Gemini's client-cert auth — and `ontehfritz` has actually built something like it: demarkus, a markdown-only protocol with client tools, TUI, server, and MCP integration. Whether it gains traction is another question, but the idea isn't just hypothetical.

**5. "Shell script slab city" — the strongest critique nobody engaged with**

`romaniv`: "Small Web, Indie Web and Gemini are terminally missing the point. The web in the 90s was an *ecosystem* that attracted people because of experimentation with the medium, diversity of content and certain free-spirited social defaults." Trying to recreate it by removing features and reverting visual styles is backwards. `pipeline_peak` landed the devastating restatement: "Every answer I've seen to corporate internet whether it be Mastodon or Gemini just sounds like shell script slab city... Just spin up your instance, map it to a port, initialize the listener daemon, download the cli viewer…" `krapp` reinforced: "Ideological zealots are more or less the only people who hate the modern web so much that they want to quarantine themselves within an entirely different and functionally limited protocol. Everyone else is fine discussing camping in Facebook groups and on Reddit and wherever, maybe just using an ad blocker." `Karrot_Kream` added: "any niche idea on the internet is like candle flame to zealous moths." These comments cut to the heart of the small web's adoption ceiling: the movement optimizes for the sensibilities of its existing members rather than for attracting anyone new. Nobody in the thread responded to `pipeline_peak` substantively.

**6. The monetization taboo is self-defeating**

`627467`: "small web can also be about sustainable monetization... There's nothing wrong with publishers aspiring to get paid." `ardeaver`: "equating good = 'no monetization' is exactly how we've ended up in a situation where everything is controlled by a few giant mega corps." `UqWBcuFx6NV4r` recalled "that ethical ad network in the late 2000s that all the cool tech bloggers would use" — a reminder that the dichotomy between ethical publishing and making money is recent, not inherent. If the small web insists on zero monetization, it structurally limits participation to people with free time, stable incomes, and intrinsic motivation to publish — which in practice means tech professionals, reinforcing the demographic monoculture that makes the small web feel like a clubhouse rather than an alternative.

**7. The 88×31 badge ecosystem is the small web's surviving social protocol**

`varun_ch` highlighted 88×31 pixel link badges and webrings — a 30-year-old tradition. `101008`: "One of the happiest moments of my childhood was when my button was placed in that website I loved to visit everyday." `NooneAtAll3` raised the maintenance problem: checking Terry Tao's blogroll found only ~8 of ~50 sites still alive. `susam` described a manual approach — visiting each blogrolled site monthly, keeping the list intentionally small. This is charming and works as social glue, but it's a curation mechanism for the already-connected, not a discovery mechanism for the disconnected.

**8. Marginalia Search is the thread's quietly most important project**

`marginalia_nu` appeared throughout the thread with informed, self-deprecating contributions. Even after NLNet funding ends in weeks, he has "enough of a war chest to work full time probably a good bit into 2029." Operational costs are self-funding. `janalsncm` made the case for why being solo is an advantage: "no PMs breathing down your neck to inject more ads... you don't need anybody's permission to deploy a new ranking strategy." `marginalia_nu` was also the most technically credible voice in the encryption debate, citing ISP injection history and the CLOUD Act's implications for Cloudflare-proxied traffic. If the small web's core problem is discovery, Marginalia is the closest thing to a structural solution — but the thread treats it as a cool side project rather than critical infrastructure that deserves institutional support.

### Common Pushbacks

| Pushback | Quality | Note |
|----------|---------|------|
| Google intentionally buries small sites for profit | Medium | More likely an emergent effect of click-optimization than a deliberate choice (`lich_king`, `marginalia_nu`); the result is the same either way |
| Removing encryption prevents commercialization | Weak | Gemini requires TLS and remains non-commercial; the constraint is cultural/format-based, not cryptographic (`zzo38computer`) |
| The small web is just nostalgia | Medium | There's real nostalgia, but 1,251 daily posts and 32K sites are present-tense activity; the critique has more bite when aimed at Gemini's aesthetic choices |
| Gemini's text-only constraint preserves simplicity | Misapplied | Preserves *implementor* simplicity; readers bear the cost. Even the mailing list argued about TLS library dependencies (`wibbily`) |
| A curated list can scale to represent the small web | Weak | Even Kagi's 32K list has structural biases; `lich_king`'s million-site question is unanswered. `cosmicgadget`'s similarity graph is a more promising direction |

### What the Thread Misses

- **The AI content flood will hit the small web's curation filters.** `heliumtera` and `apples_oranges` raised this but it was never deeply explored. "Non-commercial" and "personal" are the small web's quality signals, but LLM-generated hobby blogs pass both filters trivially. The small web needs quality signals beyond "is this person trying to sell me something?" and nobody is working on that.
- **Mobile and apps are the elephant in the room.** `Gunax` named video as a force but nobody mentioned that smartphones moved a generation's entire internet experience into apps. The small web is a desktop phenomenon debating desktop problems. A solution that doesn't work on a phone screen is invisible to most people under 30.
- **The anglophone bias is structural, not incidental.** `est`: "kagi exclude non-English sites. Sad for mixed language blogs like mine." A one-line comment revealing that the "small web movement" has already reproduced Big Tech's anglophone defaults. Entire non-English small web ecosystems may exist and be invisible to this community.
- **`tonymet`'s pragmatic middle path was ignored.** "One could formalize it as a browser extension or small-web HTTP proxy that limits JS, dom size, cookie access etc using existing Web browsers & user agents." This avoids the Gemini trap of building a parallel protocol nobody uses, and nobody engaged with it.

### Verdict

The small web is real, growing, and larger than its own advocates realize — but the movement is trapped in a loop where it builds for its existing constituency (technically sophisticated, English-speaking, nostalgic for the 90s web) and then wonders why it doesn't grow. `pipeline_peak`'s "shell script slab city" is the thread's sharpest unrefuted critique. The actual path forward appeared in the thread but wasn't recognized as central: Marginalia proves you can build discovery without popularity bias, `cosmicgadget`'s similarity graphs offer a spam-resistant scaling mechanism, and `tonymet`'s browser-extension approach avoids the protocol-ghetto problem entirely. The bottleneck isn't ideas or technology — it's that the small web community is more interested in debating protocol purity than in building the bridges that would make their content reachable by anyone who doesn't already agree with them.
