## HN Thread Distillation: "Ask HN: How are you all staying sane?"

**Thread:** https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47215569 | 137 points, 137 comments, 103 authors | Mar 3, 2026

**Thread summary:** Throwaway account posts a kitchen-sink anxiety dump — AI disruption, geopolitical conflicts, stock market uncertainty, enshittification, rights erosion. Asks how people are coping. The thread splits between stoic disengagement ("touch grass"), active-but-bounded engagement ("action absorbs anxiety"), and a handful of genuinely distressed voices who find neither answer sufficient.

### Dominant Sentiment: Rehearsed stoicism concealing unresolved tension

The thread *sounds* calm — most replies are measured, advice-oriented. But the calmness is itself the tell. People aren't describing peace; they're describing coping strategies *for the absence of peace*. The dominant move is control-boundary-setting (focus on what you can control), which is good psychology but bad epistemics — it never asks whether the boundary is drawn correctly.

### Key Insights

**1. The Manic Builder's Paradox**

The sharpest tension in the thread isn't about coping — it's about creating. `pendenthistory` captures it perfectly: building faster than ever thanks to Claude, in a "maniac state," but haunted by the feeling it's all "probably meaningless because if things progress as they have, anyone can just copy what I've built." This is a new psychological state: *productive despair*. You're shipping more than ever precisely because the tools are so good, while simultaneously believing the tools will render your output commodity. `patrickk` echoes this with "ClaudClaw" — trying to escape the corporate grind by building an AI agency while knowing the technology is "going to further hollow out the middle class." The thread treats this as a personal quirk; it's actually the core dynamic of 2026 tech work.

**2. "Action Absorbs Anxiety" — The Thread's Best Framework**

`pickleglitch` offers the most structurally useful comment: "action absorbs anxiety." Self-hosting services, studying history, writing a blog, setting a no-social-media-after-8pm rule. This is *not* the same as the "touch grass" crowd. The difference is intentionality — `pickleglitch` is building agency through small acts of sovereignty, not retreating into ignorance. `morgengold` supports this, pushing back directly on withdrawal:

> "Withdrawing into one's own little world cannot be the answer. And saying it's the only realm you can control isn't really true."

The subtle split: stoics say *accept what you can't control*; the agency camp says *you're probably wrong about what you can't control*.

**3. The Nationalization of Attention Is Destroying Local Effectiveness**

`Karrot_Kream` drops the thread's most underappreciated insight: focusing on national/global politics is actively sabotaging local civic engagement. "100x more people show up to a Gaza rally than a DOT hearing on traffic safety." This is a measurable harm, not a vibes complaint — local government runs on citizen attention, and that attention has been captured by national spectacle. The mechanism: social media rewards engagement with distant crises (emotional intensity) over local ones (boring but tractable). Nobody in the thread picks this up, which itself proves the point.

**4. The "It Was Always Like This" Defense Is Weakening**

Multiple comments invoke historical precedent — Cold War nuclear anxiety, the Balkans, 9/11. `iso1631` deploys "Que Será, Será." But `Havoc` quietly punctures it: "The threats yea, but I don't think this sense of exponential acceleration was the same." This is the right objection. Previous crises were *discontinuous* — a war starts, a wall falls. The current anxiety is about *continuous acceleration* where the ground shifts weekly. The "it was always like this" framing worked when change was episodic; it fails when the rate of change is itself accelerating. `khaledh` calls this "time compression" — a useful term the thread fails to develop.

**5. The AI Fear Has Bifurcated Into Existential vs. Economic**

Two completely different AI anxieties coexist in the thread without acknowledging each other. `kypro` is in genuine x-risk despair — "I've believed since around 2012 that the most likely way I'd die is from AI" — having nightmares, feeling vindicated and terrified simultaneously. Meanwhile `bstrama`, `vanillameow`, and `pendenthistory` worry about career displacement. These are fundamentally different concerns requiring different responses, but the thread mushes them together under "AI anxiety." The economic camp can cope by upskilling; the x-risk camp has no individual action available. `kypro`'s comment is the thread's most emotionally raw, and gets zero engagement — the thread doesn't want to go there.

**6. The Medication/Therapy Comments Are Buried**

`throwaway39328` describes being prescribed olanzapine (an antipsychotic) for what is essentially *rational distress at world events* — and being "rather distraught that it worked so well." `idontwantthis` quietly advocates therapy and medication. These comments get almost no engagement. The thread overwhelmingly prefers cognitive reframing (stoicism, media diet) over acknowledging that the distress level might warrant clinical intervention. This is HN's Achilles heel: the belief that sufficiently correct thinking eliminates the need for help.

**7. Empire Decline as Explanatory Framework**

`muzani` deploys Ray Dalio's empire-cycle model: rise through productivity → wealth → decadence → debt → decline → civil conflict. `ChrisMarshallNY` pushes back ("ruthlessness and amorality are more reliable predictors"). The interesting thing isn't whether Dalio's model is right — it's that having *any* structural model seems to reduce anxiety. The anxiety in the OP is formless: everything is going wrong, unpredictably. A decline narrative, even a grim one, offers *legibility*. Several comments pattern-match to this without citing Dalio: `dragonelite`'s "you're just feeling what the rest of the non-western world has felt for centuries" reframes American decline anxiety as normalization, not catastrophe.

**8. The Astroturf Question**

`lloydatkinson` flags the post as potential propaganda. `flowerthoughts` corroborates: "Someone is definitely on a propaganda tour trying to seed negative sentiment," citing parallel patterns on Swedish Reddit. Whether or not *this specific post* is astroturf, the pattern they describe — young throwaway accounts posting emotional doom-framing adapted to specific forums — is a real and documented tactic. The thread engages earnestly regardless, which may be the correct response (the feelings are real even if the prompt is manufactured) or may be the intended outcome.

### What the Thread Misses

- **Collective action is almost entirely absent.** 103 authors and almost every response is individual: meditate, exercise, log off, invest wisely. `tcaxle` mentions joining activist groups and organized campaigns — one of the only comments that suggests *coordinated* response. The thread's implicit model is that anxiety is a personal information-diet problem, not a symptom of atomized political impotence.

- **Nobody distinguishes between uncertainty and risk.** The OP bundles "can't predict anything" with "everything is bad." These are different problems. Uncertainty (unknown outcomes) responds to acceptance; risk (known bad outcomes) responds to action. The thread treats them as one feeling.

- **The financial anxiety is shallow.** Multiple mentions of stock market, "should I sell," but nobody discusses actual portfolio strategy beyond `AnimalMuppet`'s sensible "sell enough that you won't regret it either way." For a tech-heavy audience with concentrated equity exposure, this is surprisingly thin.

- **No one names the meta-irony:** the thread is itself an instance of the problem it describes — 103 people on Hacker News telling each other to stop being on the internet.

### Verdict

This thread is the 2026 successor to "Ask HN: How do you deal with mass layoffs?" and "Ask HN: AI fatalism coping?" — same demographic, ratcheting anxiety, progressively broader scope. The OP's list has grown from "AI will take my job" to "everything everywhere is falling apart," which is either escalation or simply honesty about what was always underneath the narrower fears.

The thread's dominant prescription — stoic disengagement plus personal hobbies — is psychologically sound but philosophically bankrupt. It works precisely because it abandons the claim that individual action can affect collective outcomes. The few comments that push against this (`pickleglitch`, `morgengold`, `Karrot_Kream`, `tcaxle`) are more interesting but less popular. The thread circles a truth it never states: the anxiety isn't about *information overload* — it's about *agency collapse*. People don't feel bad because they know too much; they feel bad because knowing more hasn't given them any more power to act. The prescription of knowing less is therefore palliative, not curative.
