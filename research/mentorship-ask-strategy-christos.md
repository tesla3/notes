# Planning a High-Stakes Ask: Research Mentorship for Daughter

Date: 2025-05-12

## Situation

Asking a CMU tenured professor (Christos Faloutsos, ML/graphs/time series/anomaly detection) to mentor my 10th-grade daughter on a summer research project. He's an external advisor to my group at work — weekly group meetings for over a year. I'm the tech lead; he's in advisory/supporting role.

## Daughter's Profile

- Going into junior year, Fall 2025
- 1 year Python, Calc BC, Honors Stats, Honors Chem
- FTC competitive robotics (programming)
- Interested in engineering, excited about ML
- Independent, articulate, passionate when engaged
- No summer programs secured yet (one math camp waitlist)

## The Ask

Him (or one of his PhD students) guides her through a scoped summer ML project. She does all hands-on work. I unblock her technically at home. Mentor provides direction and weekly check-ins (~20 min zoom). Potentially continues into junior year on biweekly cadence. Publication if something worthwhile emerges; otherwise, valuable experience.

## Key Strategic Decisions Made

### Opening: Direct, not disguised
- Don't open with a work topic and pivot. He'll sense the hidden agenda.
- Brief greeting → explicit "I have something personal to ask" → go.

### Framing: "Ask" not "favor"
- "Favor" creates a cost/debt frame before he knows the cost.
- "Something personal I wanted to ask you about" — direct without loading it.
- Let him decide whether it feels like a burden (it probably won't).

### Approach: Direct ask, not indirect "advice" gambit
- Asking "what would you suggest?" when you want HIM to do it → feels like a setup when you escalate.
- He's smart. He'll see through indirection. Just ask.
- Build multiple commitment levels INTO the ask: "you, or one of your students."

### Anti-over-explain: Rule of Three
- Three sentences about daughter, then make the ask.
- Let him pull information through questions rather than pushing it all upfront.

### Making him comfortable (primary design goal)
- Build exits INTO the ask itself (PhD student, recommendation, "totally fine if not").
- Mention fallback only if he seems hesitant: "she'll do a project either way."
- Don't fill his silence. Don't add softeners after the ask.
- Keep energy at "normal Tuesday" level.

## Key Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| He says "I don't take interns" (his policy) | "This is informal mentoring, not a position. No paperwork." |
| Her interests (robotics) ≠ his research | "She's wide open. Whatever you think is a good entry point." Let him choose. |
| Remote concern | "Everything on zoom. I can bring her to Pittsburgh to kick off if helpful." |
| Over-explaining burns the 30 minutes | Hard limit: 3 sentences setup, ask by minute 2. |
| He feels cornered (nice person, hard to say no) | Pre-built exits in the ask; fallback plan removes guilt. |

## Acceptable Outcomes (ranked)

1. He agrees to guide her directly (best)
2. He connects her with a PhD student (great — maybe better for hands-on)
3. He says "let me think" (follow up in 5-7 days)
4. He refers to a colleague or program (still useful)
5. He says no (relationship preserved; move to self-guided plan)

## Insights from the Planning Process

- The user LEADS the professional relationship. This isn't asking "up" — it's a peer asking someone who already has a giving/supporting posture.
- "Personal topic" is unprecedented in this relationship (only ever had technical conversations). The novelty itself isn't a problem — it signals trust.
- The professor's FAQ page ("no summer interns") addresses cold applicants, not professional collaborators. Different channel entirely.
- Late-career tenured professors often enjoy mentoring — low stakes, giving back, bandwidth available.
- A PhD student getting free motivated help on a side project is attractive framing (not charity case, but useful labor).

## Follow-Up Actions

- If yes: have daughter email him introducing herself. Set up 3-way zoom.
- If "let me think": follow up in 5-7 days, brief and no-pressure.
- If no: ask for referral. Proceed with self-guided summer plan.
- Either way: prep daughter with coaching on how to show curiosity, not credentials.
