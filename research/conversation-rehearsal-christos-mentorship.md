# Conversation Rehearsal: Asking Christos to Mentor Daughter

Date: 2025-05-12

## Context

- 30-minute zoom, 1-on-1, titled "quick sync/chat"
- He doesn't know the topic in advance
- Only second 1-on-1 ever (first was paper feedback)
- Relationship: purely professional, ~1 year, weekly group meetings
- You lead the group; he's in advisory/supporting role

---

## The Script

### Opening (0:00–0:30)

> "Hey Christos, how's it going?"

*(Brief small talk — 15–30 sec.)*

### Pivot (0:30–1:00)

> "So hey — the reason I grabbed this time. I have something personal I wanted to ask you about. Bit different from our usual stuff."

*(He says "sure" — keep going.)*

### Setup — THREE sentences only (1:00–1:30)

> "My daughter's going into her junior year this fall. She's been doing competitive robotics, programming in Python, finished calc and stats this year. She's getting really excited about machine learning."

### The Ask (1:30–2:00)

> "Would you — or maybe one of your students — be open to guiding her through a summer project? She'd do all the hands-on work, I can unblock her technically at home. She'd just need someone to point her in the right direction. And if it's not a fit, totally fine — I'd appreciate even just a pointer to who might be good for this."

### STOP. Say nothing. Let him respond.

---

## Ready Answers (keep to 2–3 sentences each)

**"What does she want to work on?"**
> "She's wide open within ML. I think she'd be excited by whatever you think is a good entry point — time series, graphs, anomaly detection. She's at the exploration stage."

**"What's her coding level?"**
> "About a year of Python. Not writing ML pipelines yet, but I'll prep her on whatever tools she needs before she starts."

**"How much time are we talking?"**
> "For you, maybe a 20-minute zoom check-in once a week during summer. I handle everything else day-to-day."

**"My research might be too advanced for her"**
> "Totally fair. I'm not expecting her to push the frontier — even a simplified version of a real problem would be incredible. Or if one of your students has a subproblem they'd love to offload, that could work."

**"Does she have enough background?"**
> "Solid math, a year of Python. She's not at PhD level obviously, but she's a fast learner. If there's anything she should study first, I'll make sure she does the prep before starting."

---

## Branch Responses

### He says yes / is enthusiastic
> "That'd be great — really appreciate it. What would you think is a good next step? Should she send you an email, or want to meet her on a quick zoom?"

Don't over-celebrate. Stay casual. Get the next action.

### He offers a PhD student
> "That'd be perfect. If you could connect us, I'll take it from there and make sure it's easy for them."

This is a WIN. Accept warmly.

### He says "Let me think about it"
> "Of course. No rush — and genuinely, whatever you decide is fine. I'll follow up in a week or so."

Follow up in exactly 5–7 days. Brief, no pressure.

### He deflects to a program
> "Good to know, I'll look into that. But honestly what I think would help her most is working on a real problem with a real researcher — even something small. Is there anyone you'd recommend I reach out to?"

### He seems hesitant / uncomfortable
> "Look — I know this is a bit out of the blue. She's going to do a project this summer either way; I'll guide her myself if needed. I just thought she'd benefit from someone at your level. But seriously, no pressure."

### He says "I don't take interns"
> "Totally get it. This would be different from a formal internship — no paperwork, no CMU affiliation. More like an informal mentoring relationship. But if it doesn't fit, no worries."

### He asks "Why me? Why not someone at UCSD?"
> "I've worked with you for over a year and I've seen how you think about problems. The way you break things down, find the interesting question inside a messy one. That's what I'd want her exposed to. And I trust you."

---

## Anti-Over-Explain Rules

1. **Three sentences max** for the setup. When you've said three, make the ask.
2. **Don't fill silence.** After the ask, let him think. Count to 5 in your head.
3. **Don't add softeners** after the ask ("I know it's a lot," "sorry to spring this on you"). These CREATE awkwardness.
4. **Let him pull information.** Answer his questions concisely; don't preemptively address concerns he hasn't raised.

---

## Making Him Comfortable

1. Built-in exits in the ask itself ("or one of your students," "even just a pointer")
2. Fallback plan mentioned only if he seems hesitant ("she'll do a project either way")
3. Keep YOUR energy light — "normal Tuesday conversation" not "kidney donation request"
4. Never fill his silence with reassurance

---

## If He Says Yes: Coaching Daughter for First Meeting

Tell her:
1. "Your job isn't to impress him. Show curiosity and willingness to work hard."
2. "Ask him questions about his research. Professors love explaining their work."
3. "Be honest about what excites you. Don't guess what he wants to hear."
4. "If he suggests a direction, ask 'can you tell me more about what that would look like?'"
5. "It's OK to say 'I don't know that yet, but I can learn it.'"

Frame: "This isn't an interview. It's figuring out if there's something cool to work on together. He already said yes to talking — just be you and be curious."

---

## Mindset Going In

- You're a technical leader he works with weekly. This is a natural human ask.
- "My kid is into what you do — could you help her?" is flattering, not burdensome.
- He'll almost certainly say yes to *something*. The question is at what level.
- Go in expecting a good conversation, not a negotiation.
