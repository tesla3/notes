# Avery — Reading Habit Conversation (May 2026)

**Context:** End of 8th grade. ISEE Upper Level: Math 100% (stanine 9), Reading stanine 7. Earlier ISEE had reading/vocab at stanine 6/7 — contributed to not getting into Bishop's like her older sister. HS class of 2030 (graduates 2030). Daughter motivated by "getting into a good school."

## SAT projection from ISEE (3 years out, end of 11th grade)

- **Math: 750–800.** Locked in. Top ~4% nationally; the score travels with her through normal HS math progression.
- **Reading & Writing: 640–710.** Solid, above average, not at the ceiling. Highly malleable with reading volume.
- **Total: ~1390–1510** with no extra effort. **1500+** trajectory if reading habit builds now.

Roughly: **~1450 = strong top-30 school. ~1530 = top-15 territory.** The gap lives entirely in the verbal section.

Why now matters: SAT verbal correlates with cumulative reading hours, not test prep. Compounding window is 8th–10th grade. 11th-grade vocab cramming doesn't catch up. (See [college-admissions.md](../topics/college-admissions.md) and [elite-college-admissions-2026-deep-research.md](../research/elite-college-admissions-2026-deep-research.md) Part VII.)

## Email drafts considered (NOT sent)

Drafted three escalating-soft versions of an email to Avery laying out the SAT projection and the reading-habit ask. Each version named ISEE/SAT/college tier explicitly. All discarded.

## Decision: do NOT send the email

### Why the email is the wrong medium

1. **Fresh wound + fresh score.** Reading came back at stanine 7 again — same band as the prior ISEE that contributed to the Bishop's outcome. Any email from Dad doing SAT projections off that number lands as "Dad ran the math and reading is still my problem." She'll re-read it. The wound deepens each time.
2. **Email crystallizes the sister comparison.** The unspoken second sentence of any college-strategy email to the second daughter is "…unlike your sister." Doesn't have to be written. She'll hear it.
3. **Self-contradicting with own research.** The deep-research note says premature optimization is the dominant failure mode at her age, and that strong senior profiles emerge from *discovery*, not from 8th-grade plans. A college-stakes email about an 8th-grade test result is exactly that failure mode.
4. **No urgency.** 3+ years to SAT. Nothing breaks if delivered slowly, in person, over the summer, with no paper trail.
5. **Goal is a habit, not a memo.** Habits aren't built by emails. They're built by environment, modeling, and zero-pressure access.

## Plan instead — same goal, no wound

- **Bookstore trips.** Take her. Let her pick anything. Buy without commentary. Repeat monthly through the summer.
- **Read visibly yourself.** On the couch, at the table, where she sees it. Kids copy what parents *do*, not what parents email.
- **One short in-person line, when it comes up naturally** — NOT prompted by a score:
  > "Reading widely is one of the best long-term investments you can make. Anything you want, I'll buy."
  No ISEE, no SAT, no top-15, no sister.
- **More lines that work** (see principle below — talk about the world, not about her):
  > "Reading widely is the single biggest thing that moves verbal scores between now and the SAT — bigger than any prep. The kids who end up at top schools almost all read a lot from middle school on."

  > "Math is mostly about pace through HS — keep stacking honors STEM (Physics, Chem, Bio) and you're set there. Reading is different — it's hours over years. The hours start now or they don't."

  > "It's the most leveraged habit you can build at your age, and it pays off forever, not just on the SAT."
- **Lines to avoid, even softened:**
  - ❌ "Your reading score…"
  - ❌ "The one thing holding you back…"
  - ❌ "Math is locked in, but reading…" *(implies the gap is her problem)*
  - ❌ Any sentence comparing her two sub-scores
  - ❌ "Dragging you down" / "one thing you can fix" — these deliver a verdict
- **Ask what she's reading.** Curious, not evaluative. Read one of her picks so you can actually talk about it.
- **Math + honors STEM (Physics/Chem/Bio):** deliver praise/encouragement in person, casually, on a *separate* day from anything reading-related. Mixing the two re-creates the comparison ("strong here, weak there"). Praise the math win on its own day.

## Underlying principle

"Let her be herself" doesn't mean withhold support — it means stop *optimizing* her and start *equipping* her.

- Books on the shelf, a dad who reads, an open wallet, curiosity about her interests = **equipping**.
- Strategic email with score projections = **optimizing**.

She can feel the difference, even at 13. Especially at 13.

### Talk about the world, not about her

The rule that governs every line above:

| Frame | What she hears | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| "Reading is dragging you down" | *I'm broken in this specific way* | Identity wound |
| "Reading is the one thing you can fix" | *Dad sees a defect; fixing it = approval* | Anxiety + perfectionism |
| "Reading is the highest-leverage habit you can build right now" | *There's a lever, it's mine, the world works this way* | Agency |

Same underlying message. Opposite emotional load.

- Talk about **the world** (how reading and verbal scores work) → she can act on it.
- Talk about **her** (her diagnosis) → she has to defend against it.

She's smart. She already knows where she stands. Your job isn't to deliver the diagnosis she already has. It's to give her the lever and the agency to use it.

### Order and separation

- Mention **math / STEM honors first**, on a **different day** than reading.
- The order signals what you actually believe about her. Lead with what she's winning.
- Never deliver math praise and reading suggestion in the same conversation — that recreates the comparison.

## Revisit

- **Fall 2026** — observe whether the habit took. No nudge before then.
- **9th grade PSAT 8/9** — first useful diagnostic per the deep-research note. That's the next data point worth acting on.
- **Do not** treat any further ISEE / standardized test result as a trigger for a college-tier conversation with her until at least 10th grade.
